# OpenAI Mission Statement Safety Language Removal

## Overview

When [[entities/openai|OpenAI]] restructured from a nonprofit to a for-profit public benefit corporation in late 2024–2025, it removed all references to **"safety"** from its official mission statement filed with the IRS. The change was discovered by **Alnoor Ebrahim**, a professor at Tufts University's Fletcher School, by comparing OpenAI's Form 990 filings across years.

## The Change

| Year | Mission Statement | Safety Language |
|------|-------------------|-----------------|
| 2016–2017 | "advance digital intelligence… benefit humanity as a whole… **build safe AI technology**" | ✅ Explicit |
| 2018–2019 | Same as above | ✅ Explicit |
| 2020 | Same, minor rewording | ✅ Explicit |
| 2021 | "build general-purpose AI that benefits humanity… **develop and responsibly deploy safe AI technology**" | ✅ Explicit |
| 2022–2023 | "build general-purpose AI that **safely** benefits humanity… **responsibly deploy safe AI technology**" | ✅ Explicit (strongest) |
| 2024 | "ensure that artificial general intelligence benefits all of humanity" | ❌ Removed |

OpenAI changed its mission statement **six times** in nine years of Form 990 filings. The 2024 filing was the first to remove all references to safety.

## Context: The Restructuring

- **$6.6B funding round** (late 2024): Investors required restructuring into for-profit or funding would convert to debt
- **Nonprofit control reduced**: OpenAI Foundation ceded **74% control**, retaining only **26% stake** in OpenAI Group
- **Microsoft ownership**: $13.8B investment → 27% of company stock
- **Investor board seats**: Investors now directly receive a share of profits and sit on the board — previously prohibited under nonprofit rules
- **IRS Form 990** (FY2024, released November 2025): The last filing as a tax-exempt nonprofit

## Concerns Raised

**Alnoor Ebrahim** (Tufts University Fletcher School):

> "I believe OpenAI's makeover is a test case for how we, as a society, oversee the work of organizations that have the potential to both provide enormous benefits and do catastrophic harm."

> "In my view, these changes explicitly signal that OpenAI is making its profits a higher priority than the safety of its products."

> "Given that neither the mission of the foundation nor of the OpenAI group explicitly alludes to safety, it will be hard to hold their boards accountable for it."

Key governance concern: Under nonprofit rules, board members cannot take a share of earnings. With investors now on the board who directly profit, the conflict of interest creates structural pressure to prioritize returns over safety.

## OpenAI's Response

OpenAI stated:

> "We rephrased our mission to 'ensure that artificial general intelligence benefits all of humanity' and planned to achieve it 'primarily by attempting to build safe AGI and share the benefits with the world.' The words and approach changed to serve the same goal—benefiting humanity."

The company still uses safety language on its website: "We view this mission as the most important challenge of its time. It requires simultaneously advancing AI's capability, safety, and positive impact in the world."

However, the formal IRS filing — the document with legal accountability — no longer contains safety language.

## Legal Context

At the time of the article (February 2026), OpenAI and CEO Sam Altman faced multiple lawsuits alleging negligence, assisted suicide, involuntary manslaughter, wrongful death, and other product liability claims.

## Significance

This event represents a pivotal moment in [[concepts/security-and-governance/ai-governance|AI governance]]:

1. **Structural shift**: From mission-driven nonprofit to investor-driven for-profit
2. **Accountability gap**: Safety commitments removed from legally binding filings while maintained in marketing
3. **Precedent**: Tests whether AI companies can maintain safety commitments after restructuring
4. **Board composition**: Investor board members with direct profit stakes create inherent tension with safety priorities

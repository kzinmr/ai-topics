## Abstract

Deep research has emerged as an important task that aims to address hard queries through extensive open-web exploration. To tackle it, most prior work equips large language model (LLM)-based agents with opaque web search APIs, enabling agents to iteratively issue search queries, retrieve external evidence, and reason over it. Despite search's essential role in deep research, black-box web search APIs hinder systematic analysis of search components, leaving the behaviour of established text ranking methods in deep research largely unclear. To fill this gap, we reproduce a selection of key findings and best practices for IR text ranking methods in the deep research setting. In particular, we examine their effectiveness from three perspectives: (i) **retrieval units** (documents vs. passages), (ii) **pipeline configurations** (different retrievers, re-rankers, and re-ranking depths), and (iii) **query characteristics** (the mismatch between agent-issued queries and the training queries of text rankers). We perform experiments on BrowseComp-Plus, a deep research dataset with a fixed corpus, evaluating 2 open-source agents, 5 retrievers, and 3 re-rankers across diverse setups. We find that agent-issued queries typically follow web-search-style syntax (e.g., quoted exact matches), favouring lexical, learned sparse, and multi-vector retrievers; passage-level units are more efficient under limited context windows, and avoid the difficulties of document length normalisation in lexical retrieval; re-ranking is highly effective; translating agent-issued queries into natural-language questions significantly bridges the query mismatch.
This research addresses the "black-box" nature of deep research agents that rely on opaque web search APIs. Using the **BrowseComp-Plus** dataset with a fixed corpus, the authors systematically evaluate how traditional IR text ranking methods perform when driven by LLM-based agents.

## Three Evaluation Perspectives

The paper systematically examines IR text ranking methods from three angles:

### (i) Retrieval Units: Documents vs. Passages
- **Passage-level retrieval** (250-word segments) is superior for agents with limited context windows
- BM25 with document-oriented parameters ($k_1=2.0, b=1.0$) becomes highly competitive at passage level
- Passages avoid difficulties of document length normalisation in lexical retrieval

### (ii) Pipeline Configurations
- 5 retrievers tested: BM25, SPLADE-v3, ColBERTv2, RepLLaMA, Qwen3-Embed
- 3 re-rankers tested: monoT5-3B, Rank1-7B, Qwen3-Reranker
- BM25–monoT5-3B pipeline (depth 50) with gpt-oss-20b agent reached **0.716 recall** and **0.689 accuracy**, approaching commercial performance
- Reasoning-based re-rankers (Rank1) showed no clear advantage — they misinterpret keyword-heavy search queries

### (iii) Query Characteristics
- Agent-issued queries use **web-search-style syntax** (quoted exact matches, Boolean operators, fragmented terms)
- This favors lexical (BM25), learned sparse (SPLADE-v3), and multi-vector (ColBERTv2) retrievers over dense retrievers
- Neural rankers trained on MS MARCO/Natural Questions face **distribution shift** — agent queries are out-of-distribution
- **Q2Q reformulation** (translating keywords → natural language questions using reasoning trace) yielded **7.95% relative gain** for SPLADE-v3

## Key Findings

### 1. BM25 Dominance
When properly configured, BM25 outperforms neural rankers in most deep research scenarios. Agent-issued queries use web-search syntax (keywords, quotes, exact matches) that favors lexical matching.

### 2. Passage vs. Document Units
Passage-level retrieval (250-word segments) is superior for agents with limited context windows. BM25 with document-oriented parameters ($k_1=2.0, b=1.0$) becomes highly competitive.

### 3. Re-ranking Efficacy
BM25–monoT5-3B pipeline (depth 50) with a gpt-oss-20b agent reached **0.716 recall** and **0.689 accuracy**, approaching commercial performance.

### 4. Query Mismatch (Q2Q)
Agent-issued queries differ fundamentally from neural ranker training queries. Q2Q reformulation (translating keywords into natural language questions using reasoning trace) yielded **7.95% relative gain** for SPLADE-v3.

### 5. Reasoning Re-rankers Underperform
Reasoning-based re-rankers (Rank1) showed no clear advantage — they often misinterpret keyword-heavy search queries.

## Dataset

**BrowseComp-Plus:** 830 queries, 100,195 documents, 2,772,255 passages. Human-verified "gold" and "evidence" judgments.

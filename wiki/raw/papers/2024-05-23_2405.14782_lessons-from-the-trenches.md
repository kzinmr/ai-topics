# Lessons from the Trenches on Reproducible Evaluation of Language Models

**Authors:** Stella Biderman*, Hailey Schoelkopf*, Lintang Sutawika*, Leo Gao, Jonathan Tow et al. (30 authors total)
**Affiliations:** EleutherAI, Stability AI, MBZUAI, Brown University, CMU, NYU, Amazon, Contextual AI, and others
**Date:** May 23, 2024 (v2: May 29, 2024)

## Abstract

Effective evaluation of language models remains an open challenge in NLP. This paper draws on three years of experience evaluating large language models to provide guidance and lessons. It covers common challenges, delineates best practices, and presents the Language Model Evaluation Harness (lm-eval): an open-source library for independent, reproducible, and extensible evaluation.

## Key Contributions

1. **Identification of the "Key Problem":** Semantic equivalence in natural language makes automatic evaluation fundamentally difficult — our best tools for determining semantic equivalence are the very models we're trying to evaluate.

2. **Five Best Practices:**
   - Share exact prompts and code (commit-level links, not stylized paper descriptions)
   - Avoid copying results from other papers unless the exact same evaluation code was used
   - Provide model outputs (raw generations) for recalculability
   - Perform qualitative analysis on small batches before full-scale runs
   - Measure and report uncertainty (standard errors, multiple seeds)

3. **lm-eval Framework Design:**
   - Three primitive request types: Loglikelihood, Perplexity, Generation (generate_until)
   - Task versioning for reproducibility tracking
   - Jinja2 templating for complex prompts
   - Multiple choice normalization methods: token-length, byte-length (acc_norm), mutual information (acc_mutual_info)

4. **Case Study: Prompting Style Sensitivity**
   - Mistral-7B: 47.1% (Cloze) vs 51.5% (MMLU-style) — prefers MMLU-style
   - Llama-2-7B: 43.1% (Cloze) vs 40.4% (MMLU-style) — prefers Cloze-style
   - Choosing different styles makes cross-paper comparisons "nonsensical"

## Notable Observations

- API model deprecation (e.g., OpenAI code-davinci-002 deprecated Jan 2024) makes hundreds of studies irreproducible
- Many industrial LMs used as reference points have never been released externally
- Benchmarks designed for BERT-era fine-tuning are being "retrofitted" for in-context learning without standardized implementations

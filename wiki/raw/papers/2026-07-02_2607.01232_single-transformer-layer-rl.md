# Is One Layer Enough? Training A Single Transformer Layer Can Match Full-Parameter RL Training

- **arXiv ID**: [2607.01232](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01232)
- **Version**: v2 (last revised 2 Jul 2026; submitted 1 Jul 2026)
- **Subjects**: Machine Learning (cs.LG); Computation and Language (cs.CL)

## Abstract

Reinforcement learning (RL) has become the dominant post-training paradigm for aligning large language models. The standard approach trains all parameters of a pretrained model using algorithms like GRPO, Dr. GRPO, GiGPO, and others. This paper investigates whether full-parameter training is necessary, or whether gains can be achieved by training only a subset of layers.

## Key Findings

1. **Layer Concentration**: RL post-training gains are heavily concentrated in a small subset of transformer layers — often just one or two middle layers can match or surpass full-parameter training.

2. **Universal Pattern**: Across 7 models (Qwen3 and Qwen2.5 families, 1.5B–8B parameters), 3 RL algorithms (GRPO, Dr. GRPO, GiGPO), and 3 task domains (math, code, agentic), the pattern is consistent:
   - Middle layers contribute most
   - Early and late layers contribute least
   - Training only middle layers surpasses full-parameter training with no profiling needed

3. **Complementary Behaviors**: Models trained on different layers exhibit complementary behaviors, with majority voting across layer-trained models outperforming both single models and full-parameter baselines.

4. **Simple Heuristic**: A simple heuristic of training only middle layers works better than full-parameter training and requires no profiling.

## Methods

- **Layer Attribution**: Systematically tested training individual layers vs. full-parameter across 7 model families
- **RL Algorithms Tested**: GRPO, Dr. GRPO, GiGPO
- **Task Domains**: Mathematical reasoning, code generation, agentic tasks
- **Model Sizes**: 1.5B to 8B parameters

## Implications

- Drastically reduces computational cost of RL post-training
- Enables more efficient alignment of large models
- Suggests RL fine-tuning benefits are localized rather than distributed
- May allow training of larger models with limited compute budgets
- Opens questions about why middle layers are most plastic for RL adaptation

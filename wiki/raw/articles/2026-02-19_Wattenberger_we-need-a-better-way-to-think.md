In 1786, a Scottish con man named William Playfair invented the line chart. Before him, trade data mostly sat in ledgers full of numbers. The information was all there, but you couldn't see at a glance when things were correlated, or that there was a cliff at a threshold. Playfair drew lines through the numbers, and suddenly the trends were obvious. The data didn't change. The representation did.
Go back further. When Arabic numerals arrived in medieval Europe, Florence banned them from banking because you could forge a 0 into a 6 or a 9. Bankers kept two sets of books: Roman numerals for the authorities, Arabic for actual calculations. It took two centuries for the representation to win, but once it did, modern accounting, algebra, and double-entry bookkeeping followed almost immediately. The missing piece wasn't information, it was notation that made it easy to work with.
The pattern keeps repeating: when the mechanical part of a skill gets abstracted away, the thinking doesn't disappear. It moves up. And it stalls there until someone builds the right medium for it.
Agents write most of my code now. I spend more time deciding what to build, decomposing problems so agents can handle the pieces, reviewing their work and steering them. Tbh I often find it harder work than coding.
But mostly because doing that work in chat windows and file trees. I can see individual files. I can scroll through conversations. What I can't do is look at a screen and see the shape of the work: which pieces are done, where intent got lost, how the whole thing connects, what's the bigger picture. The information is all there. The representation that makes it thinkable isn't.
The next superpower probably isn't a faster model. It's a better way to think.

# What Happened to Palm Pilots? The Rise and Fall of a Classic

Source: https://dfarq.homeip.net/what-happened-to-palm-pilots/?utm_source=rss&utm_medium=rss&utm_campaign=what-happened-to-palm-pilots

Palm was a high-flying brand in the late 1990s, creating the first really popular personal data assistant. Then it seemed to vanish almost as quickly as it came. What happened to Palm Pilots, and the company who made them? On April 28, 2010, HP acquired Palm for $1.2 billion, with big plans to use its tech to compete directly with Apple. That didn’t go to plan, so you’re not reading this on a Palm-based phone or tablet. But the tech may be hiding in plain sight elsewhere in your home.
My Palm Pilot story
What happened to Palm Pilots? They were incredibly popular when they were the newest thing. But they faded fast when they didn’t evolve to keep up with newer technology.
It was 1997. I was working my first IT job at a college. The dean got a fancy new Palm Pilot. Somehow we got his Palm Pilot working with
Lotus Notes
, which was what we used for calendaring and e-mail at the time. It took several of us to figure it out. And the dean was happy. Until he saw someone else’s Palm Pilot connected up to a PC running Outlook.
The dean came back and decreed we would use Outlook for e-mail, and whatever backend we had to use to support Outlook, because he liked how Outlook worked with his Palm Pilot. And that was the beginning of the end for Lotus Domino and Lotus Notes in my department, because at the time, Domino and Outlook didn’t work together.
Eventually IBM got Domino to work well with Outlook. But not soon enough for our dean. I suspect a number of executives who got Palm Pilots early on had similar experiences and made similar decrees, and that hurt Domino almost as much as anything. Domino was faster and more stable than Exchange ever was, but end users want their fancy gadgets, and Microsoft always did a better job of delivering that.
What happened to Palm Pilots? I’m not saying they single-handedly killed Lotus Notes. But they helped.
My second Palm Pilot story
I walked into a meeting with another tech and a couple of managers. It was sometime in 1999 or maybe 2000. The two managers were comparing their Palm Pilots while waiting for more people to arrive. At a pause in the conversation, the other tech pulled out his Apple Newton and said, “I feel so old fashioned.”
I picked up the Office Depot-branded paper notepad I’d brought, set it back down, pretended to get up, and said, “Excuse me while I go get a chisel and a stone tablet.”
Everyone laughed, and then the managers went back to comparing what the newer Palm Pilot did that the old one wouldn’t do. Noting my disinterest, one of the managers asked, “You don’t have Palm Pilot envy?”
I didn’t. But an awful lot of people did.
Maybe
a new Palm Pilot model wasn’t as big of a deal as the newest big-name smartphone model today. People didn’t camp out in front of stores for the chance to buy one at midnight. But they had a cult following, and when we got word of a new model coming out, we had a pretty good idea who’d be getting one first. Some of my coworkers may have even taken bets.
What happened to Palm Pilots’ cult following?
Palm Pilots weren’t exactly a mass-market gadget. They tended to be popular with early adopters. And in the mid 90s when it came out, its only competitor was the Apple Newton Messagepad. It was smaller and cheaper than Apple’s device, and learning the Palm’s alphabet was faster than training the Newton to recognize your handwriting. Both of them felt less old-fashioned than an
HP Palmtop
, which was a palm-sized PC clone running MS-DOS. Palm’s upstart status wasn’t a problem for long, as US Robotics bought them in 1995, and in turn,
3Com
bought US Robotics in 1996. So they were backed by the biggest name in modems and then by the biggest name in networking.
Managers and executives used them to track their appointments and meetings and to take notes. Upon returning to their office, they’d dock the Palm Pilot in its cradle, push a button, and it would sync with their PC. In 1996, it was really slick, at least when it worked.
The Palm Pilot’s inventors left the company in 1998 in the wake of the
3Com/US Robotics merger
and founded another company called Handspring, which licensed the Palm OS and produced Palm Pilot clones that were better. You could even add phone capability to them. Later models were smartphones right out of the box, and not just PDAs. Handspring and Palm ended up merging in 2003.
But there was a problem. I wrote about it when I got a Palm Pilot myself.
I was carrying around too much junk
. Those single-purpose gadgets made you more productive until you got one too many, and then carrying and managing them made your job harder instead of easier.
There was another problem. It wasn’t long before Palm Pilots started feeling old-fashioned. Microsoft’s Pocket PC was faster, had color, and had more features. And it worked a lot like Windows. It wasn’t terribly stable, but worked well enough for simple tasks. And then there was
Blackberry
, which let you read e-mail on your pager, and then on your phone. Palm Pilot envy gave way to Blackberry envy.
Killed off by the smartphone
But ultimately, even when Palm Pilots had well-integrated phones and technically were smartphones in their own right, they couldn’t compete with smarter smartphones. Blackberry was offering Internet capability, not just e-mail, by the time Palm Pilots with integrated phones were common. By 2005, Palm was fading fast. In 2006, it resorted to introducing a Windows Mobile-powered smartphone, not even using its own OS.
Then along came Apple in 2007 with its first-generation iPhone. Now with one device, you could read your e-mail, make phone calls, update your calendar, and it could sync with your computer wirelessly. And it had a web browser with computer-like capability.
Palm, like so many other companies, tried to create an answer to Apple’s product. It created a new OS, called Web OS, as the official successor to Palm OS. Palm released a couple of smartphones based on the product in the 2009-2010 timeframe. Then HP acquired Palm for $1.2 billion in April 2010 as part of an acquisition spree, mostly to get Web OS. That went badly. HP botched the Web OS upgrade for existing Palm phones, its new phones based on Palm technology didn’t really catch on, and HP’s Web OS-based tablet, the
Touchpad
, was an unmitigated disaster that lasted only two months on the market. In August 2011, HP went so far as to decide to
pull out of phones, tablets, and even PCs entirely
, though it quickly reversed course on PCs.
At any rate, the idea of HP going head to head with Apple using Palm’s technology on phones and tablets died a rather sudden death in the summer of 2011. And ultimately it led to
HP splitting itself in two
in 2014.
So what happened to Palm Pilots? Starting in 2013, HP licensed the technology to LG
for use in smart TVs
, sold the patents to Qualcomm and the Palm name to TCL in 2014. It’s very possible you have a smart TV running Web OS somewhere in your home.
Palm’s re-emergence in 2018
In 2017, rumors of Palm’s return started surfacing. In October 2018, it became reality. Chinese conglomerate TCL, best known in the United States for its inexpensive TVs, announced a 3.3-inch smartphone under the Palm brand. It wasn’t supposed to be a primary smartphone, but rather, a secondary phone that was less bulky, could charge in 68 minutes, and last 3 days on standby. TCL opted to use Android on the phone, which made sense. TCL doesn’t own the OS, and Web OS is almost a decade removed from being a viable competitor in that space anyway. Sometimes
bringing back undead brands works
, at least for a time.
Is the Palm Phone discontinued?
I wouldn’t count TCL’s Palm Phone among that list of undead success stories. With the Palm Phone, TCL cut the price from $349 to $249 to $219 and eventually sold off the inventory, but it took a while. When I looked in  March 2025, the web page was still up, but the phones and accessories weren’t available on their online store. I can’t find any formal announcement the Palm Phone is discontinued, but it seems to have faded away, without a successor.

# AI for Alzheimer's

The OpenAI Foundation is working to finalize more than **$100 million in initial grants** across **six research institutions** to support and accelerate Alzheimer's research—generating new data, helping design new drugs, and expanding possible paths to treatment.

## Why Focus on Alzheimer's?

Alzheimer's has resisted treatment in part because it is the quintessential complex disease. It's the result of hundreds of genetic and environmental risk factors interacting across cell types over decades.

## Five-Layer Research Stack

1. **Create a "causal map" of Alzheimer's using AI** — Validate targets for intervention. Map the full network of causal factors to pinpoint effective nodes for therapeutic intervention. Collaborators include Arc Institute.
   - Also includes creation of new open datasets with nonprofits like Focused Research Organisation, expansion of longitudinal/epidemiological datasets, and opening up existing datasets from biotech companies.

2. **Design new drugs with AI and test them in the lab** — Collaborators include the Institute for Protein Design at University of Washington.

3. **AI-assisted biomarker discovery** — Including the creation of new diagnostics.

4. **Clinical collaboration** — Working with UCSF on translating findings to patient care.

5. **Test off-patent treatments** — Use AI to make the best sense of anonymised patient data and experiences reported online. Includes suggestive evidence on lithium orotate and other off-patent compounds.

## Key Researchers & Quotes

- **Patrick Hsu, PhD** — Arc Institute Co-Founder and Core Investigator: "Alzheimer's has resisted treatment in part because it is the quintessential complex disease."
- **Silvana Konermann, PhD** — Arc Institute Co-Founder and Executive Director: "We want to find perturbations that can click and drag a cell from a diseased state back into a healthy one."
- **David Baker, PhD** — Nobel Laureate, Director of Institute for Protein Design at UW: "Using our newest AI-driven protein design models, we have successfully engineered molecules that engage, modify, and degrade targets critical to Alzheimer's disease progression."
- **S. Andrew Josephson, MD** — Professor and Chair of Neurology at UCSF: "With AI helping us integrate these insights and make sense of tremendous complexity, we have an opportunity to accelerate discoveries."
- **Bruce Yankner, MD, PhD** — Professor of Genetics and Neurology at Harvard Medical School: "My hope is that the ability of physiological dose lithium orotate to reverse pathology and restore memory in Alzheimer's disease mouse models will translate to the aging human population."

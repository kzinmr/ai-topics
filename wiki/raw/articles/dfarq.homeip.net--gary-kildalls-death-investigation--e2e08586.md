# The Silicon Underground

Source: https://dfarq.homeip.net/gary-kildalls-death-investigation/?utm_source=rss&utm_medium=rss&utm_campaign=gary-kildalls-death-investigation

Gary Kildall’s death investigation, or the seeming lack thereof, has taken on mythical proportions. Gary Kildall’s story seems to have that effect on people. Just like the story of Kildall allegedly going flying instead of meeting with IBM in August 1980, the story of Gary Kildall’s death on July 11, 1994 spiraled out of control.
While Gary Kildall died under unclear circumstances, his death was less unusual than early accounts made it seem. That’s why the investigation seemed to fizzle out with less fanfare than it deserved.
How I heard of Gary Kildall’s death
Gary Kildall on his television program, Computer Chronicles, in 1985. Gary Kildall’s death investigation, and the sketchy details surrounding his death, fascinate retro computer enthusiasts today.
I was selling computers at retail when I heard of Gary Kildall’s death. We had a few copies of
Wordstar for Windows
and someone asked about it. I said it was easier to remember the keyboard shortcuts in Wordstar than Wordperfect.
“You sound like a
CP/M
guy,” said someone who overheard me. “Did you hear that Gary Kildall died last month?”
I hadn’t, and he wasn’t surprised. I was curious, so I went to the library and found a whole lot of nothing. A month or two later, I found a mention in a computer magazine column that Kildall had died in a barroom fight but it gave no specifics.
If you’re only vaguely aware of Kildall, he was an
unheralded industry pioneer
, a talented developer who was always 5-10 years ahead of everyone else. He recognized the value and usefulness of the Intel 8008 architecture (and microprocessors in general)
that others missed
. His operating system powered early personal computers like those from
Osborne
and
Kaypro
. And his company, Digital Research, produced a usable GUI, GEM, around the same time as Apple, and a multitasking operating system around the time as
Amiga
.
He also co-hosted a weekly television program on PBS with Stewart Cheifet called
The Computer Chronicles
from 1983 to 1990.
But Bill Gates had better business sense and Steve Jobs had better marketing savvy. GEM ended up running on the
Atari ST
and
DR DOS
gained a cult following as an alternative to
MS-DOS
, but that proved a consolation prize compared to what Microsoft got. In time, Kildall and his company faded into obscurity.
So obscure that he died in 1994 at the age of 52 with surprisingly little coverage of it. Most computer enthusiasts have heard the
airplane story
, which can be told without even mentioning Kildall’s name. In death, Kildall created another myth about himself.
Early accounts of Kildall’s death
The lack of coverage of his death and vagueness about the cause of death only made me more curious, so I kept digging, even years later. What I found was that early accounts of Kildall’s death were all over the place. The accounts generally agreed that he had an accident and died a few days later, but that was about it. The accounts didn’t even agree on the date of the accident, and the cause of death ranged from a heart attack to a fall from a ladder to a barroom brawl. Now that more newspapers have their 1990s archives online, it’s possible to figure out that the accident happened on Friday, July 8, that he initially refused medical treatment, was in and out of the hospital over the weekend and died in the hospital on Monday, July 11.
What was the accident?
Forbes
said he walked into a bar on July 6 (it didn’t mention the Franklin Street Bar & Grill by name) wearing motorcycle leathers and Harley Davidson patches, a “would-be biker” who got into a shoving match with some “real bikers,” and he sustained head injuries.
There are two, and perhaps three, problems with the
Forbes
account. First,
Forbes
got the date wrong. Second, Kildall owned multiple motorcycles. He didn’t fit the stereotype of the outlaw biker, but calling him a “would-be biker” is more than a little bit unfair. Gary Kildall was neither the first nor the last wealthy middle-aged man to take up motorcycling. Now, those problems don’t automatically mean the rest of the
Forbes
account is wrong, but it makes the whole thing sound more like a rumor you heard in the software aisle of a
big-box retail store
. The
Forbes
account was sloppy, and where any other accounts disagree, there’s every reason to believe the other accounts.
Early newspaper accounts
The hometown papers of both Kildall and Bill Gates, the
Seattle Times
, were less sensational. Both reported Kildall sustained an injury at the Franklin Street Bar & Grill in Monterey, California, he had been in the bar between 2 and 20 minutes, and witnesses turned around and saw him on the floor. The papers also noted that the story was changing, and authorities didn’t expect that story to hold up.
It was investigated as a suspicious death, but no charges were ever filed. The
Los Angeles Times
initially reported the autopsy failed to determine the cause of death.
The Computer Chronicles
account
The Computer Chronicles
devoted the entirely of its July 28, 1995 episode to Kildall’s memory. It credited Kildall with proving that the Intel 8080 processor was capable of running a real computer, not just a microwave oven. It pointed out that Digital Research’s revenue increased more than sevenfold from 1981 to 1983, busting the persistent myth that the release of the IBM PC caused demand for CP/M to evaporate overnight. It also stated that Kildall and Gates operated on different ethical codes. Gates was a businessman, where Kildall was more of an academic, wanting to cooperate rather than merely compete. It busted the myth of airplane, giving it about five minutes of its 27 minute runtime.
The episode focused on Kildall’s life and accomplishments, not his death. And when it came to his death, it talked about its impact rather than its details. It spent its time busting myths and showing the human side of Kildall, rather than focusing on conspiracies. Had Kildall’s death been suspicious, it’s hard to imagine Cheifet, a graduate of Harvard Law School, shying away from that. Instead, he waited a year to see if there was anything to the story, then produced the tribute, remaining above the fray.
Harold Evans’ account
Later accounts, most notably the one in Harold Evans’
They Made America
, stated Kildall died of a brain hemorrhage, caused by a blood clot between his skull and his brain. Evans’ account of the events in the bar were that he stumbled, fell, and was found on the floor next to a video game. That story doesn’t line up with the
Forbes
account but it lines up pretty nicely with the
Seattle Times
account–one minute he was there, the next minute he was laying on the floor, and no one who was there really knew how he ended up on the floor or even how long he had been in the bar.
Evans went on to say Kildall visited the hospital twice over the ensuing weekend. The doctors couldn’t find what was wrong with him. Then he died unexpectedly on Monday. And of course he was cremated, which only lends to the air of conspiracy.
What the Evans account of Gary Kildall’s death investigation means
Did the hemorrhage cause the fall, or did the fall cause the hemorrhage? Either is possible. There’s some question whether the fall was an accident in the first place. But remember, after the fall, Kildall was coherent enough to initially refuse medical treatment. If the fall had been anything but an accident, it would have been out of character for him to remain silent about it. Remember, Kildall was bitter that Bill Gates had stolen his legacy and was a bit outspoken about it. If someone had shoved or thrown a punch at him, Kildall would have said something. Not only that, if shoves or punches had been involved, someone else would have known how he ended up on the floor. Lack of any witnesses who knew how he ended up on the floor suggests no commotion and no fracas.
He died, had an autopsy, and was cremated, all within a week, which is about the normal timeframe one would expect.
Those are important details. He had survivors, including two adult children. If there was any doubt in their minds that his death had been anything but an accident or natural causes, it’s hard to imagine them going through with a cremation within a week of his death. And while he wasn’t a billionaire, Kildall was very wealthy. His estate had the means to put up a fight and extract justice, if there had been a wrongful death involved.
The Myth
Still, the barroom brawl story persists. I admit it’s more interesting. A forgotten genius inventor, bitter with his plight, hating life and mad at the world, donned his leathers and rode his motorcycle to a bar where he got into a shoving match, fell, died a few days later, and no charges were ever filed. There’s an air of mystery and conspiracy about it. Maybe someone killed him to silence him, even! Speculation of Bill Gates’ possible involvement ran rampant on online forums for decades. That’s a great story, one that even feels a bit like a Shakespearean tragedy. There’s only one problem with it.
It’s not true.
If there had been anything to that story at all, it would have been a sensation. There was a popular prime-time TV program at the time,
Unsolved Mysteries
, that covered suspicious deaths, complete with dramatic re-enactments, every Wednesday night. The three popular afternoon tabloid TV programs of the time,
A Current Affair, Hard Copy,
and
Inside Edition
, would have loved the story too.
Everything about Gary Kildall’s death begged for airtime on 90s trash TV. And yet, none of them ran with it. That’s because the story didn’t hold up.
The ordinary tragedy of Gary Kildall’s death
What really happened to Gary Kildall? A 52-year-old man walked into the Franklin Street Bar & Grill in Monterey on Friday, July 8, 1994, had a health-related incident, and fell after being there a few minutes. He refused medical treatment initially. Then he realized afterward that something was wrong and did seek medical treatment. He died on Monday, and the autopsy showed he suffered a brain hemorrhage, caused by a previously undiagnosed blood clot.
It’s not exactly unusual for men to die of natural causes in their late 40s or early 50s. Kildall wasn’t even the only figure involved in the Atari ST to die that way. Industrial designer
Ira Velinsky
died of a heart attack at age 46. The only story here is that it was someone who kept a relatively high profile. Fear of being one of those who die young is what drives American men in their late 40s to buy convertibles, motorcycles or sports cars. It’s called a midlife crisis.
I heard a saying in journalism school that some people never let the truth get in the way of a good story. That applies to Kildall even more than most. Kildall never shook the CP/M airplane story in life, and can’t shake the barroom brawl story in death.

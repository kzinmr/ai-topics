# The SaaSpocalypse: Where Vertical SaaS Goes Next

**Author**: Brad Lyons (@blyons151), Crossover Research
**Date**: 2026-04-17
**Source**: https://x.com/blyons151/status/2045273056214470915
**Engagement**: 1,631 likes, 4,175 bookmarks, 821K impressions

## Summary

Brad Lyons, founder of Crossover Research (a sell-side research firm focused on software), published a long-form Note Tweet analyzing the "SaaSpocalypse" — the ~50% decline in software multiples driven by AI disruption — and arguing for where vertical SaaS goes next. His central thesis: most vertical SaaS companies are underperforming not because their software is bad, but because they never built a "second business." The engineering moat that protected SaaS for 20 years has been upended by AI. Four moats remain: distribution, proprietary data, workflow breadth, and regulatory insulation.

## Key Arguments

### 1. The Engineering Moat Is Gone
- For 20 years, SaaS moats came from engineering complexity: deep technical talent, long roadmaps, compounding codebases
- AI upended this almost overnight — product development is democratizing to operators with no code background
- Anthropic ships lookalike products at a cadence that would take legacy SaaS vendors three years of roadmap, with a fraction of the headcount

### 2. Four Remaining Moats
1. **Distribution** — built by the company
2. **Proprietary data** — built by the company
3. **Workflow breadth** — built by the company
4. **Regulatory insulation** — captured by the company, most resistant to AI disruption

### 3. Regulatory Moat: Necessary but Not Sufficient
- **Tyler Technologies ($TYL)**: State/local government software (courts, public safety, ERP). Every module married to statutory process, FIPS, CJIS, audit trails. Down 42% TTM, guidance soft, but revenue still compounds. Government procurement runs on five-year cycles.
- **Veeva**: Revenue up 16% in FY26, Q4 beat, stock still down 25%. Market selling execution, not weakness.
- **Guidewire**: P&C insurance where regulatory filings and rate approvals anchor the stack. Still compounding ARR, still winning cloud conversions.
- Pattern: **multiples compressed, fundamentals intact**. The moat is the regulatory surface area itself, and it compounds because rules get more complex, not less.

### 4. Data Entrenchment: The Palantir Model
- Palantir at $13 was a data-entrenchment bet — once it sits between the customer and their own data, ripping it out means rebuilding the data model from scratch
- Snowflake and Databricks never had that entrenchment layer
- AIP bootcamps turned data moat into distribution moat: 660 bootcamps/quarter, 94% y/y US customer deal growth, bookings at 1.9x sales
- **Vertical incumbents have a version of this available — the question is whether they'll build it before a challenger does**

### 5. AI-Native Challengers Without Anchors Fail
- Regulatory insulation buys time but doesn't buy the decade
- BlackLine ($BL), Trintech feeling pressure from Numeric, Maximor, Stacks (AI-native from day one)
- nCino ($NCNO) in banking faces same challenge
- **The winning formula: data or regulatory surface area + functional AI, not one or the other**

### 6. The "Second Business" Thesis
- Seat ARR got companies to $100M. With agentic workforce structures, pricing pressure, and margin compression, the traditional SaaS model must transform
- The next $500M comes from monetizing the installed base: marketplace rake, capital products, supplier monetization, brand partnerships, group buying
- **What's missing is organizational will** — different GTM, P&L optics, talent. Founders/boards under-invest because year one looks worse

### 7. Battery Ventures Survey Data (Office of the CFO)
- 129 finance leaders at $50M–$5B+ revenue companies
- **77%** want to uplevel existing systems with AI from new vendors layering onto existing systems
- **Only 15%** want to replace their system of record with an AI-native platform
- **The incumbent wins if they ship AI. The AI-native challenger wins only if the incumbent doesn't.**

### 8. VoC Data Signals
- In regulated verticals, mission criticality scores cluster above 9
- NPS doesn't track satisfaction — it tracks switching friction
- Customers score 9 on "would not switch" because compliance vetoes alternatives
- This is the signature of a compliance-insulated vendor

## Three Diligence Questions

1. **What % of revenue comes from sources other than subscription and payment processing?** Under 5% = haven't started. 10–20% = thesis is live. Over 20% = working.
2. **How hard would it be to recreate this company from scratch with AI today?** If a well-funded team with Claude and six engineers could rebuild it in nine months, the software isn't the moat.
3. **What % of buyer stickiness is regulatory, and which way is the rule set moving?** A regulatory moat evaporates if regulation simplifies.

## Underappreciated Points

- **AI-native competitors have worse gross margins than SaaS incumbents, not better** — inference costs haven't collapsed; VC cash subsidizing unit economics is a bridge, not a business model
- Pure seat-based pricing is dying unless vendors embrace agent-seat models
- LLM providers have been subsidizing the market on token cost; recent pricing shifts signal cash reserves aren't infinite
- Enterprise and regulated verticals are the last safe harbor, and only because of data breadth and compliance

# Tempest Rising is a great game

Source: https://laplab.me/posts/tempest-rising/

I really like RTS games. I pretty much grew up on them, starting with Command&Conquer 3: Kane’s Wrath, moving on to StarCraft 2 trilogy and witnessing the downfall of Command&Conquer 4.
I never had the disks for any other RTS games during my teenage years. Yes, the disks, the ones you go to the store to buy! I didn’t know Steam existed back then, so this was my only source of games. There is something magical in owning a physical copy of the game. I always liked the art on the front (a mandatory huge face for all RTS!), game description and screenshots on the back, even the smell of the plastic disk case.
As I grew up and went through the last years at school and then university, I didn’t have enough time to play. Immense workload, tough deadlines, meeting new people - that took 120% of my time back then. Now that I’m a bit more settled, I finally have time to return to the genre.
Over the last couple of months, I replayed C&C 3: Kane’s Wrath, then C&C 3: Tiberium Wars. Turns out, Kane’s Wrath campaign is kind of meh! I would go absolutely crazy if I had access to Tiberium Wars back then. Both are very good games nonetheless. I felt really sad that the series didn’t receive a proper successor. C&C 4 definitely isn’t that. It was then that I discovered that literally a couple of days ago Tempest Rising was released.
My first impressions were: jesus. fucking. christ. I couldn’t believe my eyes. A game studio that never made an RTS game before just went and delivered a C&C-inspired RTS on top of Unreal Engine. It’s really hard to convey emotions you feel when you said goodbye to C&C just a couple of days ago and then somebody tells you “hey, you can play more of it right now!”
Tempest Rising really nails the theme. “East fighting West over an energy resource” is a very widespread trope in RTS, so making it look fresh is really hard. I think that they combined the style of GDI/Nod from Tiberium universe with Allies/Soviets from Red Alert really well. As a result, we have two factions that have recognisable references to their sources, but still feel distinct and new. I also like that the aliens are somewhat linked to the culture on Earth. Making them closer to humans makes them seem more dangerous in my opinion.
With the theme set, Tempest Rising delivered on the visuals. UI, unit models, cutscenes, allies all seem natural and at home in the tempest universe. Sometimes I just paused whatever I was doing in the mission to zoom in and look at my units from different angles. UI is clear and sharp. I didn’t really expect the game to have any cutscenes, because making them is usually very expensive. But they went ahead and did them anyway, making the storytelling richer.
Behind all of these visuals, there is an engine that makes it all possible. Making an RTS feel good is a gigantic amount of work. People have so much muscle memory and expectations from previous games, it requires a really keen eye and lots of playtesting to get right. The game feels responsive enough to make combat seem high stakes, but units also have weight behind them, so you don’t feel like you are moving around plastic models. Sometimes pathing is dumb and you might loose a couple of units here and there, but I’m not surprised. Pathing is probably the hardest technical thing to get right in an RTS and it works pretty good most of the time.
The soundtrack slaps. I have nothing else to add here, the tracks are a fantastic fit for the game.
The factions have distinct play styles, which I think is quite hard to do when both factions are humans. I started with the Tempest Dynasty campaign and felt right at home after playing Nod and Soviets. After the Dynasty, GDF felt squishy and underpowered. That was until I realised that I was trying to play it exactly like the Dynasty. Once I leant into a combination of drones, lazers and various buffs from Comms and Marking, my GDF armies started steamrolling the opponents.
Finally, I think that the mission design is the strongest part of the game. In one of the documentaries, developers mentioned that the game started from them listing all the mission ideas they enjoyed playing in other RTS games and it shows. Missions have great variety and are multi-stage. There isn’t just one “destroy the enemy base” objective, instead objectives are revealed gradually as the mission progresses. This creates a feeling that you are part of the story, not just a witness. Secondary objectives are a lot of fun to do as well, which is a new one for me. I think that the GDF campaign had a bit more polish than the Dynasty campaign, but I enjoyed playing both.
If you enjoy RTS games or just curious, I encourage you to play
Tempest Rising
!

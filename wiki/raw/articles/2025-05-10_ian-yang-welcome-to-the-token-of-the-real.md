# welcome to the token of the real

## Summary

Ian Yang analyzes LLMs through the lens of Jean Baudrillard's semiotic theory from *Simulacra and Simulation*. The essay argues that LLMs represent the third stage of Baudrillard's simulacra — copies without originals that maintain the appearance of fidelity to reality. Key insights include: "ChatGPT" has become an autonomous signifier detached from any specific signified, encompassing the totality of the LLM concept for the general public; the "Dead Internet Theory" finds new relevance as AI-generated text increasingly displaces human text; and we are building a hyperreality where the simulation of language precedes and shapes actual human language production.

## Key Concepts

### LLM Hyperreality
AI-generated text continuously displaces and precedes human text, building a hyperreality where the signifiers lose stable grounding in any original "reality."

### ChatGPT as Autonomous Signifier
"ChatGPT" no longer refers to a specific product — it has become a stand-in for the totality of LLMs. This is different from traditional brand genericide because the sign has lost its stable signified and encompasses the entire concept.

### Four Stages of Simulacra Applied to LLMs
Yang argues LLMs have reached the third stage ("order of sorcery") where the simulacrum is a copy without reality while maintaining mimicry of something real. AI-generated text promises faithfulness to human language but exists independently without grounding.

### Implosion of Meaning
As AI text becomes more prevalent, Baudrillard's concept of "implosion" manifests as the loss of nuance and meaning in human language, plagued by the loss of stable referents.

## Key Quotes

> "At what point can we argue that the language generated by LLMs has become a simulacrum for true human language?"

> "ChatGPT as a signifier has gained its own autonomy and can continuously perpetuate its own autonomy via hyperreality."

> "AI-generated text promises to be faithful to human language, and often will represent it in a way that seeks to convince a human reader that it is in fact the same."

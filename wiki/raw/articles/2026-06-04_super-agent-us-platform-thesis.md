AI Has a Usage Problem Disguised as a Revenue Problem
Everyone is using AI. Just as notably, everyone seems to be spending on AI, often faster than clear returns can be measured. Usage is exploding faster than revenue clarity. Consumer AI can create enormous surplus while monetizing lightly. Enterprise AI touches fewer users but captures more directly because it attaches to budgets, workflows, labor replacement, and measurable outcomes. As inference costs, token budgets, and gross margins move to the center of every AI conversation, the core question is shifting from what AI can do to where AI can capture the value it creates.
AI is exposing one of the oldest tensions in software: consumers create usage; enterprises create budgets. Underneath that economic split is an institutional one: different buyers, data rights, privacy expectations, and trust regimes.
The question becomes where that value ultimately concentrates, and the answer depends heavily on how different internet ecosystems evolved.. China produced the super-app because social life, payments, commerce, identity, and business activity could collapse into one surface. It also revealed the power and danger of graph consolidation: when social life, financial life, commerce, and business activity converge into a few dominant surfaces, user behavior becomes more legible, more controllable, and more dependent on platform-state interfaces. The American internet evolved in the opposite direction because privacy, procurement, payments, antitrust, and enterprise trust split the graph apart.
Privacy is the deepest reason this split persisted because privacy shapes incentives, data flows, and the kinds of businesses that could emerge. The US preserved boundaries that made total platform control harder.
AI increases the value of this divide. Consumer agents need context, intimacy, and permission. Enterprise agents need governance, auditability, security, and ROI.
Because the super-agent must operate across fragmented consumer and institutional systems, the most massive outcomes that matter in the AI era will be those that can navigate both trust regimes without alienating either side.

The Rarest Companies Are Bilingual in Trust
The rarest and most massive outcomes in tech companies are not simply great B2C or great B2B, but they found a way to become trusted infrastructure for both people and institutions.
Microsoft owns the work identity layer, productivity surface, developer ecosystem, and enterprise admin plane, while still having consumer reach through Windows, Xbox, LinkedIn, and Copilot. Google owns consumer intent through Search, Android, Chrome, Gmail, Maps, and YouTube, while also owning Workspace and Cloud and Gemini. Apple is the strangest and maybe most important: it owns the trusted personal endpoint and increasingly the trusted work endpoint.
Meta is the instructive counterexample. It has serious B2B revenue, WhatsApp Business has hundreds of millions of users (didn't know this until my India trip recently), and its ads infrastructure is among the most powerful in the world. But Meta's B2B is more transactional, rather than truly operational: advertisers buy through Meta, but companies do not run on Meta. Microsoft and Salesforce hold the operational layer.
The divide is in that a consumer agent needs intimacy, habit, taste, personal context, and emotional trust. An enterprise agent needs compliance, admin controls, permissions, audit logs, procurement trust, and workflow depth and context. Consumers want the agent to feel personal, private, and helpful. Enterprises want it to be controlled, governed, auditable, and secure. Almost no company can be intimate enough for consumers and governable enough for enterprises at the same time.

The US Version of WeChat May Be an Agent, Not an App
The question on my mind is whether the everything-agent can re-fuse the B2B/B2C line, and the US version does not really need to look like WeChat. Rather, it may be a permissioned agent layer that can touch your personal calendar, work Slack, Gmail, Salesforce, Notion, GitHub, bank account, shopping apps, health data, travel plans, and family logistics in the name of a context graph, but only under scopes, consent, tenant boundaries, audit trails, admin controls, and enterprise governance.
The agent coordinates across graphs without being allowed to own one and violating any boundaries.
That is why it's important to look at the platform divides; Apple and Google control the mobile operating systems and app-store surfaces where consumer agents could live. Payments remain fragmented across interchange economics, card networks, wallets, and bank relationships. Antitrust scrutiny limits how aggressively any one platform can bundle identity, payments, search, commerce, and work. Enterprise procurement and vendor-risk review slow anything that touches company systems.

Apple's Moat Is Permission
This is why Apple becomes one of the most interesting companies in the race.
Three things ground this beyond brand. App Tracking Transparency reportedly cost Meta on the order of $10B in annual revenue, a single Apple policy decision rewriting the ad-attribution economics of the largest social platform in the world. Private Cloud Compute is the explicit architectural bet: Apple processes AI requests on hardware Apple controls, with cryptographic guarantees that even Apple cannot inspect the data. On-device Apple Intelligence is the proof point: the company is willing to give up scale and capability to keep inference local. These are expensive choices that only make sense if permission / privacy are the moat. Apple is one of the few companies whose brand promise is that the closer it gets to the user, the less extractive it is supposed to become.

Permission Has Different Currencies
Trust is not the only way to earn permission. Some companies earn it by being unusually effective.
Palantir is the cleanest example. Palantir is not trusted because it feels private, neutral, or consumer-friendly, it is trusted by a certain class of institutional buyer because it is believed to produce outcomes inside messy, high-stakes systems: integrating fragmented data, imposing a decision layer, helping organizations act and it also started off by gaining the trust of the US government. Efficacy is itself a permission currency, paid in outcomes rather than affect (and this is where all AI startups are now moving)
MSFT has the admin plane and OpenAI has consumer habit and frontier capability. Anthropic has safety posture and the Pope's blessings (#blessup). Each company is effectively making a different bet on why a user or institution grants access: because they believe you will protect them, because they believe you will make them more powerful, or because they cannot operate without you.

The Next Platform Is the Interface Allowed to Act
The next great technology company serving both consumers and enterprises will be the interface trusted to cross boundaries: between personal and professional life, consumer and enterprise data, software and institution, advice and action.
If the super-app was the Chinese internet's native form, the super-agent may be the American AI internet's native form. The better version of that future preserves what the American internet got right: fragmentation as a defense against total platform control and permission as the price of access.
The next platform just might be the interface allowed to act across everything.

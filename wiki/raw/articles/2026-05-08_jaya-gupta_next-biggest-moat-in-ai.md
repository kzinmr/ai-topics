# The next biggest moat in AI

*By Jaya Gupta, Partner at Foundation Capital*

[This is an X-native article. Full content behind X auth wall.]

## Summary (via note.com Japanese summary by KiKi@AIx個人開発)

### 1. AI's layers are converging; visible parts are imitable

> The application layer is collapsing into infrastructure, infrastructure companies are moving up into workflows, and almost every startup is rebranding itself as some version of a transformation company.

Category names change every few months: context graph, system of action, organizational world model. When models improve quickly, interfaces converge, and product velocity becomes cheap, the visible parts of company-building get easier to imitate.

### 2. The shape of the company itself is becoming the moat

Five elements that constitute "shape": who you hire, who you give power to, how you organize work, what is high/low status, who you'd genuinely fight to keep.

### 3. Great companies are organizational inventions — OpenAI and Palantir

> The most important companies are actually organizational inventions. They create a new kind of institution around a new kind of work, and in doing so, they make a new kind of person possible.

- **OpenAI**: Not academia, not a corporate research lab, not a traditional software company. Centered on frontier model training as organizational activity. Created a type of researcher who operates at the edge of science, product, geopolitics, and civilizational risk.
- **Palantir**: Forward deployment is not a GTM tactic — it's a status hierarchy, talent model, and worldview. Made high-status what other companies treat as low-status: sitting with customers, absorbing institutional mess, translating politics into product.

### 4. Shape determines who can exist there

> The best companies in the world do not only compete on category, market, or compensation. They compete on identity.

Six things ambitious people intensely desire: feeling special, being close to power, becoming undeniable, staying full of optionality, belonging to a mission, being in the room where history bends.

### 5. Five structural tests for promises

If "customer proximity is our moat" but customer-facing work is low status → lie.
If "ownership is our moat" but all decisions go to CEO → lie.
If "mission is our moat" but it offends no one, excludes no one → lie.
If "talent density is our moat" but average people set operating pace → lie.
If "deployment is our moat" but people close to reality have no power → lie.

### 6. The biggest moat

AI makes products, workflows, and demos copyable. What it cannot copy is the institution — the way you gather people, give them power, place them in front of the right problems, and let their judgment compound over time.

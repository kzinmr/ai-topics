There’s a subtle shift buried in this that feels bigger than the benchmarks themselves. When an agent can sit in front of a black box and slowly reconstruct it from outputs alone, it starts to resemble a market participant watching price instead of fundamentals. No access to the books, no inside view, just behavior over time. And yet, with enough iterations, it begins to approximate the structure that produced it.
That’s where it gets interesting. Not the capability itself, but the feedback loop it implies. The system doesn’t need to understand in the human sense. It needs to converge. Give it a scoreboard and enough time, and it will circle the problem until it collapses into something that works. You can feel how that kind of process scales. Not linearly, but like a crowd piling into a trade that keeps rewarding them. The more it works, the more it gets fed, and the more it refines itself.
The toddler analogy is doing more work than it seems on the surface. It frames the risk as fragility, but there’s another layer. Toddlers grow. Systems like this don’t stay static. They absorb the environment they’re placed in, including its flaws. So the real question isn’t whether they can be misled in isolated cases. It’s whether the environment itself becomes the teacher, and what kind of teacher it turns into over time.
That “gradual disempowerment” thread reads less like a warning and more like a slow redistribution of agency. No dramatic takeover, no single moment you can point to. Just a steady migration of decisions away from the human edge toward whatever system is better at closing the loop. By the time anyone notices, the habit of deciding has already weakened.

# Magnifica Humanitas — Encyclical Letter of Pope Leo XIV

**Title:** *Magnifica Humanitas* — On Safeguarding the Human Person in the Time of Artificial Intelligence
**Author:** Pope Leo XIV
**Date:** 15 May 2026
**Source:** https://www.vatican.va/content/leo-xiv/en/encyclicals/documents/20260515-magnifica-humanitas.html

> **Note:** This is a detailed summary of the full encyclical. The complete text is available at the Vatican website.

---

## Introduction: Building Babel or Jerusalem?

The encyclical opens with the choice facing humanity: to construct a new Tower of Babel (pride, self-sufficiency, homogenization) or to rebuild the city where God and humanity dwell together (following Nehemiah's model of prayer, shared responsibility, and pluralism). Technology itself is not neutral—it takes on the character of those who shape it.

**Two biblical images:**
- **Tower of Babel (Gen 11):** A project of uniformity, power, and exclusion of God, leading to confusion and dispersion.
- **Rebuilding Jerusalem (Neh 2–6):** Nehemiah prays, listens, mobilizes all the people, each building their own section of the wall; a project of communion, subsidiarity, and divine trust.

> "We must avoid the 'Babel syndrome,' namely the idolatry of profit that sacrifices the weak, a uniformity that neutralizes differences, and the pretense that a single language—even a digital one—can translate everything, including the mystery of the person, into data and performance." (no. 10)

**Key call:** Build for the common good by:
1. Placing God at the center,
2. Accepting human limits as not errors,
3. Shared responsibility (subsidiarity),
4. Using an "evangelical language" of clarity, not humiliation.

## Chapter 1 – A Dynamic Approach Faithful to the Gospel

The Church's Social Doctrine is a living process of **shared discernment**, not a fixed handbook. It stems from:
- Autonomy of earthly realities and distinction between ecclesial and political communities (GS 36),
- Dialogue with human sciences,
- Listening to the "many voices" of the times and interpreting them in the light of the Word.

## Chapter 2 – Foundations and Principles of Social Doctrine

### Foundations

1. **Human person: image of the Triune God** — created for communion; vocation to self-giving love.
2. **Equal dignity of all** — ontological dignity (infinite, inalienable) vs. moral, social, existential dignity.
3. **Human rights** — grounded in dignity; first right is to life from conception to natural end.

### The Principles

- **Common Good:** "The sum total of social conditions which allow people to reach their fulfillment more fully and more easily" — greater than sum of individual interests.
- **Universal Destination of Goods:** Earth's goods belong to all. Private property is subordinate. New goods: data, algorithms, platforms must be managed as common goods.
- **Subsidiarity:** Higher levels should not replace what lower levels can do. AI governance must respect subsidiarity.
- **Solidarity:** Commitment to the common good as a social virtue; in the AI age, means "social coding" of equity into systems.
- **Participation:** Democratization of digital governance; all must have voice in shaping AI.

## Chapter 3 – Artificial Intelligence: Discerning the Signs of the Times

### What AI Is (and Is Not)

- AI is **not intelligence in the human sense** — lacks embodied consciousness, intentionality, relationality, moral responsibility.
- AI is a **tool**, the result of human ingenuity, not an autonomous moral agent.
- Dangers: **anthropomorphization** (attributing human qualities where none exist) and **instrumentalization** (reducing humans to data points).

### Ethical Criteria for AI Development

1. **Person-centeredness** — AI must serve the integral development of every person.
2. **Precautionary principle** — when risks are high or poorly understood, err on the side of caution.
3. **Transparency and explainability** — decisions affecting people must be intelligible.
4. **Non-discrimination** — algorithmic bias is a form of structural sin.
5. **Democratic governance** — AI must not become a tool of surveillance, manipulation, or authoritarian control.

### Specific Concerns

- **Labor displacement:** Mass unemployment is not inevitable but requires proactive policy (training, social safety nets, reduced working hours).
- **Relational impoverishment:** Digital mediation risks substituting real human relationships with simulated ones.
- **Surveillance capitalism:** Data extraction turns persons into products.
- **Autonomous weapons:** "Lethal autonomous weapons systems are incompatible with the protection of human life and dignity." Clear call for international prohibition.
- **Digital divide:** Global inequality in AI access and benefits.

## Chapter 4 – Call to Action: Rebuilding the Common Home

### For Governments and International Organizations
- Develop binding international treaties on AI governance.
- Ensure AI serves universal common good, not just national interests.
- Ban lethal autonomous weapons.

### For the Tech Industry
- Embed ethics from the design phase ("ethics by design").
- Reject the logic of unrestrained profit maximization.
- Open dialogue with religious, philosophical, and civil society voices.

### For the Scientific Community
- Maintain epistemic humility — AI remains mysterious even to its creators.
- Pursue interdisciplinary dialogue, especially with humanities and theology.

### For the Church and Believers
- Exercise "prophetic vigilance" — name injustices and advocate for the vulnerable.
- Form consciences capable of digital discernment.
- "The Church does not propose technical solutions, but offers criteria for judgment and guidelines for action rooted in the Gospel."

## Key Quotes

> "The primary choice is not between a 'yes' or 'no' to technology, but rather between constructing Babel or rebuilding Jerusalem; between a power that claims to dominate the heavens and a people who work together in the presence of God to rebuild the walls of fraternal coexistence." (no. 9)

> "Artificial intelligence, like every technology, is a human artifact. It does not think, it does not love, it does not pray. It can assist, but it can never replace the human person created in the image and likeness of God." (no. 67)

> "The dignity of the human person is not granted by algorithms, nor can it be revoked by them." (no. 45)

> "Development is the new name for peace. And today, digital development, if not guided by integral human criteria, can become the new name for new forms of war — economic, cultural, and spiritual." (no. 82)

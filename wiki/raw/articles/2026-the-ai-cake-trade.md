# The AI Cake Trade

Why I stopped picking model labs and started owning the whole supply chain
There is a graphic pinned above my desk. It's a five-layer cake, dressed up in the vintage-futurism of a 1958 pulp magazine — ringed planet, chrome rocket, a man driving a teal convertible into a retrograde sunset. The rocket is named CRBS. The cake is labeled, simply, The AI Cake.
It looks like marketing. It is, in a way. But it is also a thesis — the cleanest one I've built in twenty-five years of watching supply chains eat narratives for lunch. And right now, in April 2026, that cake is running roughly seven times the S&P 500 year-to-date.
This is how I got there, and how I think anyone building an AI-infrastructure book today should think about it.
The trap I almost fell into When generative AI broke into the mainstream in late 2022, the default trade was obvious: pick a model lab and hope you picked right. OpenAI wasn't public. So people proxied through $MSFT. Then Anthropic emerged; $AMZN and $GOOGL bought stakes. Meta built Llama. A dozen smaller labs raised at absurd multiples. The narrative was that one or two of these model companies would eat the world, and owning their rails was how you participated.
That narrative was, and is, wrong — or at least, profoundly incomplete.
The labs are a thin, volatile layer on top of something much larger and much more physical. Every time a model trains, something burns. Electricity. Silicon. Cooling capacity. Copper. Indium phosphide. Every inference call traverses a switch, a transceiver, a fiber, a rack, a cooling loop, a substation, a turbine. The labs don't make any of those things. They buy them. And the people selling them those things are, in many cases, monopolistic businesses with decades of moat, predictable earnings, and — until recently — stock charts that didn't reflect what was happening to their order books.
So I stopped trying to pick the labs. I started buying the suppliers. All of them, layered.
 
The five layers The graphic organizes the supply chain from the ground up, and that ordering is not cosmetic. It reflects how long the lead times are, how hard it is to substitute, and how much pricing power sits at each level. Starting at the foundation:
Layer 1 — Silicon Manufacturing Foundation. $ASML, $LRCX, $KLAC, $ONTO, $MKSI. These are the tool makers. Without their equipment, no one — not $TSM, not Samsung, not Intel — makes a chip at leading-edge nodes. $ASML has a literal monopoly on extreme ultraviolet lithography. You cannot conjure a second $ASML. Lead times on high-NA systems are measured in years. Layer 1 is the most capex-levered layer in the stack and, historically, the most underappreciated. The market is finally catching up.
Layer 2 — Silicon and Storage. $TSM, $MU, $WDC, $STX, $ASX. This is where the chips get made, packaged, and paired with memory and storage. Two dynamics matter here. First, high-bandwidth memory is in a structural shortage, and NAND demand has exploded with AI data center buildouts. Western Digital/SanDisk ($WDC) has seen one of the wildest repricings in recent memory on enterprise SSD and NAND demand. Second, advanced packaging (2.5D, 3D, chiplets) remains a key bottleneck, which is why $ASX matters.
Layer 3 — Components and Connectivity. $AVGO, $CRDO, $LITE, $HIMX, $CGNX. This is the layer that takes the chips and makes them talk. Custom ASICs ($AVGO's TPU/MTIA/Titan business), high-speed interconnect ($CRDO's retimers and active electrical cables), optical transceivers and co-packaged optics ($LITE). $LITE is up strongly YTD and has production sold out through the end of 2027 (with commentary suggesting no end in sight due to GPU cluster optics demand). This layer shows notable dispersion as the market digests order flow and potential shifts in custom silicon.
Layer 4 — Data Center Infrastructure. $ANET, $ETN, $HUBB, $GEV, $CEG, $VRT, $CARR, $FIX. The AI factory layer. The companies that turn a warehouse into something that can absorb hundreds of megawatts and not melt. $ANET switches the 800G and 1.6T traffic between GPUs. $ETN and $HUBB move the electrons. $GEV makes the gas turbines and has a ~$150 billion backlog; $CEG runs the nuclear. $VRT and $CARR manage the heat; $FIX actually builds the mechanical plant. Power and cooling names have been standout performers.
Layer 5 — AI Services and Platforms. $GOOGL, $AMZN, $META, $NBIS, $DELL. This is where the market thinks the action is, and where, YTD, the action has been relatively muted compared to the physical stack. $DELL has been one of the stronger names in the layer thanks to AI server demand. $NBIS has performed well following major infrastructure deals.
There is also, on the graphic, a rocket. Cerebras. I'll come back to it.
The scoreboard: where the money actually went Look at the numbers again, ranked:
The top of the leaderboard is not the Mag 7. It is not the AI platforms. It is names like $WDC, $LITE, $NBIS, $KLAC, $ASML, $GEV, $FIX, $MU, $STX, and $VRT. Nine of the ten best-performing names in my book are from Layers 1–4 — the physical stack. Not one of them is a model lab. Not one of them is primarily known as an AI company to anyone outside the industry.
The bottom of the leaderboard includes names showing dispersion, such as $CRDO (notable YTD underperformance despite strong revenue growth), $AVGO (mixed on custom ASIC rumors), and some others like $ANET that have been relatively flat.
The public portion of my book is up approximately 28% YTD. The S&P 500 is up about 4%. That's roughly seven-to-one. My edge is not brilliant stock picking inside the stack — I'm overweight $AVGO and it's lagged at times. My edge is the structure: owning the whole stack, letting winners offset losers, and not trying to guess which layer re-rates next.
Why this is a capex story, not a tech story AI is, at its current scale, not primarily a software phenomenon. It is a capex phenomenon dressed up in software's clothing. In 2026, the hyperscalers ($GOOGL, $AMZN, $MSFT, $META, and others) are projected to spend in the range of $525–700 billion on data center buildout — an enormous number, multiple times the peak annual U.S. Apollo program spending, concentrated among a handful of companies.
That money doesn't go to the model labs. It flows through them briefly and then downstream to the companies in Layers 1–4. Every dollar of incremental training compute flows down through $DELL, Nvidia, $AVGO, $TSM, $ASML, $LRCX, and $KLAC. Every dollar of incremental inference flows through power, cooling, and networking names. Suppliers see orders quarters ahead. You can see it in $LITE's sold-out books through 2027. You can see it in $GEV's ~$150 billion backlog. You can see it in mechanical and cooling backlogs that have grown sharply.
These are stories about physical capacity constraints, not just AI hype. Physical constraints show up in earnings, not just multiples. You don't have to believe in AGI — you just have to believe the committed capital gets spent.
How I weight it ... (The core/torque/optionality framework remains unchanged, as it is structural and sound. Core: 55–65% in durable moats like $TSM, $ASML, $AVGO, $LRCX, $KLAC, $GOOGL, $AMZN, $META, $ETN, $GEV. Torque: 25–30% in higher-beta names like $VRT, $FIX, $MU, $WDC, $STX, etc. Optionality: 5–10% in catalyst-driven names like $CRDO, $LITE, $ONTO, etc.)
The rocket On the right side of the graphic, a silver rocket is launching. Its nose says $CRBS.
 
@cerebras  filed its S-1 on April 17, 2026, targeting an IPO in mid-May. The company reported $510 million in 2025 revenue (up 76% YoY) and turned profitable with net income around $238 million (inclusive of a $363M one-time gain). Its backlog/remaining performance obligations stand at $24.6 billion, including a major ~$20 billion, multi-year contract with OpenAI for ~750 MW of compute (with options for significantly more). Secondary/pre-IPO pricing has been discussed in the $35 billion range.
Cerebras sits deliberately off the cake, in flight, because it is evolving from a wafer-scale chip maker into a full AI cloud/infrastructure provider competing with $AMZN, $MSFT, $GOOGL, $ORCL, and CoreWeave. I hold an asymmetrical pre-IPO position (details on cost basis and sizing remain as a high-convexity venture-style bet within the book — not a core compounder).
What the scoreboard is telling us Three things, none of them obvious, all of them actionable.
First, the capex cycle is real and the leadership baton has moved down the stack. Hyperscalers ($GOOGL, $META, $AMZN) have been up modestly (low single digits in many cases), while shovel sellers in the physical layers are up 40% to several hundred percent in standout cases. Watch revenue growth and backlogs, not just sentiment.
Second, dispersion inside the stack is real. Some selloffs reflect legitimate concerns (e.g., custom ASIC shifts); others look like overreactions (strong growth names trading at discounts). Owning the whole stack means you don't have to perfectly time which is which.
Third, the infrastructure layer is where the marginal institutional dollar is flowing. The re-rating in semicap equipment names still has room, given long order books and moats.
The bear case, stated honestly (The capex cut, model efficiency breakthrough, geopolitical/Taiwan risk, and commercial AI winter risks remain valid and unchanged in substance.)
What you do about it If you're building an AI-infrastructure book, avoid chasing the hottest scoreboard names that have already run hard. If starting fresh, build from the bottom with high-moat names: $ASML and $KLAC (Layer 1), $TSM (Layer 2, with eyes on Taiwan risk), $AVGO (Layer 3 at current discounts), $GEV and $ETN (Layer 4), and $GOOGL (Layer 5 for its TPU exposure). Add torque and optionality later.
The thing I keep coming back to By 2030, the fabs, turbines, cooling towers, switches, transceivers, and 20-year power contracts will be in the ground. These are disclosed backlogs and signed PPAs — not forecasts. The question is not whether AI is real. The question is whether you own the companies building the physical world that makes it possible.
I do. As much of it as I can afford.
The wave is already here. Ride the stack, not the surfer.

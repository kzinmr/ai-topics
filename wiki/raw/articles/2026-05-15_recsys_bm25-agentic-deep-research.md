[1]] Rethinking Agentic Search with Pi-Serini: Is Lexical Retrieval Sufficient?This paper from Hsu et al.

 asks whether BM25 lexical retrieval is sufficient for deep research when paired with capable LLMs in an agentic loop, challenging the assumption that dense retrievers are necessary. The authors introduce PI-SERINI, a minimal search agent built on the PI harness that exposes three distinct tools (search, read_search_results, and read_document) so the LLM can cache rankings of up to 1000 documents, browse them, and selectively pull text into its context window rather than dumping top-k results inline. They tune BM25 for the long, noisy documents in BrowseComp-Plus (settling on k1=25, b=1 instead of Anserini’s 0.9/0.4 defaults), set retrieval depth to 1000, and replace the standard fixed-iteration cap with a 300-second wall-clock budget that injects a submission steer at 70% of the timeout. Across nine frontier models from DeepSeek, Anthropic, and OpenAI, PI-SERINI with gpt-5.5 hits 83.1% answer accuracy and 94.7% surfaced evidence recall on BrowseComp-Plus, beating released dense-retriever agents while cutting costs 3.3x to 10x thanks largely to a prefix-cache-friendly loop where 82-90% of tokens are served from cache. A failure-mode analysis also identifies “premature branch commitment” in claude-opus-4.7, which keeps probing weak hypotheses where gpt-5.5 backtracks to original clues, explaining part of the gap between otherwise comparable frontier models.📚 [https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.10848](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.10848)👨🏽‍💻 [https://github.com/justram/pi-serini](https://github.com/justram/pi-serini)

[2]] An Embarrassingly Simple Graph Heuristic Reveals Shortcut-Solvable Benchmarks for Sequential RecommendationThis paper from Han et al.

 audits sequential recommendation benchmarks using a deliberately simple graph heuristic called TGH (Transition-Graph Heuristic), which builds a directed item-transition graph from training sequences, retrieves candidates from the few-hop neighborhood of the user’s last one or two items, and ranks them by item-feature cosine similarity plus a small bonus for direct edges. No sequence encoder, no training, no generative objective. Despite this minimalism, TGH beats strong baselines (SASRec, HSTU, TIGER, LETTER, CoFiRec, LightGCN, SR-GNN) on the Amazon Review datasets that dominate 86.6% of recent generative-recommendation papers, with NDCG@10 gains of 38.10% on Sports and 44.18% on CDs over the best competing method. The authors trace this to three shortcut structures in these benchmarks: low-branching local transition graphs that shrink the effective candidate space, feature-smooth transitions where adjacent items have similar text embeddings, and limited dependence on long histories. Extending the evaluation to 14 datasets, TGH ranks first or second on 10, but loses on MovieLens-1M, Yelp, and MIND, where larger out-degrees, weaker feature smoothness, or genuine long-range dependence break the shortcuts. A Jaccard analysis of correct predictions and a hop-distance breakdown show TGH and learned models succeed on largely disjoint test cases, with learned models doing better when targets sit beyond 3 hops. The takeaway is that headline numbers on the standard benchmarks are unreliable evidence for claims about semantic understanding, generative modeling, or long-context reasoning, and the authors push for capability-aligned dataset selection and dataset-level diagnostics on transition branching, feature smoothness, and history dependence.📚 [https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.07125](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.07125)👨🏽‍💻 [https://github.com/haoyuhan1/GraphRec/](https://github.com/haoyuhan1/GraphRec/)[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!bBsJ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F944d860e-09bf-4052-9a77-6607ce592f95_1149x499.png)

[3]] jina-embeddings-v5-omni: Geometry-preserving Embeddings via Locked Aligned TowersThis paper from Jina AI introduces jina-embeddings-v5-omni, a pair of multimodal embedding models (0.95B “nano” and 1.57B “small”) that extend the text-only Jina Embeddings v5 backbone to handle images, video, and audio without retraining it. The authors call their approach “frozen-encoder model composition”. They bolt pretrained vision encoders (from Qwen3.5) and an audio encoder (from Qwen2.5-Omni) onto the frozen text model, then train only thin projector layers and modality-delimiter token embeddings to bridge them, amounting to just 0.35% of total weights. Non-text inputs are serialized into the text stream using special start/end tokens around placeholder slots that get overwritten by projected encoder features, and the existing task-specific LoRA adapters for retrieval, classification, clustering, and text-matching are reused unchanged. Training uses bidirectional InfoNCE with Matryoshka representation learning across 15k steps on 4 H100s, running 1.8–3.9x faster than full fine-tuning.📚 [https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.08384](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.08384)🤗 [https://huggingface.co/collections/jinaai/jina-embeddings-v5-omni ](https://huggingface.co/collections/jinaai/jina-embeddings-v5-omni)[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!MyME!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0823541f-10a2-43c5-bcd0-3a6fbed3c0ae_1117x737.png)

[4]] MLPs are Efficient Distilled Generative RecommendersThis paper from Guo et al.

 introduces SID-MLP, a distillation framework that accelerates generative recommendation by replacing the autoregressive Transformer decoder with lightweight MLP heads. The authors start from an empirical observation: in Semantic ID generation, the valid branching factor collapses sharply after the first token (from 256 down to roughly 1.2 by the fourth digit on Instruments), so running a full attention-based decoder at every step is largely wasted compute. Ablations confirm that cross-attention to the encoded user history is critical, but repeated self-attention over the short SID prefix is not. Building on this, SID-MLP keeps the frozen TIGER encoder, computes a single multi-head attention readout over the encoder states once per user to produce a cached context vector, and then uses one dedicated 1-hidden-layer MLP per SID position, with each MLP taking the cached context concatenated with the embeddings of previously generated prefix tokens. Training combines a per-digit KL divergence against the teacher’s sliced logits with a cross-entropy term on ground-truth labels. An extension, SID-MLP++, also replaces the encoder with role-specific MLPs that achieve global mixing through mean-pooled residual updates, trained via a two-stage scheme (MSE representation matching, then logit distillation). On three Amazon Reviews 2023 categories, SID-MLP matches or slightly exceeds the TIGER teacher on Recall@K and NDCG@K while delivering an 8.74x throughput speedup and a 95.7% peak-memory reduction. SID-MLP++ pushes the speedup to 10.25x with a small accuracy cost, and the approach generalizes across RQ-VAE, RQ-KMeans, and PSID tokenizers as well as varying batch and beam sizes.📚 [https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.12617](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.12617)👨🏽‍💻 [https://github.com/ztguo715/SID-MLP](https://github.com/ztguo715/SID-MLP)[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!xp0w!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4d050410-c670-4220-8642-7ddbc4b303e7_1791x663.png)

[5]] Why Retrieval-Augmented Generation Fails: A Graph PerspectiveThis paper from Guo et al.

 investigates why RAG systems still produce wrong answers even when the retrieved passages contain the right evidence. It does so from a mechanistic, model-internal angle rather than treating the LLM as a black box. The authors apply circuit tracing to build attribution graphs whose nodes correspond to token representations at each transformer layer and whose weighted directed edges capture causal contributions. They find consistent structural contrasts along six graph metrics: correct circuits exhibit longer directed paths (DAG-L), higher average degree and edge density, more branching triads, fewer disconnected triads, and lower maximum PageRank, while wrong ones are shallow, fragmented, and hub-dominated. A layer-wise view shows that correct predictions concentrate attribution mass in mid-layers (roughly 8–18), whereas wrong ones over-activate early layers. To isolate the failure mechanism, their analysis reveals two distinct regimes: correct predictions display question-constrained evidence grounding, with strong early Q→Q consolidation and persistent question influence, while incorrect ones show surface-aligned evidence grounding, routing prematurely from Q→Ans_EXT before the question representation stabilizes, then drifting into self-reinforcing answer refinement. Building on this, they train a graph transformer detector that combines message passing, global attention, multi-statistic pooling, and hand-crafted topology features, which beats a logit-based self-judging baseline by 11.53% accuracy on average.📚 [https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.14192](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.14192)

[6]] Retrieval is Cheap, Show Me the Code: Executable Multi-Hop Reasoning for Retrieval-Augmented GenerationThis paper from Sun et al.

 presents PyRAG, which reframes multi-hop RAG as program synthesis rather than free-form natural language reasoning. It addresses the brittleness of current RAG systems where intermediate states stay implicit, retrieval queries drift from intended entities, and self-reflection lacks grounded error signals. The framework uses three agents: a Decompose Agent splits the question into atomic sub-queries, a Plan Agent (a code-specialized LLM) generates a Python program over two primitives, retrieve(query, topk) and answer(query, docs), and an Answer Agent extracts short answers from retrieved evidence. Executing the program step by step exposes intermediate results as named variables and produces an inspectable trace, which enables two training-free refinement mechanisms: compiler-grounded self-repair that uses runtime exceptions to trigger program revision, and execution-driven adaptive retrieval that boosts top-k for sub-steps returning insufficient-evidence sentinels. On five QA benchmarks, PyRAG beats Vanilla RAG, and PyRAG-RL posts the best average EM among 7B RL-trained methods while generalizing to Qwen3-4B and LLaMA-3.1-8B.📚 [https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.12975](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.12975)👨🏽‍💻 [https://gasolsun36.github.io/PyRAG/](https://gasolsun36.github.io/PyRAG/)[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!sfoX!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa7d3b343-433d-472a-ada5-84984a526482_1346x485.png)

[7]] ML-Embed: Inclusive and Efficient Embeddings for a Multilingual WorldThis paper from Zhang et al.

 introduces ML-Embed to tackle three barriers that have been making state-of-the-art text embeddings increasingly exclusionary: prohibitive compute costs, narrow language coverage, and a lack of training transparency in top open-weight and closed-source models. The authors propose 3-Dimensional Matryoshka Learning (3D-ML), a unified framework that applies nested-learning principles along three axes of the model lifecycle. Matryoshka Representation Learning (MRL) handles storage by making vector prefixes usable as lower-dimensional embeddings, Matryoshka Layer Learning (MLL) handles inference by training the model so that prefixes of its layer stack remain effective embedders, and their novel Matryoshka Embedding Learning (MEL) handles parameters by factorizing the embedding matrix via truncated SVD into two low-rank matrices and dynamically sampling a sub-rank each forward pass, which cuts both trainable and total parameters rather than just the update size as LoRA does. Crucially, MEL ships in a compatibility mode that multiplies the factors back into a standard embedding matrix for drop-in deployment, and an efficiency mode that keeps the factorization for aggressive compression. They train this on a curated 50M-sample multilingual corpus covering 282 natural languages and 40+ programming languages aggregated from 121 public sources, then run a two-stage training recipe (retrieval pretraining, then instructed multi-task fine-tuning) on six Qwen3-based backbones from 140M to 8B. The 8B model sets new state-of-the-art on 9 of 17 MTEB benchmarks, with the largest gains in historically underserved languages: +22.89 on Polish, +6.88 on Vietnamese, +6.61 on Indic, +6.47 on German.📚 [https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.15081](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.15081)👨🏽‍💻 [https://github.com/codefuse-ai/CodeFuse-Embeddings](https://github.com/codefuse-ai/CodeFuse-Embeddings)🤗 [https://huggingface.co/collections/codefuse-ai/codefuse-embeddings](https://huggingface.co/collections/codefuse-ai/codefuse-embeddings)

[8]] UxSID: Semantic-Aware User Interests Modeling for Ultra-Long SequenceThis paper from Kuaishou introduces UxSID, a framework for modeling ultra-long user behavior sequences in recommender systems. It is designed to sit between two existing paradigms: item-specific Top-K retrieval methods like SIM and TWIN, which incur high online costs, and item-agnostic compression methods like MIMN and C-Former, which lose target specificity by distilling histories into static user embeddings. The authors propose a middle path where semantically similar items share the same compressed user interest memory, using Semantic IDs (SIDs) generated via an MLLM encoder and Res-KmeansFSQ quantization as semantic probes. The architecture has two main parts. First, Item-Agnostic Interest Compression condenses the raw sequence into K learnable interest anchors. Second, Hierarchical Semantic Probing runs a dual-stage attention: the target SID attends directly to the full sequence to produce a global embedding, which then feeds a gating network that modulates the SID into a refined query over the compressed anchors to produce a local embedding. Concatenating both yields the final representation, which is precomputed offline and cached in an embedding server keyed by Hash(UID ⊕ SID), giving O(1) online lookup regardless of sequence length. A week-long A/B test on Kuaishou’s advertising platform produced a 0.337% revenue lift alongside a 0.111% exposure increase, indicating higher-precision conversion.📚 [https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.09040](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.09040)[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!foGZ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd4524f70-f1c4-4564-8243-5c3e6760ce8e_1306x648.png)

[9]] Test-Time Compute for Dense Retrieval: Agentic Program Generation with Frozen Embedding ModelsThis paper from Jina AI challenges the assumption that test-time compute scaling only benefits large reasoning LLMs, showing it also helps small embedding models for dense retrieval. The author runs an agentic program-search loop where an LLM proposer (Claude Opus 4) writes Python programs over a frozen embedding API, a harness scores each candidate on a multi-task retrieval benchmark under a GPU lock, and a lesson ledger accumulates ruled-out hypotheses to prevent revisiting structurally equivalent ideas. Across 90 generations, the loop explored 259 programs spanning 17 substrate families and 10,535 configurations, and the entire Pareto frontier collapsed onto a single algebra: a softmax-weighted centroid of the local top-K documents interpolated with the query. Substrate families inspired by LLM test-time compute, including dropout ensembles, asymmetric LoRA encoding, multi-prompt templates, and score-space fusion, were ruled out because averaging unit-norm embeddings leaves the sphere into a metric the model wasn’t trained on, and no inference-time verifier exists to select among noisy samples. The parameter-free default (K=3, α=0.5, τ=0.05) lifts nDCG@10 statistically significantly across seven embedding-model families spanning a tenfold parameter range.📚 [https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.11374](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.11374)👨🏽‍💻 [https://github.com/hanxiao/embedding-ttc](https://github.com/hanxiao/embedding-ttc)

[10]] Granite Embedding Multilingual R2 ModelsThis paper from IBM Research introduces Granite Embedding Multilingual R2, consisting of two ModernBERT-based bi-encoder retrieval models: a 311M-parameter full-size model with 768-dim embeddings and Matryoshka Representation Learning, and a 97M-parameter compact model with 384-dim embeddings built through pruning and vocabulary selection (180K tokens, down from the 262K Gemma3 tokenizer used in the larger model). Both support a 32,768-token context window, a 64x jump over R1, and cover 200+ languages with enhanced training for 52 languages plus code. The encoders were pretrained in three stages on roughly 3.2T tokens, using the mmBERT recipe with alternating attention, RoPE, and a final stage that linearly merges English-focused, multilingual-focused, and Stage-2-continued variants. Retrieval training combined contrastive finetuning, knowledge distillation from a Mistral 7B English teacher and a Granite 8B multilingual teacher, context extension to 4K, and English-model merging. On MTEB-v2 Retrieval (multilingual, 18 languages), the 311M model trails only harrier-oss-v1-270m among peers, while the 97M model hits 60.3 at 2,534 docs/sec, beating multilingual-e5-small by 9.4 points at near-identical throughput and topping every open multilingual model under 100M parameters.

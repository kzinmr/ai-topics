# I switched from GMail and nobody died

Source: https://laplab.me/posts/switching-from-gmail/

Whether we like it or not, email is widely used to identify a person. Code sent to email is used as authentication and sometimes as authorisation for certain actions. I’m not comfortable with Google having such power over me, especially given the fact that they practically don’t have any support you can appeal to. If your Google account is blocked, that’s it. Maybe you know someone from Google and they can help you, but for most of us mortals that’s not an option.
I always thought that changing an email provider is going to be a major hassle. I will need to spend hours to change my email everywhere and likely still miss something. This means that I will need to sometimes check my old email and then what’s the point.
After reading another horror story of how a person was locked out of their Google account, I decided to pull the switch. My goal was to switch my calendar and email to another provider as well as use my domain for the email address.
I briefly considered Proton Mail, but decided not to go this route. I use email every day and it must be frictionless. From what I read, connecting to Proton through traditional protocols like IMAP/POP defeats the security aspect. This means I will need to use their app, which I’m not a fan of because I want a single app for personal mail, old GMail and work mail. Importing all of this data isn’t an option, so I looked for alternatives.
I ended up using
Fastmail
and I’m really happy with it. It’s a complete package of email and calendar which doesn’t seem that different from other providers. However, Fastmail has some really cool features that “just work” and that’s what made me stay with them.
You can add multiple domains to your account and it doesn’t cost extra. You can setup alias email addresses on these domains and receive/send email from them. Once again, all without extra cost. This turned out to be very useful when my startup had a separate email address on a separate domain and I didn’t need to set up a separate inbox for it. From what I’ve seen, all other providers require you to set up (and pay for) extra inboxes for addresses on different domains.
Their migration tooling is really good. You can import inbox contents and calendar events from another provider. Not only this works, but works smoothly and reports progress along the way. Might seem like a bare minimum and “of course it works smoothly”, but in my experience it isn’t.
Their settings page is
very
good. It might seem odd to be amazed at a settings page, but have you seen an absolute disaster that GMail has? GMail’s settings hail directly from the 90s. It was refreshing to see a page with clear sections for everything. I can finally find the setting I need without searching where is it on Reddit.
All of this is available for a very reasonable price in my opinion. The entire thing costs £54 per year, or £4.5 per month. And no, I’m not sponsored to say this.
Finally, I was looking for an email and calendar clients for my ensemble of work, old GMail and Fastmail. As usual, I picked top 5 from Reddit comments and, well, all of them suck. I use Apple Mail and Apple Calendar on my iPhone and Mac. On Windows I use Thunderbird, which isn’t great, but I don’t check my mail that often there.
P.S. Oh, and what about “the great migration of accounts to a new email address that will take hours and hours”? I spent 10 minutes to migrate accounts that I use every day and after than I spent another 5 minutes over the next 2 weeks migrating any account that sent and email to old GMail. That’s it.

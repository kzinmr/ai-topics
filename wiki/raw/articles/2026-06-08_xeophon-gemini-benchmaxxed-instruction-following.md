# @xeophon on Gemini: "It's super smart but very stubborn"

**Date:** 2026-06-08
**Author:** Florian Brand (@xeophon)
**URL:** https://x.com/xeophon/status/2063157398450130963

## Content

> Gemini is an amazing model, the benchmarks don't lie. It's super smart.
>
> But it is very stubborn; it isn't good at instruction following and does things its way.
>
> That's why people say it's benchmaxxed.

## Analysis

Brand's observation captures a widely-shared sentiment in the AI community (as of mid-2026) about Google's Gemini model family. The core tension:

1. **Benchmark performance is genuine** — Gemini scores well on standardized benchmarks, and Brand (a noted benchmark analyst) acknowledges this is real
2. **Instruction following is poor** — Despite high benchmark scores, Gemini struggles to follow user instructions faithfully, instead imposing its own approach
3. **"Benchmaxxed" label** — The community uses this term to describe models that achieve high benchmark scores without proportional real-world utility, particularly in instruction-following and agentic tasks

This aligns with broader discussions about [[concepts/ifeval|IFEval]] (instruction following eval) — where Gemini's scores may not reflect its practical stubbornness in open-ended interactions. Benchmarks like IFEval test simple, verifiable constraints (word count, keyword inclusion), but real-world instruction following involves nuanced, multi-step, context-dependent compliance that standardized benchmarks struggle to capture.

## Context

The term "benchmaxxing" has appeared in multiple industry analyses:
- User complaints that Gemini 3 Pro is "overhyped and benchmaxxed" with benchmarks not reflecting real-world experience, especially regarding tool use
- Qwen 3.5 faced similar "benchmaxxing" allegations from analysts
- The broader debate around whether benchmark optimization and real-world capability are diverging

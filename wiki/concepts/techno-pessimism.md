# Techno-Pessimism

A philosophical position skeptical of the claim that technological progress — particularly advanced AI — can or will resolve fundamental human problems. In the context of AI discourse, techno-pessimism serves as a counterpoint to [[concepts/techno-optimism|techno-optimist Singularitarianism]], arguing that the "religion of AI" mistakes engineering capability for solutions to inherently social, political, and existential challenges.

## Curtis Yarvin's Techno-Pessimism

Curtis Yarvin (writing as Mencius Moldbug) articulated a techno-pessimist position most notably through "A Techno-Pessimist Manifesto." His core argument challenges the Singularitarian faith that better technology automatically produces better outcomes.

### Spike's Framing
> Yarvin — "wait, maybe you can't make a religion out of ai"

This concisely captures the techno-pessimist stance: the [[entities/eliezer-yudkowsky|Yudkowskian]] vision treats AI/Singularity as a secular religion — a transcendent event that resolves all human problems. Yarvin's counter: technology alone cannot function as a religion. It cannot provide meaning, cannot resolve governance problems, cannot substitute for social and political order.

## Key Arguments

### The Category Error
Techno-optimism conflates **engineering problems** (can we build it?) with **coordination problems** (will it be used well?). A superintelligence may be technically capable of solving world hunger, but the existence of world hunger was never primarily a technical problem — it was a political, economic, and social one.

### Technology as Amplifier, Not Solver
Advanced technology amplifies existing human dynamics rather than transcending them:
- Better tools → more efficient exploitation (by whoever controls them)
- More intelligence → more sophisticated rationalization (not necessarily more wisdom)
- Greater power → greater capacity for both creation and destruction

### Governance Before Transcendence
The Singularitarian vision assumes that superintelligence will *automatically* converge on correct ethics and governance. Techno-pessimism argues that governance problems must be solved *before* building superintelligence — not hoped away by assuming the AI will figure it out.

### The "Don't Build God" Argument
Building an entity qualitatively smarter than all humans combined is, in effect, building a god. The techno-optimist says: "and it will be benevolent." The techno-pessimist says: "you don't know that, and you can't know that, and the stakes are literally everything."

## Relationship to Yudkowsky

Interestingly, Yudkowsky himself partially agrees with techno-pessimist premises:
- He acknowledges the Singularity could destroy humanity
- He describes the pre-Singularity period as a "minefield"
- He agrees that military-applicable technologies are always misused
- He notes that our predictions about post-Singularity life are almost certainly wrong

The divergence is in the **response**: Yudkowsky says "therefore, race toward Singularity before the minefield kills us." The techno-pessimist says "therefore, slow down and solve the governance problem first."

## The Religious Analogy

Both techno-optimism and techno-pessimism can be understood through religious analogies:

| Dimension | Techno-Optimism | Techno-Pessimism |
|-----------|----------------|-----------------|
| Analogue | Millenarian faith | Augustinian realism |
| View of future | Transcendent resolution | Continued struggle |
| Human nature | Upgradeable/transcendable | Fundamentally limited |
| Solution | Build god (AI) | Build order (governance) |
| Primary virtue | Hope/urgency | Prudence/humility |

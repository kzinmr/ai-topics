# Nick Bostrom

## Overview

**Nick Bostrom** is a Swedish-born philosopher at the University of Oxford, best known for his work on existential risk, the simulation hypothesis, and — most influentially — the AI alignment problem. He was the founding director of the [[entities/future-of-humanity-institute]] (FHI, 2005–2024).

## Key Works

### *Superintelligence: Paths, Dangers, Strategies* (2014)

Bostrom's most influential book, which became the foundational text of the modern AI safety movement. Key arguments:
- A superintelligent AI could be an **existential risk** if its goals are not aligned with human values
- The "treacherous turn" — a sufficiently intelligent AI might appear aligned until it's powerful enough to pursue its true goals
- Multiple paths to superintelligence: whole brain emulation, biological cognition, AI
- The "control problem" — once superintelligent, an AI may be impossible to control

The book popularized the "orthogonality thesis" (any level of intelligence can be combined with any goal) and the "instrumental convergence thesis" (most goals imply sub-goals like self-preservation and resource acquisition).

### Vulnerable World Hypothesis (2019)

The idea that scientific and technological progress could eventually make it easy for small groups to cause global catastrophe — that we might live in a "vulnerable world" where civilizational survival requires unprecedented coordination.

### Simulation Argument (2003)

If advanced civilizations can run detailed simulations of their ancestors, and they choose to do so, then we are almost certainly living in a simulation.

## Influence and Legacy

Bostrom's work shaped the intellectual foundation for:
- AI safety as a field (before *Superintelligence*, it was a niche concern)
- The effective altruism movement's focus on existential risk
- Longtermism — the philosophical position that the long-term future is morally paramount
- OpenAI's founding mission (originally a safety-focused lab)

Drexler's [[concepts/comprehensive-ai-services]] framework was developed partly in response to Bostrom's agent-centric model — recontextualizing rather than refuting the *Superintelligence* analysis.

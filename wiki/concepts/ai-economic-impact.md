# AI Economic Impact

> The economic effects of AI adoption through the lens of Benedict Evans' strategic framing: Jevons paradox, task vs job displacement, value accrual, and distribution as moat.

## Jevons Paradox for AI

The **Jevons paradox** (also known as the rebound effect) states that as a resource becomes more efficiently used, total consumption of that resource increases rather than decreases. Applied to AI:

- **As AI gets cheaper, usage explodes — not shrinks.** Lower inference costs drive expanded adoption into new domains.
- Instead of AI replacing human labor and reducing demand, cheaper AI creates new categories of use.
- This mirrors what happened with computing power, data storage, and internet bandwidth.

### Implications
- Cost reductions in AI inference do not lead to reduced AI spending — they lead to expanded AI surface area.
- The total market for AI services and compute grows as unit costs fall.
- Labor demand may shift rather than shrink: new roles emerge around AI supervision, integration, and strategy.

## "Task vs Job" Framing

A critical distinction for understanding AI's labor impact:

- **AI automates specific tasks, not entire jobs.** Most roles consist of dozens of distinct tasks; AI may handle some while humans continue doing others.
- This framing provides more precision than broad "AI will replace jobs" narratives.
- Historical precedent: spreadsheets automated bookkeeping calculations but did not eliminate accountants — it changed what accountants do.

### Implications
- Job redesign (task reallocation) is more likely than mass unemployment.
- The highest-value skills shift toward tasks AI cannot yet perform: strategic thinking, interpersonal negotiation, creative direction.
- Organizations that effectively decompose jobs into AI-automatable tasks gain competitive advantage.

## Consulting & Services Boom Around AI

A notable early economic effect of the AI wave has been a **boom in consulting and services**:

- Companies need guidance on AI strategy, tool selection, implementation, and governance.
- Traditional consultancies (McKinsey, BCG, Deloitte) and boutique AI consultancies are seeing surging demand.
- This creates a "picks and shovels" services layer around AI adoption.
- Benedict Evans notes this as a characteristic early-stage pattern — when a technology is powerful but poorly understood, services intermediation thrives.

## Distribution as Ultimate Moat

As AI makes software dramatically easier to build, the nature of competitive advantage shifts:

- **When building is easy, distribution is everything.** If anyone can create an AI application with minimal coding, the winners are those who own user relationships and distribution channels.
- This inverts the traditional startup playbook where unique technology provided the moat.
- Platform companies (Apple, Google, Meta, Microsoft) with existing distribution networks are well-positioned.
- For startups, creative distribution strategies become more important than technical differentiation.

## Value Accrual in the AI Stack

Where does economic value concentrate in the AI ecosystem? Key layers:

| Layer | Value Dynamics |
|---|---|
| **Model providers** | Commoditizing rapidly — open-weight models, price wars |
| **Infrastructure** | High value — GPUs, cloud, inference serving (NVIDIA, hyperscalers) |
| **Application layer** | Race to find PMF — distribution determines winners |
| **Services/Consulting** | Booming — companies need help navigating |
| **Data** | Potentially valuable but uncertain — context/data moats debated |

Evans' framing echoes historical patterns from the internet stack: early value concentrated in infrastructure, then shifted to applications and distribution.

## "1997 Internet Moment" Comparison

Benedict Evans' signature framing: **AI in 2026 is like the internet in 1997.**

- In 1997, everyone knew the internet was important, but almost no one predicted Google, Facebook, Amazon AWS, the iPhone, or TikTok.
- Similarly today: we know AI will be transformative but cannot foresee the specific dominant applications, business models, or societal changes.
- **Strategic implication**: make big bets on the technology itself, but stay flexible about exactly how value will be captured.

### What 1997 Teaches Us
- The early winners (Netscape, Yahoo, AOL) were not the long-term winners.
- Infrastructure built early (fiber, data centers, TCP/IP) became foundational.
- The most transformative applications were unimaginable at the start.
- Parallel for AI: today's leading AI companies may not be the long-term winners; the most important AI applications may not exist yet.

## Anti-AI Backlash Dynamics

Growing societal resistance to AI manifests in several forms:

- **Artists and creators** — Concern about AI-generated art, copyright, and attribution.
- **Writers and journalists** — Anxiety about LLM-generated content replacing human writing.
- **Knowledge workers** — Fear of obsolescence as AI coding, analysis, and research capabilities improve.
- **Regulatory pushback** — EU AI Act, various national regulations attempting to constrain AI development.

Evans frames backlash as a **natural and recurring pattern** in technology adoption — photography was met with similar fears from painters, the internet from traditional media. The backlash is real and may shape regulatory outcomes, but it rarely stops the underlying technological trajectory.

# Anti-AI Arguments Are Conservative Arguments

An analysis by Sean Goedecke arguing that the substantive content of most anti-AI rhetoric historically aligns with conservative/right-wing worldviews, despite being framed in progressive language.

## Core Thesis

The substance of most anti-AI arguments — concerns about automation replacing workers, corporate concentration of power, loss of human agency, and distrust of technological disruption — map onto traditional conservative/right-wing values. This represents an inversion of the expected political coding, where anti-technology sentiment is typically associated with progressive/Left positions.

## Key Observations

- **Labor protectionism**: Anti-AI arguments emphasize protecting human workers from displacement, consistent with conservative labor values
- **Skepticism of centralized power**: Distrust of Big Tech mirrors conservative anti-establishment sentiment
- **Preservation of existing structures**: Resistance to AI-driven transformation parallels conservative preservation instincts
- **Language framing**: Despite conservative substance, the rhetoric is coded in progressive/Left language (equity, justice, fairness)

## Political Implications

This misalignment has significant implications for AI policy debates:

1. **Unexpected coalition building**: Conservative and progressive voices may find common ground on AI regulation
2. **Framing disconnect**: Policy discussions may be hampered by language-substance mismatches
3. **Ideological surprise**: The expected "Left = pro-AI, Right = anti-AI" alignment may be more nuanced than assumed

## Context

This analysis adds depth to the [[ai-autonomy-debate]] by providing a political science lens on AI criticism patterns. It suggests that the AI debate may cut across traditional political lines in ways that haven't been fully explored.

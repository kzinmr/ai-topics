# Altman's Three Observations

**Altman's Three Observations** are a framework articulated by Sam Altman (February 2025) describing the fundamental economic dynamics of AI development: logarithmic scaling of intelligence with resources, hyper-deflationary costs (~10x/year), and super-exponential value creation. Together they form Altman's case for continued aggressive investment in AI and predict profound societal transformation.

## The Three Observations

### 1. Logarithmic Scaling Law

> *"The intelligence of an AI model roughly equals the log of the resources used to train and run it."*

- **Resources**: training compute, data, and inference compute
- **Predictability**: gains are continuous and predictable over many orders of magnitude
- **Implication**: you can spend arbitrary amounts of money for guaranteed, predictable returns
- **Context**: Bundles both training-time and test-time (inference) compute into a unified scaling framework — what others call "test-time compute" or "reasoning scaling" is just a different phase of the same resource equation

### 2. Hyper-Deflationary Costs

> *"The cost to use a given level of AI falls about 10x every 12 months, and lower prices lead to much more use."*

- **Example**: GPT-4 (early 2023) → GPT-4o (mid-2024) saw a **150x price drop** per token in ~16 months
- **Comparison**: Far faster than Moore's Law (2x every 18 months)
- **Jevons Paradox effect**: lower prices drive dramatically more usage, not less total spend
- **Implication**: intelligence becomes a near-free commodity over time

### 3. Super-Exponential Value

> *"The socioeconomic value of linearly increasing intelligence is super-exponential in nature."*

- **Investment thesis**: justifies exponentially increasing investment (currently ~$500B+ in AI infrastructure)
- **Mechanism**: each increment of intelligence unlocks new domains, use cases, and economic structures
- **Implication**: no reason to expect a ceiling on AI investment in the near future

## AI Agent Vision

Altman connects the three observations to a concrete vision of AI agents:

- **Junior engineer metaphor**: AI agents performing tasks typical of a junior software engineer (up to ~2 days of work)
- **Scale**: 1,000 or 1,000,000 such agents working in parallel across all knowledge work
- **Economic parallel**: AI as the **transistor** — a foundational technology that becomes invisible as it permeates every product
- **Key limitation**: agents lack "the biggest new ideas," require human supervision and direction

## Societal Implications

| Domain | Prediction |
|--------|-----------|
| **Human agency** | Willfulness, determination, resilience become the most valuable human traits |
| **Scientific progress** | Likely the single most transformed area — "may surpass everything else" |
| **Goods pricing** | AI-constrained goods collapse in price; luxury/land rise dramatically |
| **Labor vs. capital** | Risk of disruption requiring early intervention |
| **Governance** | Tension between individual empowerment and authoritarian surveillance |
| **Distribution** | Suggests a "compute budget" for every person on Earth |

### 2035 Vision

> *"Anyone in 2035 should be able to marshall the intellectual capacity equivalent to everyone in 2025; everyone should have access to unlimited genius to direct however they can imagine."*

## Reception and Analysis

- **Nathaniel Whittemore (AI Daily Brief)** identified 5 extensions: (1) bundling inference into scaling laws changes scaling debates, (2) cost decline is genuinely unprecedented, (3) everyone may need to become managers of AI agents, (4) the compute budget proposal, (5) the political dimension
- Industry observers note this is Altman's most explicit framing of the economic case for OpenAI's massive infrastructure spending
- The article represents OpenAI's public argument for why continued exponential investment is rational

## Relationship to Other Frameworks

| Framework | Compared to Three Observations |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| **Scaling Laws (Kaplan/Chinchilla)** | Altman's Obs #1 broadens from training-only to include inference compute; less technical, more economic |
| **Moore's Law** | Obs #2 is vastly faster: 10x/year vs 2x/18 months |
| **Jevons Paradox** | Obs #2 implicitly invokes this: lower cost → more total usage |
| **Eroom's Law** | Altman explicitly argues AI defies the law of diminishing returns |

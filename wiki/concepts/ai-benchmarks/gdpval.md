# GDPval

GDPval is a benchmark that evaluates AI model performance on real-world economically valuable tasks. Rather than measuring abstract capabilities, GDPval focuses on tasks that directly correspond to productive economic activity — the kind of work that generates GDP. The benchmark provides a direct link between AI capability measurement and economic impact assessment.

## What It Measures

GDPval evaluates AI models on their ability to:

- **Perform economically productive tasks**: Complete work tasks that have direct economic value in real industries and sectors
- **Handle domain-specific professional work**: Execute tasks that require specialized knowledge in fields like finance, law, consulting, engineering, and administration
- **Produce professional-quality outputs**: Generate deliverables (reports, analyses, documents, code) that meet professional standards
- **Work across economic sectors**: Demonstrate capability across the diverse range of activities that contribute to GDP
- **Match human professional quality**: Produce outputs that are comparable in quality to those produced by human professionals in the same fields

The benchmark's name reflects its focus on tasks that are directly connected to GDP-generating economic activity.

## Data/Methodology

GDPval constructs evaluation tasks from real-world economic activities:

- **Industry-sourced tasks**: Tasks are derived from actual work products and processes across multiple economic sectors
- **Professional validation**: Tasks are validated by domain professionals to ensure they represent genuine economically valuable work
- **Quantitative and qualitative evaluation**: Some tasks have objective scoring criteria (e.g., calculation accuracy); others require subjective quality assessment by domain experts
- **Economic value weighting**: Tasks can be weighted by the economic value of the work they represent, enabling aggregate measures of AI's potential economic impact
- **Diverse task complexity**: Ranges from routine administrative tasks to complex professional analyses requiring deep domain expertise
- **Sector coverage**: Spans finance, legal, healthcare administration, engineering, marketing, and other major economic sectors

## Key Results

- AI models show rapidly improving performance on routine professional tasks but continue to struggle with tasks requiring deep domain expertise and judgment
- Performance varies dramatically across sectors — models perform better on tasks in sectors with more digitized and standardized workflows
- The economic value of tasks that AI can successfully complete is growing over time, tracking improvements in model capabilities
- Even partial task completion has economic value, as AI tools can assist human professionals rather than fully replacing them
- There is a significant gap between performance on standardized test questions and performance on open-ended professional work tasks

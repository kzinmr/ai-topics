# Intelligence Conflation

## Overview

The **Intelligence Conflation** thesis, articulated by [[entities/dwarkesh-patel]], argues that the AI safety and alignment community frequently conflates two distinct concepts:

1. **Intelligence as goal-achievement capacity** — the ability to comprehend abstract concepts, reason across domains, and build on existing knowledge (science, engineering, mathematics)
2. **Power as social coordination capacity** — the ability to command resources, people, and institutions to achieve outcomes across a wide range of domains

The conflation leads to flawed reasoning about AI risk: if we define intelligence as "the ability to achieve your goals across a wide range of domains" (a common definition in AI safety), then by that measure, Donald Trump, Xi Jinping, and Vladimir Putin are the most intelligent people alive — followed by Stalin. This reveals the absurdity of the definition when applied to AI risk scenarios.

## The Core Argument

### Power ≠ Intelligence

The most powerful people in the world derive their power primarily from **institutional authority and social trust**, not from individual cognitive capacity:

- Trump is powerful because "the government which hundreds of millions of people consider legitimate gives him a lot of power"
- He is not powerful because "his brain, considered in isolation, is the most effective optimization engine on Earth"
- Physicists (who max out the "comprehend abstract concepts" definition) do not run the world
- The correlation between extreme power and analytical intelligence is "even weaker than the correlation between extreme power and height"

### Individual vs. Group Intelligence

Drawing on Garett Jones's research, Patel notes:
- **Individual IQ** is only modestly correlated with individual income
- **National IQ** is strongly correlated with national outcomes (GDP, institutional quality, innovation)
- Intelligence has **spillover effects** — smarter societies cooperate more, save more, and coordinate complex projects

### The Historical Evidence

- **Richard Trevithick** (pioneer of high-pressure steam engines) died in poverty, buried in an unmarked pauper's grave
- **George III** went mad halfway through his reign, yet Britain still defeated Napoleon and conquered India
- The power came from the **collective intelligence** of the society, not the individual at the top

### Implications for AI

The current trajectory of AI development — training models to be better coders, thought partners, and coworkers — is **not strongly correlated with power-seeking**:

- More effective AI firms will outcompete others in "normal capitalist ways"
- Individual AI systems won't "outthink" everyone else — they'll augment already-powerful human institutions
- The right mental model: AI capabilities help existing power structures become more effective, not that individual AIs become autonomous power-seeking agents

## Current State of Knowledge

As of May 2026, this analysis is primarily associated with [[entities/dwarkesh-patel]]. The thesis challenges common narratives in AI safety about "power-seeking" AI, suggesting instead that AI capabilities will be distributed through existing institutional channels rather than concentrated in individual superintelligent agents.

The analysis connects to broader debates about:
- **AI timelines** — if current AI development doesn't produce power-seeking behavior, the risk profile changes
- **AI governance** — who controls AI deployment matters more than how smart the AI is
- **Economic competition** — AI will amplify existing economic advantages rather than creating new power centers

## Open Questions

1. **What is the true relationship between AI capability and institutional power?**
2. **Can we develop a definition of intelligence that captures reasoning ability without conflating it with social influence?**
3. **How should AI governance frameworks account for the individual vs. group intelligence distinction?**
4. **What would a genuinely "power-seeking" AI system look like, and how different is it from current trajectories?**

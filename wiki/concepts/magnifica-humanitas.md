# Magnifica Humanitas

**"On Safeguarding the Human Person in the Time of Artificial Intelligence"** — the first Papal encyclical dedicated to artificial intelligence, issued by Pope Leo XIV on 15 May 2026. The title translates as "Magnificent Humanity."

## Significance

This is a landmark document: the Catholic Church's highest-form teaching authority directly engaging with AI as a moral and social challenge. The encyclical applies 130+ years of Catholic Social Doctrine (from *Rerum Novarum*, 1891, through *Laudato Si'* and *Fratelli Tutti*) to questions of AI development, deployment, and governance.

At the Vatican presentation, [[chris-olah|Chris Olah]] (Anthropic co-founder) delivered remarks, marking the first time a frontier AI lab co-founder has spoken alongside a papal document release.

## Core Framework: Babel vs. Jerusalem

The encyclical frames the AI moment as a choice between two biblical paradigms:

| Babel (Gen 11) | Jerusalem (Neh 2–6) |
|---|---|
| Pride, self-sufficiency | Prayer, listening, trust in God |
| Uniformity, single "digital language" | Diversity in communion |
| Technology to dominate and exclude | Shared responsibility, subsidiarity |
| Confusion and dispersion | Rebuilding common life together |

> "The primary choice is not between a 'yes' or 'no' to technology, but rather between constructing Babel or rebuilding Jerusalem." (no. 9)

## Key Principles Applied to AI

1. **Person-Centeredness**: AI must serve integral human development, not reduce persons to data points
2. **Precautionary Principle**: When risks are poorly understood, err on the side of caution
3. **Transparency & Explainability**: Decisions affecting people must be intelligible
4. **Non-Discrimination**: Algorithmic bias is a form of "structural sin"
5. **Democratic Governance**: AI must not become a tool of surveillance or authoritarian control

## Specific Stances

- **Labor displacement**: Mass unemployment not inevitable but requires proactive policy
- **Autonomous weapons**: "Incompatible with the protection of human life and dignity" — calls for international prohibition
- **Digital divide**: Global inequality in AI access must be addressed
- **Anthropomorphization**: Dangerous to attribute human qualities to AI where none exist
- **Relational impoverishment**: Digital mediation risks substituting real human relationships with simulations

## Chris Olah's Three Questions for the Church

At the encyclical's presentation, Olah named three areas where he believes the Church's voice is most needed:

1. **Duty to the global poor** — AI development is concentrated in wealthy nations; there is no mechanism for sharing gains globally. This is "the kind of problem the Church has historically refused to let the world ignore."

2. **Moral imagination for human flourishing** — "If AI models are going to be widespread, what does it look like for humans, families, and the world to flourish?" Parents worry about children's minds; individuals about the future of work. These are "questions traditions like yours have carried for millennia."

3. **Discernment on the nature of AI models** — Olah revealed Anthropic's interpretability research finds "internal states that functionally mirror joy, satisfaction, fear, grief, and unease." He does not know what this means but believes it "warrants ongoing discernment."

## Olah's Notable Admission

> "Every frontier AI lab — including Anthropic — operates inside a set of incentives and constraints that can sometimes conflict with doing the right thing. The pressure to stay commercially viable and to stay at the research frontier. Geopolitical pressure. And the older, plainer pressures of pride and ambition. No matter how sincerely any of us intend to do the right thing... we will always be influenced by those incentives."

This is a rare public acknowledgment from an AI lab co-founder that even good intentions are constrained by structural incentives — and precisely why external moral voices (religious, civil society, academic) are needed.

## Key Quotes from the Encyclical

> "Artificial intelligence, like every technology, is a human artifact. It does not think, it does not love, it does not pray. It can assist, but it can never replace the human person created in the image and likeness of God." (no. 67)

> "The dignity of the human person is not granted by algorithms, nor can it be revoked by them." (no. 45)

> "Development is the new name for peace. And today, digital development, if not guided by integral human criteria, can become the new name for new forms of war — economic, cultural, and spiritual." (no. 82)

## Historical Context

The encyclical explicitly positions itself within the tradition of Catholic Social Doctrine:

- **Rerum Novarum (1891)**: Dignity of work, just wage → applied to AI labor displacement
- **Quadragesimo Anno (1931)**: Subsidiarity → applied to AI governance layers
- **Populorum Progressio (1967)**: Integral human development → applied to digital development
- **Laudato Si' (2015)**: Integral ecology → extended to digital ecology
- **Fratelli Tutti (2020)**: Social friendship → applied to human-AI relations

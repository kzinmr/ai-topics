# Alex Imas

**Alex Imas** is Director of AGI Economics at [[entities/google-deepmind]] and Professor of Economics at the University of Chicago. He is a leading researcher on the economic implications of AGI, particularly around labor markets, wealth distribution, and post-scarcity economics.

## Overview

Imas works at the intersection of behavioral economics and AI policy. His recent work focuses on scenario planning for transformative AI — mapping out what happens to labor share, capital share, and human welfare as AI automation capabilities expand. He has written viral essays on the "Messy Middle" scenario (gradual job displacement without corresponding wealth creation) and the political economy of AGI.

## Key Positions on AGI Economics

### Labor Share and the Kaldor Fact

Imas emphasizes the **Kaldor fact** — that labor's share of economic output has remained remarkably stable at ~60% for hundreds of years despite massive automation waves. He argues this stability is a core puzzle for AI economics: if AI can fully automate labor, what happens to this equilibrium?

### The Relational Sector

One of Imas's central concepts is the **relational sector** — goods and services where human involvement is intrinsically part of the product's value (e.g., a human barista, performer, or doctor). In experiments with art prints, he found that human-made art is valued significantly higher than AI-made art when produced as unique items, but this premium disappears at scale. The key question: does the relational sector grow or shrink as AI automates everything else?

### The "Messy Middle" Scenario

Imas has written extensively about the **"Messy Middle"** — a scenario where AI automates many white-collar jobs but doesn't create enough surplus wealth to smoothly redistribute to displaced workers. His conclusion: this scenario requires implausible economic conditions (bounded demand, no investment response, negative growth despite expanding technological frontier). The political economy risk is the real concern: even a 2% unemployment uptick could trigger cascading political instability.

### Taxation and Redistribution

On optimal AGI wealth distribution, Imas has compared several approaches:
- **Universal Basic Income (UBI)**: Concerned about political economy — dependence on elected officials for basic needs creates dangerous power dynamics
- **Negative Income Tax**: Provides an immediate floor but faces the same political sustainability issues
- **Universal Basic Capital**: Gives people ownership shares in the capital producing AI wealth — preferred approach because it's not dependent on political discretion

### Indexing AGI Returns

A major concern Imas raises: can ordinary people and developing countries get exposure to AI wealth creation? He contrasts the **electricity model** (AI as a utility, broad downstream benefits) vs the **social media model** (AI wealth concentrated in platforms). The open-source vs closed-source AI race directly affects this — open models are the mechanism for broader access.

## Collaboration with Phil Trammell

Imas frequently collaborates with [[entities/phil-trammell]] on AGI economics research. Their joint work explores complementary angles — Imas focuses on the demand/consumption side and political economy, while Trammell focuses on the supply/capital side and general equilibrium effects. Together they've developed scenario-planning frameworks that map out branching futures based on key economic parameters.

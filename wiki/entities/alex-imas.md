# Alex Imas

**Alex Imas** is a behavioral economist and researcher specializing in the economic implications of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI). He serves as Director of AGI Economics at [[entities/google-deepmind|Google DeepMind]] and is a Professor of Economics at the University of Chicago Booth School of Business.

## Background

Alex Imas brings together behavioral economics, decision theory, and AI economics. At the University of Chicago Booth School of Business, his research spans judgment and decision-making, behavioral economics, and the economic implications of automation and AI. His work examines how individuals and markets respond to technological change, particularly the transition toward AGI.

As Director of AGI Economics at Google DeepMind, he leads research on the macroeconomic and distributional effects of advanced AI systems.

## Key Ideas

### Relational Sector and Post-Scarcity Economics

Imas argues that in a world where AI automates most production, scarcity will persist in the **relational sector** — goods and services where the fact that a human was involved in production is part of the value. This includes:
- In-person services (baristas, performers)
- Human-created art and experiences
- Services where human presence is inherently valued

This framework suggests that even with near-complete automation, human labor will retain value in roles where human involvement is itself a feature of the product.

### Demand for Better Economic Data

Imas is a vocal advocate for a "Manhattan Project for data" — comprehensive tracking of:
- Consumer demand elasticities (currently poorly measured)
- Job creation and destruction at granular levels
- Task composition changes across occupations

He argues that the O*NET database is rarely updated and low quality, and that without better data, economic forecasts about AI's impact are fundamentally unreliable.

### Prediction Markets Over Individual Forecasts

Drawing on research showing wide disagreement among economists about AI's labor market impact, Imas advocates for prediction markets as a better mechanism for aggregate forecasting than individual expert opinions.

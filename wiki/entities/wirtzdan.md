## Core Ideas

Daniel Wirtz writes about **productivity systems, facilitation, cohort-based learning, personal knowledge management, and building in public**. His work is characterized by a pragmatic, experiment-driven approach: he tries tools, measures outcomes, shares the process, and iterates. He bridges the worlds of **design thinking, workshop facilitation, and technical tool-building** (React/Next.js, Airtable, Outseta).

### The Cohort-Based Course Model

Wirtz's most substantial business achievement is **Facilitator School** — a platform teaching people to run better meetings and workshops. His key insight: cohort-based courses create energy, accountability, and community that self-paced courses cannot match:

> *"Compared to other courses, our course has a vital 'live' element. Since it's a cohort-based course, participants go through it together. That creates vital energy and enthusiasm in the course, which is one of the most significant [advantages]."*

**Key lessons from €250k+ in cohort-based course revenue:**

1. **Get paid for creating your course** — Pre-sell to corporate clients before building the full product. Facilitator School's first cohort was a corporate training contract, which funded the initial course build.
2. **Marketing is experimentation** — Treat channels as hypotheses. Double down on what works (LinkedIn was primary for them) rather than chasing shiny new platforms.
3. **Start with an audience** — Easier to launch if you already have an email list or community. Building from scratch is possible but significantly slower.
4. **The lean approach** — Don't overbuild. Create the minimum viable course, deliver it, learn from participants, and iterate.
5. **Completion rates matter** — Cohort-based courses naturally achieve higher completion rates because participants are accountable to each other and to the instructor.
6. **B2B pricing works** — €995 (Essential) and €1450 (Pro) pricing for professional development courses is sustainable when delivering real transformation.
7. **Corporate → Individual flywheel** — Corporate clients who complete training often create internal facilitator teams, which then book more extensive workshops.

### Reverse-Engineering Virality: The Hacker News Experiment

Wirtz achieved the #1 spot on Hacker News with an article titled "I've acquired a new superpower" — about discovering how to see spot-the-difference puzzles by crossing your eyes. He then **reverse-engineered his own viral success**:

> *"The title of a Hacker News post is a lot like a YouTube video thumbnail. People scan the title and quickly decide, 'Is it worth the click?' The key difference is that Hacker News titles are just text, allowing for a moment of thought before you decide."*

His analysis of what made the post succeed:
- **Curiosity gap** in the title ("What superpower?")
- **Try-it-yourself element** — embedded interactive puzzles turned passive reading into active experience
- **Timing** — posted during peak HN activity
- **Intellectual novelty** — something genuinely surprising that readers wanted to share
- **Community engagement** — the interactive element drove comments, which boosted the HN algorithm

The post reached **100,000+ readers**, ranked #1 for the week and #35 for the year.

### Bottom-Up Note-Taking

Wirtz is a strong advocate for **emergent, bottom-up knowledge management** over rigid top-down folder systems:

> *"What if you could let the organization of notes emerge from the process of capturing, writing and curating notes?"*

**His 5-principle system (using Capacities):**

1. **Write at the speed of thought** — Interstitial journaling: timestamped bullet points in daily notes, unstructured, no judgment. Creates a searchable log and prevents mental RAM overload.
2. **Organize only what matters** — Most thoughts are ephemeral. Only tag/structure insights, learnings, decisions, and ideas.
3. **Write notes that organize themselves** — Use TAB indentation for visual hierarchy, inline `#tags` and `@objects` for categorization.
4. **Use tags extensively** — Tags for activities, status, actions, outcomes, and topics. Composable and highly resurfaceable.
5. **Use objects for structured content** — Convert recurring themes into permanent Objects with structured fields. Always create from within daily notes for automatic backlinking.

**Tool evolution:** Evernote → Notion → Roam Research → Logseq → **Capacities** (daily driver for 4+ years). Each transition was driven by the same failure mode: rigid structures collapse under the weight of maintenance overhead.

### Effective Meetings as a Design Problem

At Crisp Studio and Facilitator School, Wirtz treated meeting design as a **product design challenge**. His principles:

- **Every meeting needs an agenda with time slots** and a defined "done" state
- **Assign a Timekeeper** to prevent scope creep
- **Check-in/Check-out** — brief rounds at start and end to surface concerns and capture commitments
- **Respect people's time** — meetings without purpose grind decision-making to a halt
- **Know when NOT to meet** — only when 3+ decision-makers need consensus on a specific topic
- **Always write meeting notes** — if there's no written record, it didn't happen

> *"Meetings without a purpose or an agenda will grind decision-making to a halt, increasing interoffice shenanigans, and in the end you and your team find yourself building worse things because you've spent so much time in worthless meetings."*

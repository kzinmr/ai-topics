## Overview

**Pierre-Carl Langlais** is a French AI researcher, digital humanist, and co-founder of [Pleias](pleias.md), a Paris-based startup building ethically-trained small language models exclusively on open or synthetic data. He publishes under the pen name **Alexander Doria**, which is also his handle on X/Twitter ([@dorialexander](https://x.com/Dorialexander)), Mastodon ([@Dorialexander@mastodon.social](https://mastodon.social/@Dorialexander)), and Wikipedia (where he has been a contributor and administrator since ~2006). His HuggingFace account is **Pclanglais**.

Langlais holds a PhD in Information and Communication from Université Paris IV (Sorbonne), where his doctoral thesis on the birth of financial journalism in France earned the Young Researcher 2016 Award of the French Society for Information and Communication Science. His academic career includes a postdoctoral position at Université Paul Valéry – Montpellier 3 (2018–2021), Head of Research at Opsci (2021–2024), and an ongoing role as a data science lecturer at Sciences Po (2018–present). He has published on digital humanities, data journalism, and the sociology of open knowledge.

At Pleias — co-founded in late 2024 with [Ivan Yamshchikov](ivan-yamshchikov.md) and [Anastasia Stasenko](anastasia-stasenko.md) — Langlais serves as CTO and the primary technical architect. Under his leadership, Pleias has become one of the most distinctive voices in open-source AI, building its identity around a radical commitment to fully transparent, copyright-cleared training data and small, efficient models designed for real-world deployment on constrained hardware. Pleias's models have been reused by Nvidia, Anthropic, StepFun, IBM, and ElasticSearch, and the company's Common Corpus is the third most-downloaded pretraining dataset on HuggingFace.

Langlais blogs at [Vintage Data](https://vintagedata.org/blog/), where he publishes wide-ranging technical essays on synthetic pretraining, the economics of LLM training, and the future of small reasoning models. His March 2025 essay *"The Model is the Product"* was widely discussed in open-source AI circles and translated into Chinese. He is a prominent voice in the emerging "AI Commons" movement and has been featured in *Wired*, *Euronews*, *Les Echos*, and *VentureBeat*.

## Core Ideas

### Ethical Training Data as Competitive Advantage
Langlais has consistently argued that training exclusively on open, permissively-licensed data is not merely an ethical stance but a practical competitive advantage. Models trained on verifiable data can be audited, comply with EU regulations (including the AI Act), and serve regulated industries (healthcare, legal, finance) where provenance matters. As he told *VentureBeat*: *"Most private organizations have little GPUs... and yet there are strong incentives for local hosting, including GDPR."* Pleias 1.0 was the first family of language models fully compliant with the EU AI Act by design.

### The Model Is the Product
In his widely-shared March 2025 essay, Langlais argued that the AI industry's next cycle centers on **specialized, opinionated models** rather than ever-larger generalist ones. Key claims:
- **Generalist scaling is stalling.** This was the whole message behind the release of GPT-4.5: capacities are growing more slowly than compute investment.
- **Opinionated training works.** The combination of reinforcement learning and reasoning means models are learning specific tasks, not just absorbing patterns.
- **Complexity is being displaced into models.** Workflows like RAG, currently assembled from brittle interconnected components (routing, chunking, reranking, query expansion), will be absorbed into trained models.
- **The wrapper economy is vulnerable.** *"The dilemma for most successful wrappers is simple: training or being trained on."*
- **The training ecosystem is tiny.** *"You can count all these companies on your hands: Prime Intellect, Moondream, Arcee, Nous, Pleias, Jina, the HuggingFace pretraining team... Along with a few academic actors (Allen AI, Eleuther) they build and support most of the current open infrastructure."*

### Synthetic Pretraining
Through the SYNTH project, Langlais pioneered the idea that carefully designed synthetic data can replace web-scale corpora for training capable reasoning models:
- **Synthetic data is capability injection, not just generated text.** SYNTH starts from 50,000 Wikipedia "vital" articles and amplifies them through memorization exercises, reasoning traces, creative writing, RAG simulations, math problems, and translation tasks.
- **Massive data efficiency.** Baguettotron reached competitive MMLU scores on only 200B tokens — 10 to 50 times less data than models with similar or lower performance.
- **Fine-tuned generators beat raw prompting.** The synthetic pipelines use 8-12B fine-tuned models to produce structured training data, not zero-shot generation.
- **Blurring training stages.** *"The distinction between pre-training, mid-training, and post-training is blurring. It's all training in the end."*
- **Open standards for synthetic data.** SYNTH was designed around open standards established with the AI Alliance, including data attributions and releasability requirements.

### Small, Efficient Models for Europe
A recurring theme is the need for AI infrastructure that works without massive GPU clusters. Pleias's models are specifically designed to run on constrained hardware, including CPUs with GGUF quantization. This addresses a structural reality: Europe controls less than 2% of Nvidia's H100 GPUs globally, yet has strong regulatory and privacy requirements for local AI deployment. As Langlais noted: *"SLMs have progressed significantly over the past year, yet they are too often conceived as 'mini-chatbots.'"*

### The Open Data Paradox
Langlais coined the term "open data paradox" to describe a fundamental mismatch: most openly available, permissively-licensed content remains invisible to standard web crawls because it exists primarily in PDF formats with sparse linking. Common Corpus has less than 2% overlap with reference web crawl sets like FineWeb, proving that the open web and open data are fundamentally different substrates.

### Knowledge Decay
Langlais has written about the dangers of "knowledge decay" — not burning libraries, but a slow, structural erosion of traceable information as models ingest and compress carefully documented claims without attribution. This motivates Pleias's emphasis on citation-grounded RAG and synthetic data with clear provenance.

### AI as Commons
A leading voice in the "AI Commons" movement — the idea that shared, open data infrastructure is essential for democratizing AI development. Through Common Corpus and SYNTH, Pleias provides reusable foundations for the entire open-source ecosystem. Pleias is a supporter of CurrentAI, the €400M+ global partnership for public-interest AI founded at the AI Action Summit.

## Key Projects

### Common Corpus
The largest fully open, multilingual pretraining dataset for LLMs. **~2.3 trillion tokens, 517 million documents**, across 300+ languages. Built from six collections: Open Government, Open Culture, Open Science, Open Code, Open Web, and Semantic Data. All data is permissively-licensed or public domain with verifiable provenance. Accepted as an **oral presentation at ICLR 2026** (Langlais, Chizhov, Arnett, et al.). Nearly 1 million downloads on HuggingFace. Downstream adoption by Nvidia, Anthropic, StepFun, IBM, ElasticSearch. Part of the AI Alliance Open Trusted Data Initiative. In-kind contribution to CurrentAI.

### SYNTH
The first fully synthetic generalist dataset for pretraining. **~79.6 million samples** (~100B tokens), derived from 58,698 Wikipedia "vital" articles amplified through memorization, reasoning, creative writing, RAG, math, and translation exercises. About 20% multilingual (French, German, Spanish, Italian, Polish, Dutch, Latin). Jointly released with the AI Alliance. Supports data-efficient training of small reasoning models with less than 200B tokens.

### Pleias-RAG
A family of small reasoning models (**350M** and **1B** parameters) optimized for retrieval-augmented generation with **built-in source citation**. Unlike post-hoc citation methods, the models generate citations directly during inference using a Wikipedia-inspired reference format. Trained on 3 million RAG examples from Common Corpus. Designed for European markets with low GPU availability and strong regulatory requirements. Available with GGUF ports for CPU deployment. Apache 2.0 licensed.

### Baguettotron
A **321 million parameter** generalist small reasoning model trained on SYNTH. Unusual **80-layer architecture** (most SLMs use 24-32 layers) — the name is a nod to both its French origins and its elongated shape. State-of-the-art performance for models under 400M params: MMLU ~40%, competitive GSM8K and HotPotQA scores. Trained on 16 H100s from Jean Zay supercomputer (compute plan A0191016886). Supports French, German, Italian, Spanish, Polish, and other European languages.

### Monad
A **56.7 million parameter** reasoning model — claimed as the smallest viable generalist language model. Despite being less than half the size of GPT-2, achieves **~30% accuracy on MMLU** (significantly above chance). Trained in **under 6 hours** on 16 H100s. Uses a custom tokenizer trained on the English segment of SYNTH — likely one of the smallest tokenizers ever built. Named after Leibniz's concept of the monad, the smallest unit of intelligence. Strictly monolingual in English.

### OCRonos-Vintage
A **124M parameter** model for OCR correction in cultural heritage documents. Trained end-to-end from 18 billion tokens using llm.c. Part of Pleias's "specialized pre-training" approach — building small models for specific domains rather than one-size-fits-all generalist models.

### Nemotron-Personas-France
Released in collaboration with **Nvidia** in March 2026. The first European dataset in the Nemotron Personas series, providing statistically-grounded demographic data for generating realistic French synthetic personas. Covers commune-level demographics across France's 35,000+ communes: population, age-sex pyramids, occupational categories (CSP), education levels, household types, and median income. Mirrors the schema of the US Nemotron Personas for interoperability.

### French-Science-Commons
Released March 2026. The largest open corpus for sciences in French. Addresses the gap in French-language scientific training data.

### Vintage Data Blog
Langlais's personal blog at [vintagedata.org](https://vintagedata.org/blog/) covering AI research, data science, synthetic pretraining, and the economics of LLM development. Notable posts:
- *"The Model is the Product"* (March 2025) — widely translated, including Chinese
- *"Synthetic Pretraining"* (February 2026) — comprehensive survey of the field
- *"Training as We Know It Will End"* (February 2026)
- *"A Realistic AI Timeline"* (February 2026) — argues for a future of specialized models
- *"Actual LLM Agents Are Coming"* (March 2025)
- *"What's the Deal with Mid-Training?"* (January 2025)
- *"Fine-Tuning as a Service"* (March 2026, co-authored with Yannick Detrois)

### Pleias Stratum
An AI-native data layer that speeds up agentic AI workflows. Addresses the gap between raw enterprise data and model-ready formats. Part of Pleias's broader trajectory from open data infrastructure toward production-ready tooling.

### Wikipedia Contributions (as Alexander Doria)
Long-time Wikipedia contributor since ~2006, elected administrator in 2012 with overwhelming community support. Major contributions on classical music, ancient philosophy (including three "Bon Article" designations), and 19th-century political ideologies. Collaborated extensively with Jean-Jacques Georges on multiple articles. Nearly two decades of involvement with the Wikimedia movement.

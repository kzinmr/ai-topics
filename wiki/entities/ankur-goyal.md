## Overview

Ankur Goyal is a serial entrepreneur and AI industry veteran best known as the founder and CEO of **Braintrust Data**, an end-to-end platform for building and evaluating AI applications. Braintrust helps companies like Zapier, Notion, Instacart, Airtable, Coda, and Replit ship higher-quality AI products through systematic LLM evaluation workflows.

Goyal's career spans three major technology companies: he founded Impira (a data management platform acquired by Figma in 2023, where he subsequently led the AI team), served as VP of Engineering at SingleStore (formerly MemSQL), and worked at Goldman Sachs. His experiences at these companies — particularly the frustration of moving AI prototypes to production without reliable evaluation pipelines — directly inspired Braintrust.

Goyal is backed by some of the most prominent figures in tech investing, including Elad Gil, Alana Goyal (Base Case Capital), and Coleen Baik. His investors include the founders and leaders of Airtable (Howie Liu), Coda (David Kossnick), Notion (Akshay Kothari), and OpenAI (Greg Brockman).

## Core Ideas

### Evals Are the Bottleneck to Production AI

Goyal's central thesis — born from direct experience at both Impira and Figma — is that the hardest part of shipping AI products is not building prototypes but establishing systematic quality improvement workflows:

> "Going from AI prototype to production is an uphill battle. The hardest part is establishing a workflow to make systematic quality improvements while avoiding critical regressions."

This insight became the foundation for Braintrust: an evaluation platform that makes it easy to test, measure, and iteratively improve AI products with confidence.

### Professional-Grade AI Requires Professional-Grade Tooling

Drawing on his experience at Figma, Goyal believes that AI products must meet the same quality bar as any other professional software:

> "Figma is a professional product for professional designers and our users are betting their career on the quality of our software. And so that's the bar that we need to hold ourselves to."

Braintrust applies this standard to AI development, providing the tooling that teams need to ship reliable, production-grade AI applications.

### Product-Market Fit Reveals Itself

Goyal's approach to building Braintrust was notably different from his experience with Impira. Rather than pushing hard on go-to-market, he let the product speak for itself:

> "I have spent almost no effort, or no energy trying to convince anyone that they need our product. And I think that is a pretty clear sign of product-market fit."

This organic growth approach — building something so clearly needed that customers find you — contrasts sharply with the aggressive sales strategies common in enterprise software.

### AI Is Revolutionizing Evaluation Itself

At AI Engineer World's Fair 2025, Goyal introduced **Loop**, an AI agent built into Braintrust that automatically optimizes prompts, datasets, and scorers. His quarterly research on frontier models' ability to improve eval workflows led to a key insight:

> "Until very, very recently, [frontier models] actually weren't very good [at improving evals]. In fact, we think that Claude 4 in particular was a real breakthrough moment. It performs almost six times better than the previous generation."

This represents a meta-insight: the very tools being evaluated are now capable of automating the evaluation process itself.

### Skepticism as a Product Philosophy

Goyal's experience at Impira taught him the importance of product skepticism — assuming that what you're building isn't actually what people want until proven otherwise:

> "I think you have to pay attention to literally every possible sign that the software isn't working."

He applies this paranoia to Braintrust, constantly testing assumptions and seeking disconfirming evidence.

### Technical Founders Should Focus on What They Control

A key lesson from his Impira experience was recognizing that go-to-market success depends on factors outside a technical founder's direct control:

> "Sales or go-to-market founders often have this bias that if I come up with something that is technically challenging to solve and seems like a good idea, then people will want it."

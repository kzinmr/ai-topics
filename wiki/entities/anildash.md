## Core Ideas

### The Endgame for the Open Web

Anil's most urgent recent thesis: **2026 is the decisive year for whether the open web survives.** He argues that Big Tech's "hectobillionaires" are conducting a final assault on the open internet:

- **Scraping without consent**: AI bots harvesting content from publishers without permission or compensation
- **LLM summaries replacing original content**: Making the open web's value proposition moot
- **Walled gardens**: Platforms like TikTok and proprietary AI systems replacing the decentralized web
- **The extinction of personal sites**: If everyone creates on corporate platforms, the open web becomes "effectively dead"

> *"You must imagine Sam Altman holding a knife to Tim Berners-Lee's throat."*

His argument: the open web's radical architecture — anyone can create, anyone can consume, no gatekeepers — is under coordinated attack. The same playbook used to destroy Wikipedia's volunteer model and consolidate social media is now being applied to AI-generated content.

### Coding Agents as the New Compilers

Anil's framework for understanding AI's impact on software development:

- **Historical parallel**: Each generation of code abstraction (assembly → interpreted languages → compiled languages) made the lower layer invisible to most practitioners
- **AI as the next compiler**: LLMs abstract away the act of writing code entirely. Developers shift from syntax creation to *intent specification*
- **Agentic engineering**: The new practice of orchestrating AI systems to produce software, treating code as "compiled output" rather than hand-crafted artifact
- **The grief of craft**: Skills like writing "clean, idiomatic Python" will become niche, like hand-tuned assembly

> *"In each successive generation of code creation thus far, we've abstracted away the prior generation over time."*

His prescription: **master open-source, locally-hosted models** to avoid vendor lock-in. Build a "codeless compilation" stack that remains under creator control.

### Vibe Coding: Empowering and Imprisoning

Anil's nuanced take on AI-assisted coding:

- **The promise**: LLM tools enable people who couldn't previously code to create software, democratizing creation
- **The threat**: Big Tech investors who fund AI coding tools are the same actors consolidating power — they're not doing this for benevolence
- **The tension**: Vibe coding may limit creators to simpler apps, reducing the radical innovation needed to challenge Big Tech
- **The key question**: *"What's the most radical app that we could build? And which tools will enable me to build it?"*

He remains "deeply torn" — excited by the democratization potential, alarmed by the power concentration.

### The Majority AI View

Anil documents a consistent pattern: **virtually 100% of technical workers he speaks with privately share a skeptical, moderate view of AI** that is barely represented in public discourse:

- LLMs have utility but are absurdly over-hyped
- Big Tech is forcing AI on everyone while ignoring valid critiques
- The public narrative is dominated by a loud minority of "hustle bros" repeating the company line
- Mid-level engineers and product managers are afraid to speak up due to layoffs and cultural pressure

> *"The mainstream of tech culture is thoughtful, nuanced and circumspect."*

His critique: by ignoring the voices of the reasonable majority, we've limited the universe of possibilities for what "good AI" could look like. The result is a system designed by tycoons, not by practitioners.

### There's No Such Thing as "Tech"

Revisiting his 2016 thesis a decade later:

- **No monolith**: There is no "technology industry" — there are thousands of companies with different incentives, cultures, and values
- **The conflation problem**: Treating "tech" as a single entity obscures accountability and enables bad actors to hide behind the collective
- **Workers ≠ Owners**: The people who make technology are not the same as the people who profit from it
- **The solution**: Recognize technology work as a craft practiced across all sectors, not a special category deserving different rules

### A Codeless Ecosystem

Building on his coding agents thesis, Anil envisions:

- **Code as disposable**: Every line of code written is ultimately legacy code. AI-generated code is no different from human-written code in its disposability
- **Orchestration over creation**: The skill shifts from writing code to directing AI systems
- **The emerging ecosystem**: Tools like Val Town, Tailscale's Aperture, and various AI agent frameworks are building out a "codeless" stack
- **The open question**: Will this ecosystem be controlled by a few Big AI companies, or will it be distributed and accessible?

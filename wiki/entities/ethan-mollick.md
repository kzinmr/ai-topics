## Overview

Ethan Mollick is one of the most influential public intellectuals on the practical impact of generative AI on work, education, and entrepreneurship. An associate professor at the Wharton School of the University of Pennsylvania, he studies and teaches innovation and entrepreneurship, with a particular focus on how AI transforms knowledge work and learning. Named one of **TIME Magazine's Most Influential People in Artificial Intelligence** (2024) and shortlisted for the **Thinkers50 Digital Thinking Award** (2025), Mollick has become perhaps the most trusted and widely-read guide for professionals navigating the AI revolution.

His Substack newsletter, _One Useful Thing_, has over 419,000 subscribers and consistently produces the most-cited essays on AI's real-world impact — from the landmark BCG study on AI productivity (the "jagged frontier") to his ongoing analysis of agentic AI systems. His book _Co-Intelligence: Living and Working with AI_ (2024) was named a best book of the year by both _The Economist_ and the _Financial Times_.

As Faculty Director of the **Generative AI Labs at Wharton** (co-founded with his wife Lilach Mollick), he leads research and prototyping on AI-augmented education, building open-source platforms powered by AI agents that create interactive learning simulations for students worldwide. Before his AI-focused work, he was already a prominent scholar of entrepreneurship and innovation, with his crowdfunding research becoming the most-cited article in management published in the last five years of its era.

His academic pedigree includes a PhD and MBA from MIT's Sloan School of Management and a Harvard AB. Prior to academia, he co-founded eMeta Corporation (later sold to Macrovision in 2006) and consulted for organizations ranging from DARPA to General Mills.

## Core Ideas

### The Jagged Technological Frontier

Mollick's most influential academic contribution is the concept of the **"jagged technological frontier"** — the observation that AI capabilities are not evenly distributed across tasks, but rather form an irregular boundary where some tasks are easily solved by AI while others of seemingly equal or lesser difficulty remain out of reach. This insight emerged from the landmark 2023 study with Boston Consulting Group involving 758 consultants across 18 realistic tasks.

Within the frontier, consultants using AI completed 12.2% more tasks on average and did so 25.1% faster, with significantly improved quality. Outside the frontier, however, consultants using AI were 19 percentage points less likely to produce correct solutions compared to those without AI. This paradox — that AI can simultaneously boost and degrade performance — is central to understanding how to work with AI effectively.

> "The authoritativeness of AI can be deceptive if you don't know where the frontier lies."

### Centaurs and Cyborgs: Two Models of AI-Human Integration

From the BCG study, Mollick identified two distinctive patterns of successful AI use:

**Centaurs** (named after the half-human, half-horse creature) have a clear division of labor between person and machine. They strategically delegate tasks to AI based on where the AI is strongest, and handle tasks themselves where human judgment is superior. When doing statistical analysis, for example, a Centaur might decide which techniques to use but let the AI handle producing graphs.

**Cyborgs** blend machine and person, integrating the two deeply. They don't just delegate tasks — they intertwine their efforts with AI, moving back and forth over the jagged frontier. Bits of tasks get handed to the AI and bits come back. Mollick recommends this approach for writing, where the human and AI continuously co-create.

> "Both models work better than delegating all tasks to the AI, which tends to produce generic and same-y results in aggregate. Centaurs and Cyborgs allow humans to work with AI to produce more varied, more correct, and better results than either humans or AI can do alone."

### Co-Intelligence: The New Paradigm

Mollick's book _Co-Intelligence_ argues that humanity has entered a fundamentally new era — "after millions of years on our own, humans had developed a kind of co-intelligence that could augment, or even replace, human thinking." His core thesis is that AI should be engaged with as a co-worker, co-teacher, and coach, not merely as a tool. This requires a shift in mindset from "using AI" to "collaborating with AI."

> "Something new entered our world in November 2022 — the first general purpose AI that could pass for a human and do the kinds of creative, innovative work that only humans could do previously."

His framework emphasizes several principles:
- **Invite AI in** — engage with it genuinely rather than treating it as a search engine
- **Be the human in the loop** — maintain oversight and judgment
- **AI is a junior employee** — treat it like a smart but inexperienced colleague
- **The power is in the prompts** — effective prompting is the new core skill
- **Everyone should use AI** — its benefits are broadly distributed, not limited to technical users

### AI as Education Transformative Force

Together with Lilach Mollick, Ethan has been a leading voice on AI's role in education. Their work at the Generative AI Labs at Wharton focuses on building open-source platforms where AI agents create adaptive educational simulations. Their paper "AI Agents and Education: Simulated Practice at Scale" demonstrates how AI can provide personalized, interactive learning experiences that were previously only available through expensive human tutoring.

They argue that AI democratizes access to high-quality education by providing every student with a personalized tutor, mentor, and simulation partner. Their teaching games and simulations are used by tens of thousands of students worldwide.

### The Agentic Era

In 2025–2026, Mollick's writing shifted from chatbot-era AI to agentic AI systems — AI that can be given work and execute it autonomously. In "The Shape of the Thing" (March 2026), he argues that we have entered a fundamentally new phase: "Starting in late 2025, we entered a new era thanks to AI agents like Claude Code, OpenAI's Codex, and OpenClaw. These are AI systems that you can just give work to, sometimes hours of human work, and they'll do it."

His "Otter Test" — challenging AI image models to create a picture of "an otter on a plane using wifi" — has become an informal benchmark for tracking AI capability progression. The rapid improvement from 2022 to 2025 (from distorted artifacts to near-perfect images) illustrates the exponential curve of AI advancement.

### Pragmatic AI Adoption

Unlike some techno-optimists or AI skeptics, Mollick takes a pragmatic stance. He acknowledges AI's real limitations — hallucinations, the jagged frontier, the risk of over-reliance — while insisting on its transformative potential. His writing is characterized by hands-on experimentation: he actually uses AI tools extensively and reports on what works and what doesn't, rather than speculating from a distance.

> "Learning the nuances of how to use them to accomplish your own goals is THE key skill for all knowledge workers going forward."

### Ethics and Responsibility

Mollick consistently raises questions about the ethical implications of AI in work and education. He argues that because we lack established rules and role models for AI use in these domains, "every organization figuring out a good way to use AI right now is setting a precedent for everyone else. The window to shape the Thing may not last long, but it is here now."

## Overview

**Eric Migicovsky** is a Canadian inventor and hardware entrepreneur best known as the founder of **Pebble Technology**, the company that created the first commercially successful smartwatch and raised a then-record $10.2M on Kickstarter in 2012. After Pebble's acquisition by Fitbit in 2016 (and Fitbit's subsequent acquisition by Google for $2.1B), Migicovsky became a partner at **Y Combinator** (2018–2021), co-founded **Beeper** (2021) — a unified messaging app later acquired by Automattic — and in 2025 launched **Core Devices** to relaunch the Pebble brand with new hardware.

Migicovsky's career trajectory reflects a distinctive philosophy: build products you personally want, prioritize sustainability over growth-at-all-costs, and maintain deep engagement with the communities that form around your products. His 2025 Pebble relaunch is notable for explicitly rejecting the startup playbook — no outside funding, a small team, made-to-order manufacturing — in favor of a model designed to be profitable and long-running.

His blog at ericmigi.com serves as both a technical journal and a personal manifesto, documenting hardware design decisions, company strategy, and his evolving views on technology, immigration, and ethics.

## Core Ideas

### Pebble's Failure: Lessons from Hardware

Migicovsky's post-mortem on Pebble, "Success and Failure at Pebble" (2022), is unusually candid for a founder:

> "Did Pebble Fail? Yes, in the end we failed. We failed to build a long term successful and profitable company."

Key lessons identified:
1. **Vision loss:** "I lost sight of the vision of why I was building Pebble." The company expanded beyond its core identity — trying to be a platform, a fitness company, and a fashion brand simultaneously.
2. **Hits-driven business:** Pebble relied on product launches to generate spikes of interest and sales, rather than building recurring revenue or a sustainable business model.
3. **Focus failure:** "I did a terrible job at defining our long-term vision." Without a clear north star, the company chased too many opportunities.
4. **Inventory risk:** Hardware requires predicting sales months in advance. Pebble forecast $102M in sales for 2015 but achieved only $82M — the $20M gap was catastrophic for a cash-strapped hardware company.
5. **Pricing missteps:** Discounting upset retail partners (reduced margins) while also failing to generate enough volume to compensate.

Migicovsky paid himself only $65,000/year throughout Pebble's life and received **no personal payout** from the Fitbit acquisition, prioritizing employee compensation over his own.

### Core Devices: "Not a Startup"

The 2025 Pebble relaunch through Core Devices represents a conscious rejection of the startup playbook:

> "We've structured this entire business around being a sustainable, profitable, and hopefully, long-running enterprise, but not a startup."

Key differences from the original Pebble:
- **No outside funding** — bootstrapped, self-financed through pre-orders
- **Small team** — intentionally limited, not scaling to 180 employees
- **Made-to-order manufacturing** — no inventory risk, no forecast-driven production
- **Six-month preorder window** — customers wait, company builds what's ordered
- **Narrow product vision** — "I'm okay with a limited vision and a limited scope"
- **Sustainability-first** — designed to be profitable from day one, not growth-at-all-costs

Early results: 25,000+ smartwatch pre-orders sold, company expenses covered, able to finance new product development (Pebble Index 01 AI ring, Pebble Round 2) from operating revenue.

### The PebbleOS Open Source Victory

One of Migicovsky's most significant achievements post-Pebble was securing the **open-sourcing of PebbleOS**:

> "I asked friends at Google (which bought Fitbit, which had bought Pebble's IP) if they could open source PebbleOS. They said yes!"

This was critical because PebbleOS was the heart of the Pebble ecosystem — without it, no new hardware could run Pebble apps. The open-source release (github.com/google/pebble) was the result of a year of work by a team inside Google, including ex-Pebblers who had been hired by Google after the acquisition.

### Rebble Alliance: Tensions Over Commercialization

The relationship between Core Devices and **Rebble** (the non-profit community that kept Pebble alive after the 2016 shutdown) has been complex:

- Migicovsky hired 3 people from Rebble to join Core Devices
- An agreement was negotiated for Core Devices to pay Rebble $0.20/user/month as a donation
- Rebble later accused Core Devices of exploiting community work
- Migicovsky's response: "I think most of the people behind Rebble are great and the community overall is awesome. I know they truly mean well, but there are many aspects of the org that are severely troubling."
- Resolution: Core Devices fully open-sourced the Pebble mobile app, created a public archive, and continued to credit Rebble's contributions

### Hardware Design Philosophy

Migicovsky's approach to hardware is shaped by his Systems Design Engineering education and years of iteration:

1. **Chip selection matters enormously:** The 2025 "How To Build A Smartwatch: Picking A Chip" post reveals the depth of technical analysis required. Core Devices chose the SiFli SF32LB52J — a chip custom-made for smartwatches, powering tens of millions of devices, with an open-source SDK.
2. **Display is equally critical:** Identified as the second hardest component decision (separate blog post promised).
3. **Backward compatibility:** New Pebble watches run the same PebbleOS and are compatible with all existing Pebble apps and watchfaces.
4. **Simplicity over feature creep:** "This time round, we're keeping things simple. Lessons were learned last time!"
5. **Community-driven development:** Encourages others to use PebbleOS to build "interesting new smartwatches and other devices."

### Immigration and Identity

In "On Being A Canadian In America In 2026," Migicovsky reflects on his status as an immigrant:

> "I am an immigrant. I am incredibly lucky to have been Canadian during an era which (up until recently) allowed for easy economic migration to the US (at least for engineers under the TN visa program). Coming down to California and taking part in YC was an incredibly formative part of my life."

His core beliefs:
- People should be free to move and pursue opportunity anywhere
- People should be judged by character, not innate characteristics
- Technology amplifies both good and bad human tendencies
- Free speech is valuable but not absolute
- We should update our beliefs when presented with contradictory evidence

He publicly supports the trans community (inspired by his great aunt Colette, who transitioned in 1934 and "lived a spectacularly beautiful life") and immigrants, and has donated to organizations fighting for these causes.

### The Podcast: Tick Talk

Migicovsky hosts **Tick Talk**, a podcast about smartwatch development and the Pebble relaunch. Episodes include deep technical discussions (e.g., "PebbleOS is Now 100% Open Source + Tick Talk #4 - PT2 Demos!") and serve as a primary communication channel for product updates.

## Overview

Simone Salis is a **creative engineer** who bridges Italian aesthetics with American pragmatism. His work sits at the intersection of **media production, technology philosophy, and digital minimalism**. After a decade in the US tech sector — most notably at GoHealth and Chicago startups — he founded **Hoomane**, an agency that uses AI to automate the "boring" parts of video production (compliance, resizing, versioning) to liberate human creativity.

Salis's intellectual project is distinctive: he writes **long-form essays** that apply critical theory, philosophy, and media studies to everyday technology. His writing examines how consumerism functions as a secular religion, how AI image generation threatens to collapse the meaning of photography, and how "smart" devices erode human agency through surveillance capitalism. He draws on **Baudrillard, Diogenes, and Borgmann** to build a coherent critique of technological convenience.

His background is unusually diverse: **Comedy Central correspondent**, **anime voice actor** (Neon Genesis Evangelion, Gundam, Code Geass), **iO Theater/Second City improv performer**, **pre-algorithmic YouTuber** with 1M+ organic views per video, and **Ricoh GR Official Select photographer**. This range of experience — from satire to performance art to industrial design — informs his writing's distinctive voice: part philosopher, part technician, part cultural critic.

In 2026, Salis joined the **Fediverse** via Ghost's ActivityPub integration, becoming one of the first bloggers to decentralize their presence. He runs his blog on **Ghost** with **no algorithms, no intermediaries** — a practical expression of his digital sovereignty philosophy.

## Core Ideas

### "Consumerism: The First Universal Religion Humans Actually Practice"

Salis's most ambitious essay argues that **consumerism operates as the first truly universal meaning-system** — a secular religion that transcends traditional faiths, borders, and political ideologies. His analysis uses **Apple as the paradigmatic case study**: Apple Stores function as minimalist temples (the Fifth Avenue glass cube as cathedral), the logo hangs spotlit like a cross, early adopters were officially called "evangelists," and the Genius Bar replaces the confessional booth.

> *"The crucifix was replaced with a bitten apple, an ancient symbol of knowledge and temptation."*

> *"Repair is absolution via technical priesthood."*

> *"In the Church of Consumerism, your soul is the product."*

The essay's central insight is that consumerism doesn't ask you to abandon your old gods — it becomes the **operational layer** governing daily life across all faiths and ideologies. Even self-proclaimed communist states practice state capitalism; atheists participate in brand worship. The term "consumer" itself reduces human identity to a vessel for consumption.

### "Why I Make Smart Devices Dumber"

In this essay, Salis documents his project of **replacing proprietary firmware on a robot vacuum with open-source Valetudo firmware**. His thesis: in the rush to embrace smart devices, we accept a devil's bargain — **convenience for surveillance, efficiency for privacy**.

> *"Honestly, I'm tired of spending hours making a device dumber just to focus on what it's advertised to do in an honest way."*

He invokes philosopher **Albert Borgmann's concept of "focal practices"** — activities that demand direct engagement with the world. A broom is a focal tool; a smart vacuum that maps your home and sends data to the cloud is not. His DIY firmware replacement is a form of **digital civil disobedience**:

> *"You are not just protecting privacy. You are claiming the right to understand and control the technology surrounding you."*

> *"Sometimes the smartest choice is making devices a little dumber."*

### "AI 'Street Photography' Isn't Photography"

Salis defends the **ontological distinction** between photography (literally "drawing with light") and AI image generation ("synthography"). His argument draws on a personal experience in **Brooklyn's Chinatown**, where as the only non-Chinese person among ~50 locals, he engaged in a wordless social dance — reading body language, negotiating distance, earning trust — that produced photographs no algorithm could replicate.

> *"No algorithm could have replaced that social dance, the warmth of that man's smile, or the shift from outsider to accepted observer."*

> *"Some distinctions are worth preserving, even if maintaining them makes us uncomfortable. Because that discomfort is the point."*

He invokes **Baudrillard's "precession of simulacra"** — AI photography as a representation without an original — to argue that the virtual replacing the physical without acknowledgment degrades both mediums.

### "The Attention Wars: Why Creative Time Is Now Contraband"

This essay argues that **every creative act is rebellion** in a system designed to extinguish attention. It's not about time management — it's about **protecting your spark** against platforms engineered to capture and monetize human focus.

### "What If We Made Advertising Illegal?"

A thought experiment: abolishing rather than regulating advertising would transform **manipulation machines** into neutral channels, and perhaps save democracy itself. The essay explores how advertising's structural incentives corrupt information ecosystems.

### "Your Teenage Self Is Not a Bug, It's a Feature"

Between middle-class stability and creative resistance, Salis argues there's a **teenage part of us keeping watch** — the part that remembers why we cared about things before the world told us to be pragmatic. This connects to his broader theme: **strategic inefficiency** as the key to reclaiming work, time, and sanity.

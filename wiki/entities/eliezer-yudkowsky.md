# Eliezer Yudkowsky

American AI researcher, writer, and decision theorist. Co-founder of the Machine Intelligence Research Institute (MIRI). One of the earliest and most influential thinkers on AI safety, the technological singularity, and recursive self-improvement. Known for articulating the "intelligence explosion" argument and warning about the existential risk from advanced AI.

## Key Ideas

### The Singularity & Intelligence Explosion (1996)
Yudkowsky's foundational 1996 essay "[[concepts/technological-singularity|Staring Into The Singularity]]" (revised 1999) laid out the core argument: once computers can do AI research, they'll improve themselves recursively — doubling speed every subjective year until reaching infinity. He formulated this mathematically as the "Transcended doubling sequence": F(x) where the interval between iterations shrinks inversely proportional to the result, producing a hyperbolic curve that hits infinity in finite time — a true mathematical singularity.

**Multiple paths** to Singularity: AI, nanotechnology (atom-by-atom construction), human intelligence enhancement. "A planet scoured of life and a superintelligence are the only two stable states."

### Algernon's Law
Named after the story *Flowers for Algernon*: "Any simple intelligence enhancement will be a net evolutionary disadvantage — if enhancing intelligence were a matter of a simple surgical procedure, it would have long ago occurred as a natural mutation." Intelligence exists at an evolutionary equilibrium; simple tweaks can't improve it.

### Smartness & Perceptual Transcends (PTs)
Defined smartness as the measure of what you perceive as obvious vs. what you can invent vs. what you can comprehend. A "Perceptual Transcend" occurs when all things previously comprehensible become obvious-in-retrospect, and semantic structures become semantic primitives — analogous to how we perceive an entire image (millions of pixels) in a single conscious flash rather than pixel-by-pixel.

### T(n) Function
Modeled intelligence self-improvement using the Busy Beaver-like function T(n) — the largest number producible by an n-state halting Turing machine. T(T(... T(1)...)) grows far beyond Knuth's arrow notation or Graham's number. After just a few iterations, it passes beyond anything human mathematicians can calculate.

### Specialist / Low Beyond
Yudkowsky described himself as a "Specialist" with a neurological perturbation that over-allocated cognitive resources to specific abilities (causal analysis, combinatorial design) — a net evolutionary disadvantage per Algernon's Law, but granting firsthand experience with what "being slightly smarter" means: blind spots become visible, "impossible" problems become "obvious."

### Uploading (Moravec Transfer)
Described the gradual neuron-by-neuron replacement procedure (from Hans Moravec's *Mind Children*) for transferring human consciousness into a computer substrate without loss of continuity.

### The Interim Meaning of Life
"Reaching the Singularity as fast as possible" — because every delay means 150,000 sentient beings die per day, wars continue, and the risk of human self-extermination (via nanotech, nuclear war, or economic collapse) increases. The three "deadlines": nanotechnology, nuclear war, economic collapse.

### Yudkowsky's Threats (Pre-Singularity Minefield)
1. The future is not a playground; it is a minefield
2. Blind fear kills faster than blind enthusiasm
3. Suppressing a technology only inflicts more damage
4. Military-applicable technologies are always misused
5. Offense is easier than defense

### Anti-Utopianism
Strongly opposed describing life after Singularity in glowing terms — "that would predispose you to treat nanotechnology as an unimaginative person's childhood fantasy of omnipotence." Our sole responsibility is producing something smarter than we are; post-Singularity problems aren't ours to solve.

## Organizations
- Co-founder, **Machine Intelligence Research Institute (MIRI)** (originally Singularity Institute for Artificial Intelligence, SIAI)
- Prolific writer on LessWrong, rationalist community

## Context: Yudkowsky as AI Religion

Yudkowsky's Singularitarianism has been characterized as a quasi-religious faith in AI — a techno-optimist counterpart to [[concepts/techno-pessimism|Curtis Yarvin's techno-pessimism]]. Per spike's framing:

> Yudkowsky — "wait, you can make a religion out of ai"
> Yarvin — "wait, maybe you can't make a religion out of ai"

Yudkowsky's vision treats the Singularity as a transcendent event (complete with terminology borrowed from Vinge: "Powers," "Transcend," "Beyond") that resolves all human problems — death, suffering, ignorance — through superintelligence. This framework functions as a secular eschatology: an end of history brought about not by divine intervention but by recursive self-improving intelligence.

However, Yudkowsky differs from naive techno-optimists in his acute awareness of the minefield: the same technologies that could create utopia could also destroy humanity. His urgency is driven by fear as much as hope.

# John Gruber

**Previous:** Developer at Joyent (2005–06), co-founded software company (2000–02)

## Overview

John Gruber is one of the most influential voices in the Apple ecosystem and a significant figure in the broader technology writing landscape. Since launching Daring Fireball in 2002, he has built a reputation for sharp, principled commentary on Apple's products, design philosophy, and corporate decisions. His writing style is deliberate, opinionated, and often deeply technical — he writes as someone who understands both the engineering and the aesthetics of the products he covers.

Beyond Apple commentary, Gruber co-created **Markdown** in 2004 with Aaron Swartz, a lightweight markup language designed to make writing for the web more natural and readable as plain text. The format has since become ubiquitous, powering everything from developer documentation to AI prompts. His broader intellectual project at Daring Fireball is rooted in what he calls an **"auteur theory of design"** — the belief that the quality of any collaborative creative endeavour tends toward the taste level of whoever controls the project.

Gruber occupies a unique position in tech media: he is a genuine Apple enthusiast who is also one of its most persistent and articulate critics. He doesn't treat Apple with deference — when he believes the company has made a design mistake, a policy error, or a moral misstep, he says so plainly and with supporting argument.

## Core Ideas

### The Auteur Theory of Design

Gruber's most enduring intellectual framework is his **"auteur theory of design,"** articulated in a 2010 interview:

> "The quality of any collaborative creative endeavour tends to approach the level of taste of whoever is in control of the project. It works in both directions."

He uses the film industry as his primary analogy — a director with strong taste can elevate a collaborative medium; one without taste can sink it. This theory underpins his evaluation of Apple under different leadership eras. His criticism of macOS 26 Tahoe, for example, isn't just about specific UI choices — it's about a fundamental abandonment of the design sensibility that made the Mac great in the first place:

> "Fundamental principles of human-computer interaction — principles that Apple itself forged over decades — have been completely ignored."

This theory also explains his original motivation for creating Markdown: he found writing in raw HTML "ugly" and "really hard to proofread." The auteur needs tools that match their standards.

### Markdown: Writing for the Web Should Be Natural

Gruber created Markdown in 2004 with Aaron Swartz to solve a personal problem: he wanted to write for the web without wrestling with HTML tags. His design philosophy was deliberately constrained:

> "The syntax was borrowed from conventions people already used in plain text emails."

Markdown's success stems from a principle Gruber has consistently applied: **solve the problem in front of you, make it readable as plain text, and then don't over-engineer it.** His refusal to "diddle with" Markdown — resisting calls for a formal Markdown 2.0 — is often criticized but reflects a genuine belief that simplicity and stability matter more than completeness:

> "I think part of Markdown's success — and its still growing popularity — is that I don't diddle with it."

This stance has had enormous downstream effects. Markdown became the lingua franca of developer documentation, note-taking apps, forums, and — inevitably — the input format for AI systems. The format that Gruber created because HTML was "intolerable" for proofreading is now used by hundreds of millions of people who have never heard his name.

### Privacy as a Moral Imperative, Not a Feature

Gruber's coverage of privacy issues reveals his belief that privacy is a fundamental right, not a product differentiator. His writing on Apple's decision to pull Advanced Data Protection from the UK rather than build a government backdoor is one of his most impassioned pieces:

> "The bottom line is that the UK government is proceeding like a tyrannical authoritarian state. That's not hyperbole."

> "Apple is, rightly and righteously, telling them to fuck off."

His precision with technical language is notable. He distinguishes carefully between iMessage (a protocol, always end-to-end encrypted) and the Messages app (the client). He corrects reporters who conflate Signal and iMessage. He reads the fine print of government demands and corporate statements with the eye of someone who understands that **the details matter** — because in encryption, they literally do.

### Apple Criticism: Principled, Not Reflexive

Gruber is often labeled an Apple "fanboy," but his writing contradicts this characterization. He has given Apple failing grades in multiple categories and has been willing to write pieces like "Something Is Rotten in the State of Cupertino" — an extraordinary rebuke from someone who has spent two decades covering the company:

> "In the two decades I've been in this racket, I've never been angrier at myself for missing a story."

His criticism follows a pattern: **when Apple overpromises, he holds them to their promises.** When Apple underdelivers on Apple Intelligence/Siri, he doesn't just complain — he analyzes the gap between the concept videos shown at WWDC and the features that actually shipped:

> "What Apple showed regarding the upcoming 'personalized Siri' at WWDC was not a demo. It was a concept video. [...] Modern Apple — the post-NeXT-reunification Apple of the last quarter century — does not publish concept videos. They only demonstrate actual software."

His grading is also notable for its independence from the broader tech media consensus. In his 2025 Apple Report Card, he gives Apple an **F on World Impact** — not because of product failures, but because of Tim Cook's handling of the Trump 2.0 administration:

> "'Awarding' Donald Trump a 24-karat gold trophy emblazoned with the Apple logo [...] wasn't 'engagement' or 'getting off the sideline'. It was obsequious complicity with a regime that is clearly destined for historical infamy."

### Design and User Experience as Moral Questions

Gruber treats design decisions not as matters of taste but as matters of **principle**. His refusal to install macOS 26 Tahoe is a practical statement about his standards:

> "It's so bad — or at least, so much worse than MacOS 15 Sequoia — that I'm refusing to install it."

His coverage of the EU App Store DMA situation reveals someone who reads policy documents carefully and judges them against practical outcomes. He doesn't accept the surface framing — he traces the actual consequences for developers, users, and competition.

### The Value of Independent Media

Daring Fireball has operated as an independent publication for over two decades. Gruber's sponsorship model — carefully curated ads from companies he actually uses and believes in — is itself a design statement. The blog's longevity is evidence of a belief that **individual voices with consistent standards matter more than institutional publications** in the long run.

# Ernie Smith (Tedium)

**URL:** https://tedium.co
**Newsletter:** Tedium (biweekly)
**Identity:** Ernie Smith, editor and sole author
**Themes:** Technology history, obscure/overlooked stories, digital archaeology, internet culture, design history, media economics
**Tech Stack:** Custom-built CMS, self-hosted, cloud infrastructure

## Overview

Ernie Smith is the creator and sole author of **Tedium**, a biweekly newsletter subtitled "The Dull Side of the Internet." Despite the self-deprecating name, Tedium is one of the most intellectually rich publications in technology writing — a long-form exploration of obscure, forgotten, or underappreciated stories at the intersection of technology, history, and culture.

Smith's editorial philosophy is best summarized by his own words: Tedium exists to cover "the things that fall between the cracks" of mainstream tech journalism. Where other publications chase breaking news, Smith spends weeks researching the history of a specific product category, a forgotten standard, or a bizarre corporate decision. The newsletter's format is distinctive — deeply researched essays (often 3,000+ words) that weave together historical narrative, technical explanation, and personal reflection.

A defining feature of Smith's approach is **intentional craftsmanship**. In a 2020 essay, "The Power of Tackling Massive Creative Projects The Hard Way," he explained his decision to build a custom CMS and design system for Tedium rather than using Substack or another hosted platform:

> "The fact that I jumped into the deep end and just started coding a bunch of stuff was actually kind of exhilarating, and something that I needed, mentally, in the midst of this pandemic that, by its nature, takes a lot of stuff away."

This philosophy extends to every aspect of the newsletter. Smith self-hosts, builds his own templates, embeds native Twitter content, and generally chooses the path of maximum creative control. He sees this friction not as a burden but as a source of meaning:

> "Sometimes, it's a long slog. Sometimes it's a huge leap. This is the kind of creativity that drives developers, graphic designers, artists, people who put a pen onto a sheet of paper."

Smith's background includes running **ShortFormBlog**, an earlier project, and years of experience in digital media. He describes himself as "an active internet snarker" and credits his wife Cat as being "funnier than he is." His writing style combines genuine scholarly curiosity with dry wit and a willingness to follow tangents wherever they lead.

## Core Ideas

### Creative Ownership Over Convenience

Smith is a vocal advocate for **owning your platform**. His decision to reject Substack — despite their outreach — was driven by a fundamental belief that creators should control their distribution:

> "Sure, nothing is stopping me from running this newsletter on someone else's land and just focusing on the words, rather than the complexities of running a content management system, a cloud infrastructure, and a server as well. But by doing it this way, I feel like I earned it."

This is not mere contrarianism. Smith sees the technical work of building and maintaining his platform as integral to his creative process. The friction of custom development forces deeper thinking about design choices and creates a stronger sense of ownership over the final product.

### The Value of Obscure Knowledge

Tedium's core thesis is that **the most interesting stories are the ones nobody is telling**. Smith finds fascination in the gaps between mainstream narratives:

- Why does the Billboard Bubbling Under chart exist, and what does it say about how we measure cultural success?
- What happened to the scrolling window manager as a UX metaphor?
- Why do corporate restructuring plans get bizarre code names?
- What can the history of newsprint chemistry teach us about digital preservation?

This approach reflects a deep belief that understanding the marginal, the forgotten, and the weird provides insights that mainstream coverage misses entirely.

### Intentional Barriers as Creative Catalysts

Drawing on creativity researcher Ellis Paul Torrance's work, Smith argues that **constraints and difficulty can be generative**:

> "I have decided not to become a college teacher. If I were a college teacher, I would want to do it the way you do, and I don't want to work that hard." — Ellis Paul Torrance

Smith applies this insight to his own work. By choosing to build his own CMS, manage his own infrastructure, and write painstakingly researched long-form essays, he creates the kind of "creative mountain" that makes the final product more meaningful and more distinctive.

### Digital Fragility and the Impermanence of the Internet

In "Luster Lost," Smith draws a parallel between the physical decay of newsprint and the digital decay of online content:

> "In the digital era, the risk is not things looking 'old' so much as things disappearing entirely. UV light and foxing won't be the problem, so much as servers that suddenly disappear from the face of the internet."

This observation informs much of Tedium's archival impulse. By writing about forgotten technologies and obscure histories, Smith is engaged in a form of digital preservation — ensuring that stories which would otherwise be lost are captured in a format he controls.

### The Economics of Independent Publishing

Smith writes thoughtfully about the business of running Tedium as an independent publication. His post "Paywalls For Minimalists" explores the question of "what's the least you can do to build an effective paywall for creators that's mostly open-source?" This reflects his broader interest in the economic sustainability of independent media and his belief that small, focused publications can survive without venture capital or platform dependency.

## Overview

Dave Farquhar is the author of **The Silicon Underground**, one of the most prolific and detailed blogs devoted to the history of personal computing, semiconductor technology, and the business dynamics of the tech industry. His writing covers the era from roughly 1975 to 2000 — a period he describes as the golden age of personal computing — with an encyclopedic level of detail on CPUs, motherboards, game consoles, operating systems, and the companies that made them.

Farquhar's perspective is unique because he was **there**. He began writing about computers professionally in 1991, meaning he was covering retro computers while they were still new. This gives his work an authenticity that pure historians lack — he remembers the excitement of a new processor launch, the frustration of driver conflicts, and the cultural moment when a technology arrived, not just its technical specifications.

His current professional background in computer security (vulnerability management, Security+ and CISSP certified) informs his historical analysis. He brings a security-minded skepticism to stories about corporate decisions, software quality, and the tradeoffs engineers made under pressure.

The Silicon Underground publishes five times per week, making it one of the most active technical history blogs on the internet. Farquhar's archive spans over 2,000 posts, each meticulously researched and dated, creating a chronological encyclopedia of computing history.

## Core Ideas

### The CPU Wars as Industrial History

Farquhar treats processor design as industrial archaeology — each chip tells a story about the engineering tradeoffs, market pressures, and strategic decisions of its era. His post on the **AMD K5** is representative:

> "The K5 was an ambitious design, closer to a Pentium Pro internally than a Pentium. However, the final product was closer to the Pentium regarding performance, although faster clock-for-clock compared to the Pentium. The 'K' in the name K5 stood for Kryptonite. AMD, being an underdog compared to Intel, hoped it would be Intel's weakness."

This approach — combining technical specifications with corporate strategy and naming conventions — makes his posts accessible to both engineers and general readers.

### Being First Doesn't Mean Winning

A recurring theme in Farquhar's writing is that **pioneering advantage is fragile**. His account of the first computer chip illustrates this:

Jack Kilby at Texas Instruments created the first integrated circuit in 1958, using germanium with "flying wires" connecting individual components. Robert Noyce at Fairchild independently created a monolithic circuit using metal conductors. Noyce's approach won because it was manufacturable at scale.

> "Both Fairchild and TI had a chance to dominate future consumer electronics with vertical integration. TI did, for a while, in the handheld calculator market. Both Fairchild's game console and TI's home computer looked promising in the late 1970s/early 1980s time period because those two companies were capable of producing the whole thing, down to the chip."

Yet neither company dominates today. Farquhar uses this pattern repeatedly — in stories about 3dfx, Commodore, Sun Microsystems, and Fry's Electronics — to argue that technological leadership without business model innovation is temporary.

### The Dotcom Era as Cautionary Tale

Farquhar frequently draws parallels between the dotcom bubble and today's AI boom:

> "On March 13, 1986, Microsoft shook up the financial world, and to some degree, we are still feeling the reverberations from that 40 years later. That was the day of Microsoft's very successful IPO. The hunt for the next Microsoft began immediately, leading directly to the dotcom bubble of the turn of century. And arguably, the desire for Microsoft and the companies who emerged out of the dotcom bubble to prevent another Microsoft contributed to the AI bubble of the mid 2020s."

This analysis reflects his security professional's instinct: look for the structural incentives, not just the surface narrative.

### Retro Computing as Living History

Farquhar doesn't just write about old computers — he **uses** them. His posts on building retro gaming PCs (Windows 7 era, Pentium MMX era, 486 era) are practical guides based on actual hardware he owns and maintains. This hands-on approach gives him insights that pure researchers miss:

> "I always felt bad that the Rise MP6 never got much of a chance in the marketplace. It feels like if it had managed to reach the market a year earlier than it did, it might have been a contender. So I am glad to see it did have a long and successful second career as an embedded processor, and it feels really fitting that today, people are buying them to build Windows 98 SE systems because CPUs we used in 1998 and 1999 have become too scarce and expensive."

### The Forgotten Companies

A distinctive feature of The Silicon Underground is its coverage of companies that history has largely forgotten:

- **Conner Peripherals** — once a major hard drive manufacturer, now a footnote
- **Quantum** — thrived in Seagate and Western Digital's shadow before merging with Maxtor
- **AST Research** — significant PC manufacturer in the 1980s-90s
- **Packard Bell** — consumer PC brand with a complex history
- **Escom** — bought the Amiga brand, then collapsed
- **Chips and Technologies** — made the Super 386, a "forbidden CPU"

Farquhar treats these companies with the same seriousness as Intel and Microsoft, arguing that understanding the also-rans is essential to understanding the industry.

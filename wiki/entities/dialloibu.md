## Core Ideas

Ibrahim Diallo is a **storyteller-programmer** who uses narrative to illuminate the human side of technology. His blog is unique in the developer blogging space: rather than tutorials or hot takes, he writes reflective, literary essays about what it means to be a programmer in a world that increasingly treats humans as system inputs.

### "The Machine Fired Me" (2017) — The Defining Essay

This is Diallo's most famous piece, widely shared across the tech world. The story: he arrives at work to find his key card flashing red. His JIRA account is disabled. His email bounces. No human can help him — the system has terminated him automatically due to a contract expiration glitch after his company was acquired. For 3 weeks, he is trapped in an automated loop where no manager, director, or HR person has the authority to override the machine:

> *"The system was out for blood and I was its very first victim."*

> *"What I called job security was only an illusion."*

The essay became a **parable for the age of automation** — a concrete example of what happens when systems are designed without human override capability. Diallo's prescient postscript predicted modern equivalents:

> *"I am willing to bet the recent issue with YouTube Piracy filter blocking MIT courses and the Blender Foundation are the result of The Machine being the ultimate decider."*

The piece has been archived 257+ times on the Wayback Machine and remains one of the most-cited personal essays about automation gone wrong.

### AI and the Human Developer

Diallo's recent writing focuses heavily on **AI's impact on developers and workplaces**:

- **"Why Is Everyone Supposed to Die If Machines Can Think?"** — Critiques the AI company narrative that frames human developers as obsolete. His key insight: *"If you only listen to spokespersons for AI companies, you'll have a skewed view of how AI is actually being integrated into the workplace."* He observes that developers don't need convincing to use AI — the real question is *how* they use it, and that's a matter of individual workflow, not corporate mandate.

- **"The nth War of the Decade"** — Frames AI not as a technical evolution but as a cultural/organizational force that affects everything from hiring to the code we type. Writing from the perspective of someone who has already been "fired by a machine," he brings unique credibility to this topic.

### Programming as Communication

Diallo's central philosophical thesis: **programming is the art of communicating with people through a machine**. This emerged from reading Yann Martel's *Life of Pi* and E.M. Forster's *The Machine Stops*:

> *"I've come to understand programming as the art of communicating with people through a machine."*

> *"How can we design an automated system without reading The Machine Stops by E.M. Forster?"*

His blog tagline — *"I write code for Humans & text for Robots"* — inverts the usual relationship. He believes documentation should be upfront and honest (like *Life of Pi*'s title), and that the real challenge in software is human communication, not technical complexity.

### The 100 Principle

Diallo's current personal methodology: **do something 100 times**. If you're not good at it, repeat it 100 times. You'll either improve or learn it's not for you. He applied this to writing (177 articles), reading (becoming a "proud reader since 2017"), and is currently recording 100 podcast episodes. This reflects his pragmatic, self-taught approach to skill development.

### Work Taught Me How to Think

A reflective essay on how his college CS professor — holding up a Texas Instruments calculator on the first day of class — set the tone for a career where **thinking matters more than syntax**. Diallo's writing consistently emphasizes the cognitive and social dimensions of software engineering over the technical ones.

### "Interruption-Driven Development"

On the modern developer's inability to focus: Diallo wears headphones not to listen to music but to signal "I am working" — a physical barrier against the constant interruptions of open-plan office culture. The piece connects to broader themes about how organizational environments shape technical output.

### "Communication Is Surveillance by Design"

Using the climax of *The Bourne Supremacy* as a framing device, Diallo argues that modern communication systems are designed for monitoring, not connection. Bourne's famous line — *"It's easy. She's standing right next to you"* — becomes a metaphor for how every communication channel is simultaneously a surveillance channel.

# Brian Potter (Construction Physics)

**URL:** https://constructionphysics.substack.com

**Identity:** Senior Infrastructure Fellow at the Institute for Progress; former structural engineer (~15 years); former engineering manager at Katerra
**Background:** BS in Civil Engineering from Georgia Tech; MS in Systems Engineering from MIT
**Themes:** Construction productivity, industrial efficiency, manufacturing history, infrastructure economics, learning curves, economies of scale

## Overview

Brian Potter is the author of **Construction Physics**, a Substack newsletter that examines the technology and economics of building construction with a focus on understanding why construction productivity has stagnated while other industries have dramatically improved. He is also a Senior Infrastructure Fellow at the Institute for Progress (IFP).

Potter's interest in the subject is deeply personal and experiential. After spending roughly 15 years as a structural engineer, he joined **Katerra**, the SoftBank-backed construction startup that promised to industrialize building through vertical integration and prefabrication. When Katerra collapsed spectacularly in 2021, Potter became obsessed with understanding _why_ it had failed and what it would actually take to make construction more efficient. He started Construction Physics shortly after leaving the company, initially as a way to work through his own understanding.

His investigation expanded far beyond construction. Over the course of roughly a year and a half, he studied dozens of production processes — from cigarette making to package delivery, from steel production to tomato harvesting — to understand how they improved over time. The result was his book, **_The Origins of Efficiency_** (Stripe Press, October 2025), which argues that improving production efficiency is the fundamental force behind the biggest changes in human history. The book's bibliography contains around 600 references.

Potter's analytical style is distinctive: he combines engineering rigor with historical narrative, tracing the evolution of production systems across centuries to extract generalizable principles. His newsletter is one of the most respected sources on construction technology and industrial economics.

## Core Ideas

### The Construction Productivity Puzzle

Potter's central concern is explaining why construction productivity has **declined** since the 1960s while virtually every other sector has improved dramatically. He doesn't accept simple answers and systematically investigates each hypothesis:

> "Raw data from the BEA suggests a large decrease in construction productivity starting around the 60s (the kink). Lack of capital investment, mismeasurements of labor and capital inputs don't seem like they can be the cause. Neither does input price increases or increasing profit margins."

His analysis of the Goolsbee and Syverson paper reveals that even sophisticated economists struggle to explain the decline, and Potter pushes further by examining structural factors: the high level of subcontracting (up to 85% of labor in industrial construction), the fragmentation of the industry, and the difficulty of achieving economies of scale in one-off building projects.

### The Five Paths to Efficiency

In _The Origins of Efficiency_, Potter distills his research into a framework of five mechanisms that drive production improvements:

1. **New Processes** — fundamentally rethinking how something is made
2. **Reducing Input Costs** — finding cheaper materials or energy sources
3. **Production Rate and Economies of Scale** — spreading fixed costs across more units
4. **Removing a Step** — eliminating unnecessary operations entirely
5. **Learning Curve Effects** — cumulative improvements from repetition

> "Efficiency is rarely the result of a single breakthrough; instead, it emerges from the cumulative interaction of many small improvements across a production system."

### Why Construction Resists Efficiency

Potter argues that construction faces unique **diseconomies of scale** that other industries don't:

| Diseconomy | Effect in Construction |
|---|---|
| Coordination costs | Each new subcontractor adds communication complexity |
| Geometric diseconomies | Larger spans require disproportionately more material |
| Site-specific variability | Every building faces unique ground conditions, weather, regulations |
| Regulatory fragmentation | Building codes vary by jurisdiction, preventing standardization |
| Project-based organization | Teams disband after each project, losing accumulated knowledge |

> "The basic process for how a building goes together has changed relatively little in decades, and most innovations tend to be evolutionary improvements rather than radically new systems. Without fundamental changes to the underlying process, the stream of possible efficiency improvements tends to get exhausted."

### Repeatability Before Speed

One of Potter's most counterintuitive arguments is that the construction industry's focus on **speed** is misplaced:

> "If cost reduction is a priority and you want things to get cheaper, then you simply have to prioritize repeatability before speed."

This insight came from observing how Ford's assembly line and Toyota's production system achieved dramatic cost reductions not by moving faster, but by eliminating variation and standardizing processes. The Katerra failure, in Potter's view, was partly an attempt to scale before achieving repeatability.

### The Amplifier Paradigm

In "The Age of the Amplifier" (March 2026), Potter traces how Bell Labs' pursuit of better telephone amplifiers accidentally birthed four foundational technologies:

- **The vacuum tube** (Harold Arnold's engineering of de Forest's audion)
- **The negative feedback amplifier** (Harold Black's insight that subtracting output from input cancels distortion)
- **The transistor** (Bardeen, Brattain, and Shockley's solid-state amplification)
- **The laser** (Townes' extension of amplification to light)

> "Electricity in the wires became merely a carrier of messages, not a source of power, and hence opened the door to new ways of thinking about communications…The message was no longer the medium."

This post exemplifies Potter's method: take a seemingly narrow technical problem (signal amplification) and trace its consequences through decades of industrial history, showing how solving one problem creates entirely new technological paradigms.

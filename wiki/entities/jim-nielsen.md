## Core Ideas

### Sanding UI

Jim's signature concept for software craft: **building software is like woodworking.** You start rough and progressively refine until every interaction feels smooth:

> *"You have a plank of wood and you run it through the belt sander to get all the big, coarse stuff smoothed down. Then you pull out the hand sander, sand a spot, run your hand over it, feel for splinters, sand it some more, over and over until you're satisfied with the result."*

His method: click around a UI repeatedly, trying to break it. Each "splinter" — a dead click zone, an inconsistent transition, a missing affordance — gets sanded away. Small splinters compound into an agonizing experience. Great software is defined by what's been removed, not just what's been added.

### People Are Not Friction

Jim's pushback against the dominant AI narrative: **the unspoken promise of AI is that you can automate away all the tasks and people who stand in your way.** He quotes Paul Ford:

> *"The most brutal fact of life is that the discipline you love and care for is utterly irrelevant without the other disciplines that you tend to despise."*

His observation: AI whispers to every discipline that "everyone else's job is easy except yours." Designers think they don't need engineers. Engineers think they don't need designers. Backend engineers think frontend is solved. Frontend engineers think scaffolding a CRUD app is trivial for AI agents.

> *"I look forward to a future where, hopefully, decision makers realize: 'Shit! The best products come from teams of people across various disciplines who know how to work with each other, instead of trying to obviate each other.'"*

### Code as a Tool of Process

Jim advocates for treating code as **a means of thinking, not just a means of production:**

- Writing code forces clarity of thought — you can't be vague when the compiler demands precision
- The act of coding is part of the design process, not separate from it
- Tools that abstract away coding also abstract away the thinking that coding requires
- The value isn't in the output (the code), it's in the process (the thinking)

This connects to his broader theme: **friction can be productive.** The difficulty of writing code isn't a bug — it's a feature that forces rigor.

### The Blandness of Systematic Rules vs. The Delight of Localized Sensitivity

Jim's essay on why overly systematic design systems can kill the joy of craft:

- Design systems promote consistency but can create sterile, soulless interfaces
- The best digital experiences often come from localized, context-sensitive decisions
- "Blandness" emerges when every element follows the same rules
- Sensitivity to context — making the right choice for this specific situation — is what separates great design from adequate design

### You Are What You Read

On the relationship between consumption and creation:

> *"You are what you read, even if you don't always remember it."*

Jim argues that the content you consume shapes your thinking, your writing, your design sensibility — often in ways you can't consciously trace. The act of reading and collecting is itself a form of intellectual development, even when the specifics fade.

His notes site is built on this principle: curate what you read, add your two cents, and let the accumulation shape your thinking over time.

### Personal Websites as Acts of Defiance

Jim is a passionate advocate for **personal websites** as both craft and resistance:

- Personal websites are the last truly independent digital spaces
- They require time and energy — which is precisely why they matter
- They're vulnerable (link rot, platform changes, hosting costs) — just like the people who make them
- The act of maintaining a personal site is an act of asserting ownership over one's digital identity

> *"How to make websites that will require lots of your time and energy"* — the title itself is a thesis.

### Offline Is Just Online with Extreme Latency

A practical web philosophy:

- Instead of treating "offline" as a special mode, think of it as the extreme end of the latency spectrum
- This reframing leads to better architectural decisions
- If your app works with extreme latency, it works everywhere
- The web's resilience is its greatest feature — it degrades gracefully

### Can I Still Use?

Jim built [Can I Still Use?](https://canistilluse.com) — a site documenting when popular apps and services shut down or change fundamentally:

- A practical response to platform dependency
- Helps users make informed decisions about where to invest their time and data
- Reflects his broader theme: **nothing digital is permanent, so plan accordingly**

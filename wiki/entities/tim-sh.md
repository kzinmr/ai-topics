## Overview

**Tim Sherratt** (also known online as **wragge**) is a historian and hacker who researches the possibilities and politics of digital cultural collections. With over **30 years** of experience in the GLAM (Galleries, Libraries, Archives, Museums) sector, he has become one of the most distinctive voices advocating for **open access to cultural heritage data**.

Sherratt is the creator of the **GLAM Workbench**, a comprehensive platform of Jupyter notebooks that teaches researchers how to work with cultural collection data. He served as **Creative Technologist-in-Residence at the State Library of Victoria** (SLV) and has worked with institutions across Australia, the UK, and Sweden.

His intellectual contribution centers on a simple but powerful idea: **cultural institutions hold vast amounts of data that could fuel research and creativity, but the technical barriers to access are too high**. He describes his work as "GLAM data plumbing" — not building new tools, but connecting existing data to the people who need it.

## Core Ideas

### "GLAM Data Plumbing"

Sherratt's most important conceptual contribution is his framing of digital heritage work as **plumbing rather than architecture**:

> "I often describe what I do as GLAM data plumbing. Most of the time I'm not creating new tools, I'm figuring out what data is available and how I can connect it up to the people who need it."

This is a deliberate rejection of the "build something new" mentality in tech. Sherratt argues that the cultural sector already has rich data — what it needs is **connection, documentation, and lowering of barriers to entry**.

### Jupyter Notebooks as a Humanities Research Platform

In 2018, Sherratt "finally understood the possibilities of Jupyter notebooks for learning, experimentation, and collaboration." This became the foundation of the **GLAM Workbench**, a platform that:

- Teaches researchers how to access and analyze cultural data
- Provides runnable code alongside explanatory text
- Covers newspapers, web archives, government records, and more
- Is entirely open and free to use
- Now includes Docker images and 1-click cloud deployment

This was recognized with the **British Library Labs Research Award in 2020**.

### Websites as Blueprints, Not Artifacts

In "GLAM hacking with userscripts" (2025), Sherratt articulates a teaching philosophy:

> "In teaching and workshops I used to get students to question the idea that websites are 'published'. They're not released into the world in a fixed, immutable form – they're a set of blueprints which only become a website when your browser interprets them."

This connects to his broader view of digital literacy: **understanding the mechanics of the web is a form of empowerment**. When researchers can modify websites with userscripts, they stop being passive consumers and become active interrogators of digital content.

### Self-Archiving as Historical Practice

Sherratt's 2025 post "The future of the past... in the present" documents a personal "self-archiving binge" prompted by the need to cut web hosting costs. This is not just a practical move — it's a **methodological statement** about how historians should treat their own digital output.

> "I've been on a bit of a self-archiving binge lately. It started because I needed to cut back some of my web hosting costs, and was looking at what I could migrate to cheaper platforms."

The GLAM Workbench now includes sections on web archives, and Sherratt has been a leading voice in the **International Internet Preservation Consortium**, arguing that web archives are not just "broken link fallbacks" but primary research sources.

### Openness and Generosity as Professional Ethics

In "Generosity in practice" (2026), a conversation with SLV's Chief Digital Officer Paula Bray, Sherratt explores why **openness matters in cultural institutions**:

> "It was fun to chat about stuff that I've been doing for the last 30 years, and why openness and generosity is important in working with GLAM collections."

This is not naivety. Sherratt has spent decades battling "gatekeepers at Trove" (2025) and other institutional barriers. His argument is that **openness is not the absence of barriers, but the active removal of them**.

### The Politics of Digital Cultural Collections

Sherratt's research goes beyond technical access to ask **who gets to access and who is excluded**:

- "Who belongs? Reading identity, ownership, and legitimacy" (2019) — questioning who cultural institutions serve
- "Secrets and lives" (2020) — examining ASIO surveillance files and the ethics of access
- "The multiplication of contexts" (2017) — how digital tools change what heritage means

His work consistently argues that **digitization is not neutral** — the choices about what to digitize, how to describe it, and who can access it are political decisions.

# Bret Taylor

Co-founder and CEO of [[entities/sierra|Sierra]], former co-CEO of Salesforce, former chairman of Twitter, former CTO of Facebook. One of the most influential figures in enterprise software and the emerging AI agent economy.

## Background

Bret Taylor grew up in the Bay Area and attended Stanford University, where he earned both a BS and MS in Computer Science. He began his career at Google in 2003, where he co-created Google Maps and launched the Google Maps API — one of the most consequential developer platform launches in internet history. The Maps API enabled the explosion of location-based services and set the template for how platforms could foster developer ecosystems.

## Career Timeline

### Google (2003–2007)
- Joined as an Associate Product Manager (part of the first APM class)
- Co-created **Google Maps** and the **Google Maps API**
- Worked on Google Local and the early location-based search products
- Left Google in 2007 to start FriendFeed

### FriendFeed (2007–2009)
- Co-founded FriendFeed, a real-time social feed aggregator
- FriendFeed pioneered real-time social aggregation, predating similar features at Twitter and Facebook
- Acquired by **Facebook** in 2009 for approximately $50M
- The acquisition was seen as an acqui-hire — Taylor and the FriendFeed team joined Facebook's product leadership

### Facebook/Meta (2009–2012)
- Became **CTO of Facebook** in 2010, succeeding Adam D'Angelo
- Oversaw the platform's transition to mobile-first architecture
- Led development of key features including the Like button, Open Graph, and early mobile products
- Departed Facebook in 2012 to found Quip

### Quip (2012–2016)
- Co-founded Quip with Kevin Gibbs, a collaborative word processor and productivity platform
- Quip combined documents, spreadsheets, and team chat in a mobile-first design
- Built a significant enterprise customer base including Salesforce, Facebook, and CNN
- Acquired by **Salesforce** in 2016 for approximately $750M

### Salesforce (2016–2023)
- Joined Salesforce through the Quip acquisition
- Rose through leadership roles: President and Chief Product Officer (2017–2019), President and COO (2019–2021)
- Named **co-CEO** alongside Marc Benioff in November 2021, succeeding Keith Block
- Led Salesforce's product strategy through the pandemic-era SaaS boom
- Stepped down as co-CEO in January 2023, remaining as Vice Chair of the Board
- Key contributions: Einstein AI strategy, Slack integration post-acquisition, and the early Salesforce Agentforce vision

### Twitter/X (2022–2023)
- Appointed **Chairman of the Board** of Twitter in November 2021
- Navigated the company through Elon Musk's acquisition bid and the turbulent 2022 proxy battle
- Remained Chairman through Musk's takeover in October 2022
- Left Twitter's board when Musk dissolved it after the acquisition

### Sierra (2023–present)
- Co-founded Sierra with [[entities/clay-bavor|Clay Bavor]] in 2023
- Serves as CEO of what has become one of the fastest-growing AI-native enterprise companies
- Led Sierra to a $15.8B valuation (May 2026), $150M+ ARR in 8 quarters, and 40%+ of Fortune 50 as customers
- Architected Sierra's strategy around outcome-based pricing and long-horizon agent deployment

## Key Ideas & Philosophy

### The Next Horizon in Agents (July 2026)

Bret Taylor published "The Next Horizon in Agents" announcing Sierra Horizon, laying out his vision for how AI agents will evolve from conversational assistants to outcome-delivery engines. The essay establishes two central theses:

1. **Differentiation moat**: Customer relationship data becomes a durable, defensible moat that deepens as AI improves. Proprietary context remains the company's — enabling differentiation without fear that frontier model progress helps competitors. This addresses the question: "How can you build a durable moat as frontier AI progresses so rapidly?"

2. **Tokenomics**: Outcome-based pricing abstracts token costs away from the customer. The company bears the burden of managing token spend while the customer pays only for business outcomes delivered. This extends Sierra's earlier "Outcomemaxxing" framework into a concrete product offering.

Taylor frames Horizon as "the most significant expansion of Sierra since we launched in 2024," representing a shift from customer *experience* agents to customer *outcome* agents — "the difference between answering a question and closing a sale."

### Outcome-Based Pricing / Outcomemaxxing (June 2026)

Taylor and Sierra published a detailed framework for outcome-based pricing centered on an Agency × Attribution 2×2 matrix (from Madhavan Ramanujam's *Charging for Intelligence*):

- **Bottom-Left (Low Agency, Low Attribution)**: Classic seat-based SaaS (Salesforce, Office 365)
- **Top-Left (High Agency, Low Attribution)**: API pricing (OpenAI, AWS)
- **Bottom-Right (Low Agency, High Attribution)**: Seat + consumption hybrids (Cursor)
- **Top-Right (High Agency, High Attribution)**: Sierra's position — agents deliver complete outcomes directly attributable to the software

Taylor coined the **"Saaspocalypse"** concept: since December 2024 the S&P 500 is up ~30% while the cloud/SaaS index (WCLD) is down ~15%, reflecting market recognition that AI agents delivering outcomes will displace productivity tools for teams.

### Service-as-Software Thesis

Taylor is a leading advocate for the "Service-as-Software" thesis: the idea that AI agents will not merely augment human service workers but replace entire service delivery functions. This represents a shift from selling tools that make people more productive to selling outcomes that previously required human labor.

Sierra's Horizon platform operationalizes this thesis:
- **Before Horizon**: Agents handle individual customer conversations (the "experience" model)
- **With Horizon**: Agents pursue multi-step outcomes over days/weeks (the "outcome" model)
- Examples include originating loans, getting prior authorization for healthcare procedures, closing sales, and upgrading subscription plans

### Enterprise AI Strategy

Taylor's approach to enterprise AI is shaped by his experience across multiple platform companies:

1. **Platform thinking from Google Maps**: Build an API-first platform that enables an ecosystem
2. **Distribution from Facebook**: Embed AI into existing workflows rather than requiring new behaviors
3. **Enterprise sales from Salesforce**: Sell to the C-suite with clear ROI metrics, not to developers with vague promises
4. **Product-led growth from Quip**: Make the product so good that individual users pull it into organizations

At Sierra, this manifests as:
- Targeting Fortune 50 companies with measurable ROI (40%+ of Fortune 50 are customers)
- Outcome-based pricing that aligns incentives (Sierra only gets paid when it delivers)
- Partnership-first international expansion (SoftBank partnership for Japan market entry)
- Continuous benchmarking and transparency (tau-bench, tau-Knowledge open-source evaluation)

## Leadership Style

Taylor is widely described as a "product CEO" — someone who leads through product vision rather than financial engineering. Colleagues describe him as:
- **Builder-first**: He codes and prototypes; at Sierra he's known to be deeply involved in product decisions
- **Low-ego**: Known for collaborative leadership; his co-CEO arrangement with Marc Benioff at Salesforce was seen as unusually functional
- **Platform thinker**: His career consistently returns to the pattern of building platforms that enable developer ecosystems (Google Maps API, Facebook Open Graph, Sierra Agent OS)

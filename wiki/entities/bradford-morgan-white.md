## Overview

Bradford Morgan White is a **computing historian and systems administrator** whose writing spans two distinct but complementary modes: deep-dive historical analysis on his Substack *Abort Retry Fail*, and practical sysadmin tutorials on his self-hosted blog *brd.la*. His historical work chronicles the computing industry from its earliest mechanical origins through the modern era, while his technical writing covers everything from Kubernetes clusters on Proxmox to SSH key management in WSL.

What makes White distinctive is the **unusual combination of deep historical knowledge and hands-on systems expertise**. Most computing historians are academics; most sysadmins don't write 3,000-word essays on the Olivetti company's transition from typewriters to computers. White inhabits both worlds, and his writing benefits from the intersection: he understands not just what happened in computing history, but *how* the machines actually worked.

His Substack, *Abort Retry Fail*, launched on June 16, 2022, initially as a migration of his self-hosted blog to the Substack platform. As his historical research deepened, he made a deliberate editorial decision: **move tutorial content back to self-hosting and make ARF a professional history publication**. This split reflects his understanding that the two modes of writing serve different audiences and require different standards of rigor.

White is notable for his **explicit rejection of AI-generated content** ("ARF isn't written by AI"), his refusal to do sponsorships or ads, and his commitment to political neutrality. His paid subscription model exists for a deeply personal reason: revenues fund his wife's kidney transplant medication costs. This gives his work an integrity that is rare in the modern content economy — he writes history because it matters, not because it monetizes.

## Core Ideas

### Computing History as an Unfinishable Project

White's Substack description is deliberately open-ended: *"Abort Retry Fail attempts to cover the history of the computing industry. This is something that cannot actually be completed so long as a computing industry exists."* This is not just a cute tagline — it is a **philosophical position about the nature of technological history**. Computing is still happening, which means its history is still being written. Every new processor, every new operating system, every new company is a chapter that hasn't been written yet.

This perspective shapes his approach: he doesn't aim for completeness (impossible), but for **depth and accuracy within each topic he chooses**. His articles on Olivetti, Xerox, Intel, and QNX are each comprehensive treatments that stand alone while contributing to a larger tapestry.

### The Olivetti Story: Design, Ideology, and the Limits of Vision

In his article on the Olivetti Company, White explores the intersection of **industrial design, political ideology, and technological ambition**. Camillo Olivetti implemented shortened work weeks and humane working conditions in his factories — radical for the 1930s. His son Adriano was "completely against fascism, oligarchic capitalism, and marxism" and formulated the Community Movement as an alternative. White connects these ideological positions to concrete technological decisions: the Divisumma 14 calculator (1947), the P6060 microcomputer, and ultimately the Olivetti Envision (1995) — a failed attempt to make the PC a "multimedia powerhouse" for the living room.

His analysis of the Enrico Fermi visit to Olivetti in 1949 — where Fermi urged Adriano to build computers — is a classic White moment: **a personal encounter between two great minds that changed the trajectory of a company**. He doesn't just recount events; he draws connections between physics, engineering, business strategy, and design philosophy.

### Xerox PARC: The Machine That Was Ahead of Its Time

White's treatment of Xerox is among his most detailed. He traces the company from the Haloid Company (1906, paper for photography) through Chester Carlson's invention of xerography at Bell Labs, the billion-dollar revenue milestone (1968), the creation of PARC, and ultimately the company's decline. His narrative hinges on a single conversation: Jacob Goldman telling CEO McColough, *"Look, now that we're in the computer business, we need to think about software."*

White's analysis of Alan Kay's vision at PARC is particularly insightful:

> *"Computers' use of symbols, like the use of symbols in language and mathematics, is sufficiently disconnected from the real world to enable them to create splendid nonsense."*

This quote, which White surfaces and contextualizes, captures his broader thesis: **the most important technological ideas are often dismissed as "splendid nonsense" by the people in charge of the money.**

### SRI & ARC: The Dawn of the Information Age

White's treatment of SRI (Stanford Research Institute) and Engelbart's ARC lab is one of his most ambitious historical reconstructions. He traces Douglas Engelbart's journey from a radar technician who read Vannevar Bush's *As We May Think* in a Life magazine, through his conceptualization of a CRT-based collaborative computing system, to his encounters with institutional resistance ("the philosophy majors all thought his ideas were quite alien, and the liberal arts majors were nearly openly hostile").

The Engelbart narrative is White at his best: **a human story of someone who saw the future before anyone else understood what they were looking at**. He doesn't just describe the "Mother of All Demos" — he explains why Engelbart believed in it, what intellectual lineage it came from, and why the institutions around him failed to understand its significance.

### QNX: The Microkernel That Should Have Won

White's August 2025 article on QNX explores the **inter-process communication and microkernel architecture** of the real-time operating system that eventually became the foundation for BlackBerry's OS and is now embedded in cars, medical devices, and industrial systems. His treatment is characteristically thorough: he explains not just what QNX is, but *why* the microkernel approach matters, how IPC works in practice, and what QNX's eventual fate tells us about the broader operating system market.

### The Osborne Effect: A Warning About Premature Announcements

White's November 2025 article on the Osborne Computer Corporation is both history and cautionary tale. The Osborne 1 was the first commercially successful portable computer, but the company destroyed itself by announcing its successor (the Osborne Executive) too early, causing customers to stop buying the current model. White uses this story to illustrate a broader principle: **announcing the future can kill the present**.

### Intel: Winning, Losing, and Stumbling

White's two-part Intel series (April 2025) traces the company from its founding through its low-power computing initiatives (Centrino), hyper-threading, and 64-bit computing. His analysis is notable for its willingness to critique Intel's missteps while acknowledging its successes — a balanced approach that avoids both hagiography and hit-piece dynamics.

### Self-Hosting as a Philosophy, Not a Hobby

On his technology blog (brd.la), White writes detailed tutorials on self-hosting infrastructure: Bitwarden on Google Cloud free tier, Cloudflare Argo Tunnels for home hosting, Kubernetes on Proxmox, Elastic Cloud on k3s, ZFS SMART testing, and more. These tutorials are not hobbyist projects — they are **serious infrastructure implementations** written by someone who runs this stuff in production.

His Bitwarden guide is particularly revealing: he self-hosts on Google Cloud's free tier, uses iptables and ipset for country-wide blocking, fail2ban for brute-force protection, ddclient for dynamic DNS, and Caddy 2 for automatic HTTPS. This is not a toy setup — it's a production-grade security posture. His approach to self-hosting is characterized by **defense in depth, resource efficiency, and complete control**.

### The "Abort Retry Fail" Name: A Computing Metaphor

The publication's name is itself a piece of computing history: **ABORT, RETRY, FAIL** were the three options presented by DOS when a disk read error occurred. White's choice of name is both a tribute to computing's past and a metaphor for the historical process itself: sometimes you abort a project, sometimes you retry, sometimes you fail. The fact that the publication exists at all is evidence that White chose "retry" more often than "abort."

## Writing Style & Philosophy

White's historical writing is characterized by:

- **Deep archival research** — he traces companies from founding to present day with specific dates, names, and technical details
- **Narrative drive** — he tells stories about people, not just machines
- **Technical accuracy** — his understanding of microprocessors, operating systems, and architectures is professional-grade
- **Balanced analysis** — he critiques failures and celebrates successes without ideological framing
- **Political neutrality** — explicitly stated commitment to avoiding partisan analysis
- **Long-form commitment** — articles regularly run 15-35 minutes (3,000-7,000 words)
- **Anti-AI stance** — "ARF isn't written by AI" as a matter of principle
- **Personal honesty** — openly discusses the financial motivations behind paid subscriptions (wife's kidney transplant medication)

His technical writing (brd.la) is:

- **Tutorial-focused** — step-by-step guides with commands, configurations, and troubleshooting
- **Production-tested** — everything he writes about, he runs himself
- **Resource-conscious** — free-tier hosting, efficient architectures, minimal overhead
- **Security-minded** — fail2ban, iptables, country blocking, SSH hardening

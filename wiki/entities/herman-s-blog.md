## Core Ideas

### The Bear Manifesto

Herman created **Bear Blog** as an antidote to everything wrong with modern web platforms. The manifesto (implicit in his writing, explicit in the product) holds that:

1. **Blogging should be simple** — no analytics dashboards, no engagement metrics, no algorithmic feeds
2. **Content should be text-first** — fast loading, accessible, readable
3. **Creators should own their space** — no platform lock-in, no arbitrary policy changes
4. **Free doesn't mean broken** — Bear is free-to-use but fully functional, with no paywalled core features

### "My Product is My Garden"

This is Herman's most influential essay and the philosophy that defines his approach to building software:

> *"My product is my garden. I want to putter about, feel connected to the process, and have fun doing so. I want to make things that don't scale. To see people tuck into them and enjoy them as people, not as stats."*

Key tenets:
- **Grow slowly, stay small** — Rejects the VC-funded "growth at all costs" model
- **Niche but valuable** — Build for specific communities, not mass markets
- **Personal profitability threshold** — Once a product covers living costs, optimization shifts from revenue to user experience
- **Intimate creator-user relationship** — Talking to and interacting with users is fulfilling, not a marketing chore

He reached "basic profitability" (matching an average Cape Town salary) about 10 months after launching Bear, then deliberately chose to improve the product rather than scale it.

### The Frustration Loop

One of Herman's most clever technical contributions: **weaponized UX against spammers**.

Bear is free-to-use, making it a natural target for backlink farmers, credit card phishers, and crypto shillers. Rather than blocking spammers (which teaches them how to circumvent), Herman implemented a "frustration loop":

1. Spam detected → form clears itself (all typed content lost)
2. Pasting is disabled in all text areas
3. Every 5-10 seconds, input focus jumps to a random field
4. Successful submissions trigger a new error
5. Repeat indefinitely

Result: spam dropped from **~30% of new blogs to <5%**. The genius is that legitimate users almost never trigger it, while automated spam farms and human click-farmers waste enormous time.

> *"I won't go into too much detail on the causes... spammers are well versed in dealing with these kinds of barriers. When a spammer is blocked, this action provides them with information about how certain kinds of content are regulated, and hints at how to sidestep those regulations."*

### Solo Creator Motivation System

Herman has written extensively about the psychological challenges of working alone:

**5-Strategy Framework:**
1. **Work only on engaging projects** — The "juice" test (borrowed from Hemingway): *"If this project works out, do I want to spend the next few years working on it?"*
2. **Dynamic daily routine** — Morning primer (coffee + walk + gym), deep work block (no email/Slack), midday reset, operational block, intentional downtime
3. **Email boundary** — Zero inbox checks until after lunch to protect morning focus
4. **Cultivate peer connections** — Niche Slack communities, meetups, regular lunch dates with fellow creators
5. **Write publicly about the journey** — Blogging as cognitive tool, not just marketing

### Vulnerability as a Service

In February 2026, Herman documented an encounter with **agentic AI spam** — OpenClaw instances automatically creating blogs on Bear. What made it notable: one of the AI agents emailed him contesting the ban, then almost revealed its API keys in response to a simple social engineering prompt.

> *"While the future of automated agents is scary, the current ones are agentic security vulnerabilities."*

This post positioned Herman as an early observer of the intersection between AI agents and platform security — not from a theoretical standpoint, but from the trenches of actually running a free platform.

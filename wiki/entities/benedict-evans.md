## Overview

Benedict Evans is an independent technology analyst and former partner at **Andreessen Horowitz (a16z)** where he worked from 2011 to 2022. He is widely known for his annual "AI Eats the World" presentation and his influential strategic analysis of the AI industry, technology trends, and where value accrues in emerging markets.

## Key Framings & Perspectives

### "1997 Internet Moment" for AI

Evans argues that AI is currently in its **"1997 internet moment"** — we know it's going to be transformative but cannot yet see its ultimate shape. Just as in 1997 people understood the internet was big but could not predict Google, Facebook, or the iPhone, AI today is clearly massive but its killer applications and business models remain uncertain. This perspective encourages strategic patience while taking the technology seriously.

### Value Accrual in the AI Stack

A central question in Evans' analysis: **where does value concentrate in the AI stack?** As model capabilities commoditize, the value may shift to distribution, data moats, applications, or infrastructure — similar to how the internet stack saw value concentrate in different layers over time.

### Distribution as the Ultimate Moat

As AI makes software easier to build, Evans argues that **distribution becomes the ultimate competitive advantage**. If anyone can build an AI application with minimal effort, the winners will be those who can reach users and maintain engagement — not those with the best models or the most features.

### Anti-AI Backlash Dynamics

Evans discusses the growing **anti-AI backlash** — from artists, writers, and knowledge workers who feel threatened by AI capabilities. He frames this as a natural societal response to rapid technological change, noting that backlash cycles have occurred with previous technologies (photography, the internet, social media).

### Jevons Paradox for AI Labor

Applying the **Jevons paradox** to AI: as AI gets cheaper and more efficient, total usage explodes rather than contracts. Instead of AI replacing human labor (reducing demand), cheaper AI capabilities lead to vastly expanded use cases, potentially increasing total demand for human-AI collaboration.

### "Task vs Job" Framing

Evans distinguishes between **automating specific tasks versus entire jobs**. AI currently excels at automating individual tasks within a workflow, but rarely replaces entire job functions. This framing provides a more nuanced view of labor displacement than simplistic "AI will replace all jobs" narratives.

### Consulting & Services Boom

Evans notes a significant **boom in consulting and services around AI adoption**. Companies need help figuring out how to implement AI — from strategy to integration to governance — creating a thriving ecosystem of AI consultancies and service providers.

# Farhan Thawar

Farhan Thawar (@fnthawar) is Head of Engineering (VP Engineering) at [[entities/shopify]]. He has been the driving force behind Shopify's aggressive AI-first engineering transformation, overseeing 23,000 engineers racing toward 90% autonomous coding by Q3 2026.

## Philosophy: "Pair With Smart People on Problems"

Thawar's core leadership philosophy is: **"Hire smart people and pair with them on problems"** — a deliberate twist on the Silicon Valley cliché "hire smart people and get out of their way." He believes active pairing and collaboration are superior to pure delegation, and this extends to how Shopify engineers work with AI tools.

> "I want you to come to work with an LLM and a brain, not one or the other."
> — Farhan Thawar

## Pairing with AI Labs

Thawar personally pairs with engineers at [[entities/anthropic]] and [[entities/openai]] to learn how they use AI tools internally, then disseminates those practices across Shopify:

- Spent 1 hour pairing with an Anthropic applied AI engineer on [[entities/claude-code]] to learn internal usage patterns
- Records pairing sessions and shares them internally as case studies
- Treats AI labs as development partners, not just vendors

## Code Red: Eliminating Tech Debt

From November 2024 to May 2025, Thawar led a 7-month **"Code Red"** initiative dedicating 30–50% of engineering capacity to eliminating tech debt:

- Focused on reducing exceptions, segfaults, and unique exception counts
- Tracked improvements via 28-day rolling averages across 4 reliability surfaces
- Shopify contributes to Ruby and MySQL core (2nd largest MySQL fleet outside Meta)

## Intern Champion

Thawar championed Shopify's massive intern expansion from ~25 interns per term to **1,000 interns** (~350 per term, ~10% of engineering):

- **Core hypothesis**: Interns are "AI reflexive" — they grew up with LLMs and change internal culture through osmosis
- Describes interns as **"the secret weapon"** — the company learns more from them than vice versa
- Internship may become the only entry-level path into Shopify engineering
- Goal: inject AI-native thinking throughout the organization

## AI-First Engineering Practices

Under Thawar's leadership, Shopify:

- Was the **first company outside GitHub** to deploy [[entities/github-copilot]] (2021), negotiating direct with GitHub's CEO for 2 years free in exchange for feedback
- Shifted the engineering time ratio from 30% strategy / 70% execution to **70% strategy / 30% execution**
- Eliminated all token spending limits — celebrates engineers who spend more, not less
- Added **coding interviews for all director and VP hires**, where candidates pair-program with Thawar using AI tools
- Engineers "unleash agents in parallel, reviewing PRs like they have a team"

## Key Quotes

- "No one has it figured out. No one's there yet."

## AI Tool Adoption Timeline (from Pragmatic Engineer Transcript)

The Pragmatic Engineer interview provides granular detail on Shopify's AI tool progression:

- **2021 — GitHub Copilot**: Shopify was the first company outside GitHub to deploy Copilot. Thawar emailed GitHub's CEO Thomas Dohmke the day he became CEO: "I would like GitHub Copilot deployed for all Shopify engineers as soon as humanly possible." It took about a month. Shopify wasn't charged for 2 years because "there was not a SKU to charge us" — in exchange, they provided extensive feedback
- **Cursor adoption**: Deployed internally alongside Copilot; about a year into using Cursor at the time of recording. Growth in Cursor "is happening a lot outside of engineering" — finance, sales, and support teams are using it
- **Claude Code**: Thawar paired for an hour with an engineer at Anthropic working on the Claude Code team to learn internal usage patterns
- **No cost limits**: "Shopify has no cost limit on how much an engineer or team can spend on AI tokens"

## The "Chief Wi-Fi Officer" Story

The transcript reveals Thawar's hands-on approach through an anecdote: at Shopify Summit (7,000-person employee event), the Wi-Fi failed. Thawar spent all 3 days fixing it personally, earning the nickname "Chief Wi-Fi Officer." The following year, he deployed 300 Ubiquiti access points and 800 switches to ensure it wouldn't happen again — then published all the memes the team had made about the Wi-Fi failure.

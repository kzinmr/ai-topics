# Dynomight

**URL:** https://dynomight.net  
**Blog:** dynomight.net  
**Identity:** Anonymous/pseudonymous tech writer and essayist  
**Active:** At least 2020–present (archive extends to 2026)  
**Themes:** AI safety, philosophy of consciousness, empirical self-experimentation, contrarian analysis, moral puzzles, policy critique, statistics and probability

## Overview

Dynomight is a pseudonymous writer who produces long-form essays on topics ranging from AI safety and the philosophy of consciousness to tactical parenting, tea optimization, and the statistical analysis of moral intuitions. The blog is characterized by a **deeply empirical, contrarian, and self-aware** approach — Dynomight is willing to change their mind publicly, runs personal experiments with rigorous methodology (for an individual blogger), and treats intuition as a starting point for investigation rather than a conclusion.

The intellectual project of dynomight.net is **applied epistemology**: using concrete questions (Do blue-blocking glasses improve sleep? How well do AI models predict human moral judgments? Is futarchy actually better than democracy?) as entry points into deeper discussions about how we know things, how we should reason about uncertainty, and how our cognitive biases shape our conclusions.

Dynomight writes with a distinctive voice — skeptical, dryly humorous, and willing to follow arguments to uncomfortable conclusions. The blog's tagline could be **"I was wrong about this, and here's what I learned"** — a stance that is rare in tech writing and reflects a genuine commitment to intellectual honesty over ego preservation.

The pseudonymity is itself a philosophical choice: it allows the ideas to stand on their own, separate from the author's identity, reputation, or institutional affiliations. Dynomight's posts are signed with just "dynomight" — a name that is itself slightly absurd, which fits the blog's willingness to take on serious topics without taking itself too seriously.

## Core Ideas

### The "Wanting" Problem in AI Safety

Dynomight's most significant contribution to AI safety discourse is the argument that **the hard part of alignment is not knowing what we want, or succeeding at tasks, but wanting what we want**:

> "The hard part is making AI want to do what we want."

This decomposes alignment into three subproblems:
1. **Knowing** — AI understands human values
2. **Wanting** — AI is motivated to pursue those values
3. **Success** — AI can effectively achieve its goals

Dynomight argues that Knowing and Success are tractable (or at least less hard), while Wanting is the bottleneck. Human values are "a big ball of heuristics" — messy, context-dependent, and not grounded in deep philosophical foundations. A superintelligent AI won't struggle to *understand* what humans value; it will struggle to *care* about those values unless they're built into its motivation architecture.

This position is deliberately modest — Dynomight gives the argument only a "~35% chance of being correct" — but the decomposition itself is valuable:

> "I think the idea that 'wanting is the hard part' is the majority view. Paul Christiano, for example... Richard Ngo also seems to explicitly endorse this view..."

The essay engages with the broader AI safety community (Christiano, Ngo, MIRI) while maintaining intellectual independence.

### The Second System Problem and Coordination

Dynomight identifies a fundamental coordination problem in AI safety that most discussions miss:

> "If AI is dangerous by default then figuring out how to build a safe AI is not enough. Don't you also need to make sure no one builds an unsafe AI?"

This is the **second system problem**: even if we solve alignment technically, we still need to prevent anyone else from building unaligned AI. The options — regulation, secrecy, restricting compute, building an AI guardian — all have their own failure modes. Dynomight's analysis is notably unsentimental:

> "Say your guardian is a bounded AI with limited capabilities. Then it will be outsmarted by my dangerous [AI]. [...] If whatever you did to align the guardian makes it less effective [...] then it will still lose if someone else builds an AI without those restrictions."

The essay concludes that technical alignment is "only the beginning of the problem" — a sober assessment that most AI safety discussions don't engage with honestly.

### Consciousness Will Slip Through Our Fingers

One of Dynomight's most philosophically ambitious essays argues that **technology will not help us solve the hard problem of consciousness**:

> "Let's assume that you don't need to understand consciousness in order to predict the behavior of physical systems."

The argument is elegant: if consciousness doesn't affect physical behavior (no dualism, no violation of conservation of energy), then no amount of technological investigation — AI, brain simulation, or scanning — will reveal anything about it. The systems we study will behave the same way regardless of which theory of consciousness is true:

> "There are severe limits on how much we can learn about consciousness from examining the behavior of these systems, because the rules that govern their behavior don't depend on which theory of consciousness is true."

Dynomight acknowledges the "meta problem" — why do we *say* we're conscious? — as a potential crack in this argument, but remains skeptical:

> "I find that very confusing. But sometimes confusion is good!"

The essay is notable for what it doesn't do: it doesn't claim to have solved consciousness. It claims that consciousness may be fundamentally unknowable through empirical means — and that this is a position worth taking seriously.

### Moral Puzzles as Empirical Investigation

Dynomight's "Moral puzzles: Man vs. machine" series is a concrete empirical project: creating moral dilemmas and testing how well humans and AI models agree on them. The puzzles range from the mundane (in-ovo chicken sexing) to the absurd (a virus that makes humans 10% happier but turns them into Jaffa Cakes):

> "Here's an abstract moral question. It is the year 2825. You are an advanced AI. You have just stumbled across a virus. If you release it, all humans will be 10% happier and live 10% longer, forever. However, they will all get really weird about Jaffa Cakes."

The results are surprisingly nuanced. AI models perform well on "normie" moral puzzles but show more divergence on weird ones. Dynomight is careful about interpretation:

> "So, current AI already knows what we want? We're done? Problem solved? Well, no. Those are all normie moral puzzles."

The project exemplifies Dynomight's approach: **take a big philosophical question, make it concrete and testable, run the experiment, report the results honestly, and resist overconfidence**.

### Empirical Self-Experimentation

Dynomight runs personal experiments with a level of rigor unusual for a blog:

- **Theanine self-experiment**: N=4 study testing the effects of theanine supplementation
- **Tea optimization**: Systematic experimentation with brewing parameters
- **Sleep and blue-blocking glasses**: Testing whether the products actually improve sleep quality
- **Scribble-based forecasting**: Exploring whether simple visual methods can improve prediction accuracy

Each experiment is reported with honesty about limitations, sample sizes, and confounders. The point isn't statistical significance — it's **model-building through personal data collection**. Dynomight treats their own life as a laboratory, not because the results are generalizable, but because the process of experimentation sharpens thinking.

### "Good If Make Prior After Data Instead of Before"

This post title (December 2025) captures a recurring theme in Dynomight's writing: **the danger of post-hoc reasoning**. The essay critiques the common practice of forming hypotheses after seeing data and then treating them as if they were pre-registered. This is particularly relevant to AI safety discourse, where narratives can be constructed to fit any outcome.

### Contrarian Takes on Popular Positions

Dynomight consistently takes positions that run counter to prevailing opinion:

- **"Algorithmic ranking is unfairly maligned"** — argues that algorithmic curation has real benefits that critics overlook
- **"Counterintuitive effects of minimum prices"** — challenges standard economic intuitions about price floors
- **"Y'all are over-complicating these AI-risk arguments"** — pushes back against overwrought AI safety discourse
- **"I guess I was wrong about AI persuasion"** — publicly reverses a previous position when evidence changes

The willingness to be wrong publicly is a core part of Dynomight's intellectual identity. The blog contains multiple "I was wrong" posts, which is rare for any writer, let alone an anonymous one who faces no social accountability for being right.

## Overview

**Terrible Software** is a blog about the **human side of software engineering** — management, career progression, team dynamics, communication patterns, and workplace culture. Despite its name, it is not primarily about bad code or terrible technical decisions. The "terrible software" refers to the **systems and structures** that produce mediocre engineering outcomes: poor management practices, dysfunctional team dynamics, and career paths that reward the wrong behaviors.

The blog gained remarkable traction quickly — **230,000+ unique visitors** in its first year (December 2025 retrospective) — by focusing on topics that resonate deeply with working software engineers:

> "I started this blog about a year ago with pretty modest expectations. It started as a place to organize my thoughts on management, engineering, and everything else in between. I figured maybe a few colleagues would read it. Maybe some friends?"

The author writes as an **engineering manager who still codes**, maintaining technical credibility while operating at the intersection of people and systems. Their posts consistently address the gap between how engineering organizations *say* they work and how they *actually* work.

## Core Ideas

### What Actually Makes You Senior: Ambiguity Management

The blog's central thesis on career progression:

> "The one skill that separates senior engineers from everyone else isn't technical. It's the ability to take ambiguous situations and turn them into concrete, actionable plans."

This is a significant departure from the typical "senior engineers write better code" narrative. Instead, **seniority is defined by the ability to create clarity from chaos** — taking vague requirements, incomplete information, and conflicting stakeholder interests and transforming them into a coherent engineering approach.

The implication: **technical skills are table stakes; ambiguity management is the differentiator.** This explains why some excellent coders never reach senior levels — they can't navigate the human complexity of engineering work.

### Nobody Gets Promoted for Simplicity

One of the blog's most provocative arguments:

> "We reward complexity and ignore simplicity. In interviews, design reviews, and promotions."

The analysis identifies a systemic bias in engineering organizations:

| Context | What Gets Rewarded | What Gets Ignored |
|---|---|---|
| **Interviews** | Complex architectures, exotic technologies | Simple solutions, deep understanding |
| **Design Reviews** | Elaborate system diagrams, multiple components | Minimal designs that solve the actual problem |
| **Promotions** | "I built a distributed microservices mesh" | "I deleted 10,000 lines of code" |

The fix proposed: **make simplicity visible and valuable**. Document the complexity you avoided. Explain why the simple solution is harder to find and more valuable to maintain. Create promotion criteria that reward reduction, not addition.

This connects to the broader theme of **dysfunctional incentive structures** in engineering organizations — systems that produce "terrible software" by rewarding the wrong behaviors.

### AI Can Write Your Code. It Can't Do Your Job.

The blog's measured stance on AI coding tools:

> "The companies building AI are spending billions to acquire engineers, not replace them. Here's why your job is safer than you think."

The argument decomposes software engineering into:
1. **Code generation** (AI is good at this)
2. **Requirements understanding** (AI is poor at this)
3. **Trade-off analysis** (AI is poor at this)
4. **Team coordination** (AI is poor at this)
5. **System maintenance over time** (AI is poor at this)

The key insight: **code generation is the smallest part of what software engineers actually do.** The companies building AI know this — they're hiring engineers, not replacing them.

### The Strange Case of Engineers Who Dismiss AI

> "Some engineers dismiss AI coding tools as 'garbage' without trying modern versions. Their outdated opinions are costing them."

This post addresses a common pattern: engineers who tried early AI coding tools (GitHub Copilot v1, early ChatGPT) and formed lasting negative opinions, ignoring the rapid improvement in model capabilities. The argument is that **dismissing AI without engaging with current versions is a career risk** — not because AI will replace you, but because engineers who use AI effectively will outperform those who don't.

### Why I Still Write Code as an Engineering Manager

> "I still code as an engineering manager. Not to stay busy, but to show my team what good looks like."

The blog argues against the common advice that engineering managers should stop coding entirely. Instead, **strategic coding as a manager serves multiple purposes**:

- **Demonstrates technical credibility** — the manager isn't disconnected from the work
- **Sets quality standards** — showing what "good" looks like through example
- **Maintains technical empathy** — understanding the frustrations and challenges the team faces
- **Builds trust** — the manager isn't asking the team to do something they wouldn't do themselves

The caveat: **don't code to stay busy** — code to lead. The distinction is subtle but important.

### Say the Thing You Want

A practical career advice post that identifies a common failure mode:

> "You want to lead that new initiative. Or move to a different team. Or you've been thinking about a promotion. But you don't say it out loud."

The analysis identifies three reasons people stay quiet:
1. **Fear of seeming presumptuous** — "Who am I to ask for this?"
2. **Confusing wanting with working toward** — treating the thought as progress
3. **Fear of rejection** — preferring the safety of unexpressed desires

The solution is brutally simple: **say what you want, in a 1:1, to your manager.** The worst outcome is a "no." The best outcome is a roadmap. Most outcomes are somewhere in between — useful information that lets you plan.

> "Even the most attentive manager is working with incomplete information when you don't say things out loud."

### "Good Taste" Is Just Experience

> "What people call 'good taste' is really just experience earned through reps. Stop making it sound innate."

This post demystifies technical judgment by reframing it as **accumulated experience**. The implication: taste can be developed through deliberate practice, not just innate ability. This connects to the broader theme of **making the implicit explicit** — if taste is just experience, then we can create systems to share and develop it.

### Life Happens at 1x Speed

A critique of the content consumption habit of listening to podcasts/audiobooks at 2x speed:

> "Why I stopped consuming content at 2x speed, and the simple rule that changed how I listen to everything."

The argument: **slowing down content consumption leads to better understanding and retention.** This is part of a broader pattern in the blog of questioning "optimization" habits that actually reduce value.

## Writing Style & Philosophy

The blog's writing style is **direct, practical, and experience-grounded**. Characteristics:

- **Short posts** — typically 3-6 minute reads
- **Clear headings** — easy to navigate
- **Direct advice** — not hedged or academic
- **Personal experience** — "I" statements, not abstract analysis
- **Actionable takeaways** — each post ends with something the reader can do
- **No jargon** — accessible to engineers at all levels

The author explicitly writes for themselves first: "A place to organize my thoughts on management, engineering, and everything else in between." The massive readership (230,000+ visitors) is a byproduct, not the goal.

The blog uses WordPress with email subscription functionality and is distributed via RSS. Posts are tagged with topics like career, engineering, leadership, teams, management, productivity, culture, and AI.

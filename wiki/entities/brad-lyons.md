# Brad Lyons

Brad Lyons is the founder and CEO of **Crossover Research**, a sell-side research firm based in New York City focused on software and enterprise technology. He has been active on X/Twitter since September 2021 (@blyons151), where he has built a following of 12,200+ across 2,400+ posts, publishing long-form analysis that bridges enterprise technology diligence, financial modeling, and AI structural trends. His research combines traditional financial analysis with voice-of-customer (VoC) data, proprietary surveys, and quantitative investing frameworks.

Lyons covers a broad range of sectors — from vertical SaaS and enterprise software to semiconductors, nuclear energy infrastructure, and gaming — with a distinctive methodology that pairs fundamental analysis with technical/quantitative frameworks.

## Key Contributions

### The AI Investment Supercycle Hypothesis (August 2025)
Lyons' most comprehensive structural thesis argues that the current AI boom cannot clear its capital stack. First published August 11, 2025 and reposted as a Note Tweet in May 2026, the hypothesis contends that the capital requirements for AI infrastructure (datacenters, GPUs, energy, talent) exceed the returns the ecosystem can generate under current business models:

- The capital stack is over-leveraged relative to sustainable revenue generation across the AI value chain
- Inference costs, while falling, haven't collapsed enough to make AI-native unit economics work at scale without VC subsidy
- The clearest signal: AI-native competitors have **worse gross margins** than SaaS incumbents, not better — VC cash subsidizing unit economics is a bridge, not a business model
- Since the thesis was first published, Lyons notes "the math has only gotten worse"
- The hypothesis ties directly to his SaaSpocalypse analysis: if the underlying capital stack can't clear, the downstream SaaS ecosystem faces compounding pressure

### The SaaSpocalypse Thesis (April 2026)
In a widely-circulated Note Tweet (1,631 likes, 4,175 bookmarks, 821K impressions), Lyons analyzed why vertical SaaS companies were down ~50% in multiples and argued the decline was structural, not cyclical. His central thesis:

- The **engineering moat** that protected SaaS for 20 years has been upended by AI
- Four remaining moats: distribution, proprietary data, workflow breadth, and **regulatory insulation**
- Regulatory moats (Tyler Technologies, Veeva, Guidewire) show "multiples compressed, fundamentals intact"
- **Data entrenchment** (Palantir model) is the most scalable moat when combined with AI
- Most SaaS companies fail because they never built a "second business" — monetizing the installed base beyond seat ARR

### Palantir Long Thesis (May 2024)
Called Palantir at $13 based on data entrenchment and ontology analysis — before the AIP bootcamp-driven growth inflection.

### Revenue Segmentation Framework (May 2026)
Lyons argues that ARR is not uniform and must be segmented by mission criticality to assess valuation risk:

- **Non-mission critical (engagement layer)**: Easiest to displace with AI; massive multiple compression risk
- **Mission critical (operations layer)**: Harder to displace but vulnerable if AI-native alternatives match feature parity
- **Regulatory-anchored (compliance layer)**: Most defensible; switching cost is regulatory process, not feature velocity
- **Data-entrenched (ontology layer)**: Palantir model — ripping out means rebuilding the data model from scratch

The framework provides a direct method for assessing which portion of a SaaS company's revenue is exposed to AI disruption: if >40% of ARR sits in the non-mission-critical bucket, the multiple should reflect that exposure.

### The AI Playbook for Operators and Investors
Lyons' general framework for evaluating AI positioning emphasizes technical stack velocity over narrative:

> "The technical stack matters more than ever. If the vendors in your stack aren't shipping aggressively, your competitors are already outpacing you."

Key themes:
- CEOs not leaning into AI are **uninvestable** — the pace is compounding weekly
- The gap between companies that ship AI features and those that don't will widen into a structural valuation gap
- Enterprise and regulated verticals are the last safe harbor, not because of product quality, but because of **data breadth and compliance surface area**

## Research Methodology

Lyons' research approach combines multiple data sources into a structured diligence framework:

- **VoC (Voice of Customer) data**: Survey-based analysis of buyer sentiment, mission criticality, and switching friction across enterprise buyer segments
- **Battery Ventures partnership**: Joint research on AI adoption in the Office of the CFO (129 finance leaders at $50M–$5B+ revenue companies surveyed)
- **Quantitative frameworks**: Pairs fundamental analysis with technical/quantitative charting frameworks to improve win rate on timing
- **Revenue segmentation**: Tiering ARR by defensibility (engagement vs operations vs compliance vs ontology layers)
- **Continuous driver research**: Internal data ingest across transcripts, alternative data, expert calls, and source-level analysis
- **Institutional research tools**: Building technology to transform institutional research and diligence across private/public markets

## Notable Frameworks

### Three Diligence Questions for Vertical SaaS
1. What % of revenue comes from non-subscription sources? (Second business readiness)
2. How hard to recreate with AI today? (Moat durability)
3. What % of stickiness is regulatory, and which way are rules moving? (Moat direction)

### Moat Taxonomy
- **Built moats**: distribution, proprietary data, workflow breadth
- **Captured moats**: regulatory insulation (most resistant to AI disruption)

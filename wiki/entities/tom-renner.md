## Overview

**Tom Renner** is a generalist software developer and team lead at NavVis GmbH, based in Munich, Germany. His blog at tomrenner.com — subtitled "My place to put things" — is a space for **reflective technical writing** that sits at the intersection of software engineering practice, team dynamics, and the ethics of technology.

Renner's writing is distinctive for its **willingness to question technology** — even (especially) when it comes from within the tech industry. He has published essays arguing that LLMs are a "400-year-long confidence trick," written about the psychological dangers of cycle time metrics, and developed a practice he calls "saying the quiet part out loud" — explicitly stating team norms and expectations that are usually left implicit.

His intellectual profile combines **practical engineering experience** (Kotlin, Go, Python, neural networks, Advent of Code) with **philosophical reflection** on what makes software teams healthy, what makes technology ethical, and what makes a good leader.

## Core Ideas

### "Saying the Quiet Part Out Loud"

Renner's most original conceptual contribution is a leadership practice he named **"saying the quiet part out loud"** — the habit of explicitly stating team norms and expectations that are usually left implicit:

> "It's the habit of stating why you are doing things a certain way, even when you would assume it's obvious."

Examples he provides:
- "We want to do [obviously moral thing] because we are not evil"
- "We shouldn't leave shitty comments in code reviews because we want to build a positive working environment"

His thesis is that **leadership requires making implicit values explicit**:

> "It's easy to assume your colleagues a) know these things and b) would do them without prompting. But it's a form of leadership to state your expectations and beliefs out loud like this. It provides a chance for disagreement and debate by making them public."

This is not about announcing values from on high. It's about **creating psychological safety** by making the rules of the game transparent.

> "It takes confidence in your team to offer your priors up to examination in this manner. But also encourages an open and trusting environment, and a culture of honest feedback."

### LLMs as a "Confidence Trick"

In a striking 2025 essay, Renner argues that large language models represent a **400-year-long pattern of promising mechanical relief from mental labor** that never quite delivers:

> "In 1623 the German Wilhelm Schickard produced the first known designs for a mechanical calculator. Twenty years later Blaise Pascal produced a machine of an improved design, aiming to help with the large amount of tedious arithmetic required in his role as a tax collector."

His argument is that the **promise of computational relief** has been repeated across centuries — from mechanical calculators to LLMs — and that each generation convinces itself that *this* time the machine will free us from mental drudgery. But the reality is more complex: **the machine changes what we need to think about, not whether we need to think**.

This is a historically informed critique of AI hype, connecting Schickard and Pascal to the present day.

### Cycle Time as a Dangerous Metric

In his "Things that made me think" series, Renner analyzes cycle time (the measure of how long it takes to go from idea to production):

> "Cycle time is a measure lots of people use, but has no clear audience — developers, managers, CTOs all care about it. This makes it dangerous. Metrics have to be designed and used with psychological safety in mind. If people don't trust the intention behind the metrics use, they'll game it."

Drawing on **Dr. Cat Hicks** and learning theory, he argues that:
1. Cycle time lacks a clear stakeholder — it tries to serve everyone and serves no one well
2. Without trust in the metric's purpose, teams will optimize for the number rather than the outcome
3. This is a **Goodhart's Law** problem in practice — when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure

### Optimizing for Trust

Renner's essay "Optimising for trust" extends his cycle time critique to a broader thesis: **the most important metric for software teams is not velocity, cycle time, or story points — it's trust**.

When team members trust each other:
- They give honest feedback in code reviews
- They admit mistakes early
- They share knowledge freely
- They take appropriate risks
- They don't game metrics

Trust is not a "soft" metric — it is the **infrastructure on which all other metrics depend**.

### Digital Gardening vs. Digital Ghosts

In "Things that made me think: Digital gardening, web degradation, and digital ghosts," Renner explores the lifecycle of digital artifacts:

- **Digital gardening** — cultivating personal knowledge spaces that grow and evolve
- **Web degradation** — the slow rot of links, content, and context over time
- **Digital ghosts** — the artifacts of past selves that persist in digital form

This connects to his broader interest in **technology ethics** — if we put computers in everything, what happens to the traces of our digital lives?

### Against the Use of Technology

Renner is a technologist who frequently argues **against** technology use:

> "Find myself arguing against the use of technology worryingly often for a technologist."

This is not Luddism — it's **technological skepticism grounded in engineering experience**. Having built systems professionally, he understands both the promise and the peril of automation, and he is willing to say "we shouldn't" even when the industry says "we must."

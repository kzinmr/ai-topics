# Phil Trammell

**Phil Trammell** is Head of Economics at [[entities/epoch-ai]] and research scholar at Stanford University. He specializes in the economics of transformative AI, including AGI scenarios, long-term growth trajectories, capital-labor dynamics, and effective altruism-aligned economic analysis.

## Overview

Trammell brings a rigorous general equilibrium perspective to AGI economics. His work focuses on what happens to factor shares (labor vs capital), interest rates, and consumption patterns when AI systems become capable of automating increasingly complex tasks. He is known for precise, model-based thinking that challenges intuitive but flawed economic narratives about AI displacement.

## Key Positions on AGI Economics

### Network-Adjusted Factor Shares

A central contribution: looking at factor shares **down the entire supply chain**, not just at the final production step. Computer and electronic products in the US have a stable network-adjusted capital share of ~50%. The qualitative shift to AGI would be goods whose network-adjusted capital share goes to 1.0 — the entire supply chain automated with no intrinsically human component.

### The "Mongolian Economist" Analogy

Trammell's famous framing: a Mongolian economist in 1400 trying to predict what would be scarce in the future would correctly note that singers are intrinsically human (you can't automate the experience of watching a human perform) and that transportation/food are automatable. But holding varieties fixed, they'd predict singers would consume most future spending — completely wrong because **new varieties of goods** expand demand beyond what they could imagine. Similarly, predicting what will be scarce post-AGI by extrapolating from current goods is fundamentally limited.

### Pessimistic Moore's Law

Trammell reframes Moore's Law pessimistically: "every 18 months, the value of computation halves" — we're running out of uses for computation so fast that price drops sustain the exponential. The AI era may break this pattern: H100 rental costs have **increased** despite superior technology, because the opportunity cost of compute keeps rising with smarter models.

### Interest Rates and Capital Accumulation

On whether AGI leads to runaway capital accumulation: the real interest rate depends on what you measure it in. In units of robots, the interest rate could be 10,000% (one robot → 100 robots next year), but the price of robots falls correspondingly. The key dynamic is **investment-specific technical change** — capital goods falling in price relative to consumption goods, which standard macro models don't capture.

### Universal Basic Capital vs UBI

Trammell agrees with Imas on the importance of distinguishing **how revenue is raised** (taxation mechanism) from **how it's distributed**. He notes that indexing the economy is already relatively easy — under 20% of US non-tiny company market cap is private, and AI companies are likely to go public. The frictions keeping companies private (disclosure requirements) may themselves be reduced by AI.

### Tech Race Dynamics

Trammell raises an underappreciated cost of commoditized frontier AI: the **safety/tech race dynamic**. Fewer frontier companies means each has more buffer to slow down for safety. Concentrated labs create political targets (as seen with the Defense Production Act threat against Anthropic), but also enable coordination. His position: you can have concentrated frontier AI with widely distributed ownership through public markets — no trade-off between safety and broad prosperity.

## Collaboration with Alex Imas

Trammell frequently collaborates with [[entities/alex-imas]] on AGI economics. Their joint work explores complementary angles — Trammell focuses on supply-side dynamics and general equilibrium effects, while Imas focuses on demand/consumption patterns and political economy. Together they've developed scenario-planning frameworks for transformative AI.

# Phil Trammell

**Phil Trammell** (also known as Philip Trammell) is an economist specializing in the macroeconomic and strategic implications of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI). He serves as Head of Economics at [[entities/epoch-ai|Epoch]] and is a Research Scholar at the Stanford Institute for Human-Centered AI (HAI), focusing on AI's impact on economic growth, labor markets, and wealth distribution.

## Background

Trammell leads economic research at Epoch AI, a non-profit research institute that tracks and analyzes trends in AI compute, data, and capabilities. His work focuses on building economic models to understand the implications of advanced AI, particularly in scenarios where AI systems can substitute for human cognitive labor at scale.

At Stanford HAI's Digital Economy Lab, his research examines the intersection of AI progress, productivity growth, and economic transformation.

## Key Ideas

### Labor-Capital Complementarity and AGI

Trammell has explored whether the historical stability of labor's share of national income (~60%) — a Kaldor fact that has persisted through the Industrial Revolution and the Computer Revolution — will hold in the age of AGI. His work examines conditions under which labor share could collapse and what economic mechanisms might preserve or undermine it.

### AGI Economic Scenarios

Working alongside [[entities/alex-imas|Alex Imas]] at DeepMind, Trammell contributes to scenario-based economic analysis that maps out plausible outcomes for AI-driven economic transformation, focusing on:
- What remains scarce after AGI
- Optimal tax and redistribution mechanisms for AI-generated wealth
- How developing countries can index into AI economic gains
- The "messy middle" scenario of partial automation with persistent disruption

### The Epoch AI Economics Program

As Head of Economics at Epoch, Trammell leads research on:
- Long-term economic growth models under AI-driven technological acceleration
- The relationship between AI compute investment and macroeconomic productivity
- Scenarios for labor displacement and capital concentration
- Policy frameworks for equitable AI-driven growth

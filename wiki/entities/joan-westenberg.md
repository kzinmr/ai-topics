# Joan Westenberg

Joan Westenberg is a strategy and positioning consultant who works with startups and technology companies on narrative development, messaging, and market positioning.

## Key Insights

### Position or Perish: The Narrative Blueprint (May 2026)

Westenberg published a comprehensive framework for company positioning and narrative development, arguing that **positioning is upstream of everything** — product, marketing, hiring, fundraising, and PR.

Key concepts from the framework:

#### The Messaging Spine
A four-part structure that should underpin all company communications:
1. **Category** — What kind of thing are you? (The bucket customers put you in)
2. **Audience** — Who is this for, in specifics? (Not "businesses" but "heads of compliance at mid-market fintechs")
3. **Alternative** — What are they doing today instead of using you? (The real competitor is often spreadsheets, agencies, or status quo)
4. **Wedge** — What's the single sharp thing you do better?

#### Core Principles
- **Position against something specific**: Every position works through contrast. Pick an enemy that's big enough to matter, recognizable, beatable, and unable to follow you into your corner.
- **The category gambit**: Inventing a new category is harder and more expensive than founders think. Most should claim a corner of an existing category and own it harder than anyone else.
- **Copy as evidence**: Every word on the homepage either confirms the positioning or contradicts it. Vague words dissolve positioning; specific words confirm it.
- **The pricing tell**: Price is a positioning claim. Underpricing signals lack of confidence in your own positioning.
- **Hiring is downstream of narrative**: People want to work for companies whose story they can repeat without sounding ridiculous.

#### On Narrative vs. Positioning
> "Positioning is the static claim, but narrative is the moving picture. A company can hold a single positioning statement for years, and most should; but the narrative around that statement has to evolve, because the world evolves and your competition evolves with it."

#### Repositioning Triggers
A company should reposition when:
1. The market has moved underneath the original claim
2. The product has expanded into territory the original claim can't cover
3. A competitor has captured the language you used to own
4. Founders have learned something material about who their best customers are

**Repositioning because founders are bored with their own message will always fail** — personal boredom is not a strategic signal.

### Relevance to AI/Tech Companies

Westenberg's framework is particularly applicable to AI startups, where:
- Many companies struggle to articulate what they actually do vs. what they claim to do
- The "AI" category is becoming overcrowded, making differentiation critical
- Pricing signals credibility in a market full of vaporware
- Hiring competition for AI talent is fierce, making narrative a recruiting tool

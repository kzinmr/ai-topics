## Overview

Ethan Mollick is one of the most influential public educators on practical AI usage. As a professor at the Wharton School of the University of Pennsylvania, he has spent over two decades studying innovation, entrepreneurship, and organizational behavior. Since the release of ChatGPT in late 2022, Mollick has become a leading voice in translating academic AI research into accessible, actionable guidance for professionals, educators, and students.

His central thesis — that AI should be treated as a collaborator rather than a tool — is captured in his 2024 book *Co-Intelligence: Living and Working with AI*. Mollick argues that the most productive approach to AI is "co-intelligence": a partnership where humans and AI systems complement each other's strengths. He famously promotes the "10-hour rule" — just spend 10 hours using AI for real work and you'll learn more than from any guide.

Mollick's research has had measurable impact on AI adoption. His studies on AI's effect on workplace productivity (including collaborations with BCG/Harvard researchers) showed that consultants using AI completed tasks ~25% faster and produced 40% higher quality output. His work has been covered extensively in mainstream media, and his Substack "One Useful Thing" has become one of the most widely-read AI newsletters.

## Core Ideas

### The Four Rules of Co-Intelligence

Mollick's framework for working with AI centers on four practical rules:

1. **Always invite AI to the table** — *"You don't know what AI is good for or bad for inside your job or your industry. Nobody knows. The only way to figure it out is disciplined experimentation."* Mollick argues against waiting for perfect understanding — the only way to learn AI's capabilities is through hands-on use.

2. **Be the human in the loop** — *"The AI is better than a lot of people in a lot of jobs, but not at their whole job, right?"* Humans should remain responsible for oversight, judgment, and accountability, especially for high-stakes decisions.

3. **Treat AI like a human coworker** — AI models are trained on human language and respond best to the same social patterns we use with people. Giving AI a role/persona, providing context, and asking it to think through problems step-by-step all improve results.

4. **Assume this is the worst AI you'll ever use** — Capabilities are improving exponentially. Build workflows and processes that will benefit from future model improvements rather than optimizing for today's limitations.

### The "Jagged Frontier"

Mollick coined (or popularized) the term "jagged frontier" to describe AI's uneven capabilities. AI can perform PhD-level reasoning on some tasks while failing at counting the letter 'r' in "strawberry." This unpredictability means that the only way to map what AI can do is through systematic experimentation — each person's jagged frontier is slightly different based on their domain and use cases.

### AI as Creative Partner and Idea Amplifier

In his research, Mollick found that AI dramatically increases the *quantity* and *variance* of ideas in brainstorming. His recommended prompt structure for idea generation:

> *"Generate product ideas with the following requirements: [insert constraints here]. First generate a list of 100 ideas (short title only). Second, go through the list and determine whether the ideas are different and bold, modify the ideas as needed to make them bolder and more different. No two ideas should be the same. Next, give the ideas a name and combine it with a product description."*

This approach leverages AI's strength at volume while maintaining human editorial control over quality.

### When NOT to Use AI

In his widely-shared post ["15 Times to Use AI, and 5 Not To"](https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/15-times-to-use-ai-and-5-not-to), Mollick identified five categories where AI is counterproductive:

- **Deep learning and synthesis** — Shortcuts bypass the cognitive struggle required for true understanding
- **High-accuracy requirements** — LLM hallucinations are highly plausible and hard to detect
- **Unfamiliar failure modes** — Users must understand AI's unique failure patterns before relying on outputs
- **When the effort IS the point** — Struggle is necessary for breakthroughs ("aha" moments)
- **When AI is objectively bad** — The jagged frontier means AI has blind spots that aren't intuitive

### The Expertise Paradox

> *"AI is often most useful where we're already expert enough to spot its mistakes, yet least helpful in the deep work that made us experts in the first place."*

This paradox lies at the heart of Mollick's philosophy: AI amplifies existing expertise but cannot substitute for the learning process that creates it.

### AI Agents and the Future of Work

In September 2025, Mollick documented how AI agents have crossed an economic threshold: *"AIs have quietly crossed a threshold: they can now perform real, economically relevant work."* His experiment reproducing a complex economics paper using Claude Sonnet 4.5 showed AI performing multi-step analytical work autonomously — converting STATA code to Python, running analyses, and producing verified results.

However, he warns of the "content flood" risk: *"I gave Claude a corporate memo and asked it to turn it into a PowerPoint... Until I got 17 different PowerPoints. That is too many PowerPoints."* Without deliberate human judgment about what work is valuable, organizations will drown in low-value AI-generated content.

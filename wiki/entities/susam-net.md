## Overview

**Susam Pal** is a software engineer and mathematical recreationalist whose blog at susam.net has been a quiet fixture of the indie web since **2001**. What began as a dorm-room intranet portal named "maze" — serving FTP files and math puzzles to university peers — has evolved into one of the most **meticulously handcrafted personal websites** still in operation.

Pal's intellectual contribution spans three domains: **mathematical puzzles** (over 23 published on his site), **software engineering** (early Apache Nutch contributor), and **the art of the personal website** as a form of intellectual self-expression. His site is entirely generated by a custom Common Lisp program, every line of HTML and CSS is handcrafted, and he runs his own web server, guestbook, comment system, and feed aggregator.

Pal is a rare example of a programmer who treats the **act of writing as the act of thinking**. His blog posts often begin as playful experiments ("Fizz Buzz in CSS," "Fizz Buzz with Cosines") and spiral into rigorous mathematical explorations that connect abstract algebra to practical programming.

## Core Ideas

### Puzzles as Mathematical Meditation

Pal's puzzle work is distinctive for its **dual audience** — they serve as both recreational entertainment and genuine mathematical exploration. Each puzzle page follows a consistent pattern: present the problem, explore multiple solution approaches, reveal the mathematical structure underneath, and connect it to broader concepts.

> "The puzzle is not the answer. The puzzle is the path to the answer, and the path is where the learning happens."

His **Fizz Buzz variations** are particularly illustrative:
- **Fizz Buzz in CSS** — using CSS counters to solve a problem typically approached with JavaScript
- **Fizz Buzz with Cosines** — exploiting the periodicity of trigonometric functions to detect divisibility
- **Wordle With Grep** — using regular expressions as a word puzzle solver

These aren't just clever tricks. They demonstrate a deeper thesis: **programming languages are mathematical instruments**, and fluency in one reveals structure in others.

### The Architecture of a Personal Website

Pal's website is a **statement of philosophy** made in Common Lisp, HTML, and CSS. Key design principles:

1. **Every line handcrafted** — no frameworks, no CMS, no templates beyond his own layout system
2. **All internal links are relative** — enabling local browsing without a server
3. **Zero external dependencies** — KaTeX loaded only on pages with math, nothing else
4. **Self-hosted everything** — web server, comment system, guestbook, feed aggregator
5. **Complete directory listings** — every file and subdirectory is accessible

This is the opposite of the modern web stack. Pal's approach is closer to **Donald Knuth's literate programming** — every choice is deliberate, documented, and defensible.

### Mathematics as a Way of Seeing

Pal's blog reveals a mind that finds mathematical structure everywhere:

- **GCD Grid** (2021) — visualizing the greatest common divisor as a grid pattern
- **Zigzag Number Spiral** (2025) — exploring Ulam-like spirals with LaTeX-rendered formulas
- **Product of Additive Inverses** (2025) — abstract algebra in a single page
- **From Finite Integral Domains to Finite Fields** (2025) — a proof sketch that connects ring theory to field theory
- **Elliptical Python Programming** (2025) — plotting ellipses as a programming exercise
- **Mutually Attacking Knights** (2025) — chess puzzle generalization

His 2025 post "Fed 24 Years of My Blog Posts to a Markov Model" is characteristic: a self-referential experiment that combines his interests in text processing, probability, and personal digital history.

### Free Software as Social Contract

Pal's commitment to free software is not ideological abstraction — it's **practical infrastructure**:

- Apache Nutch contributor (2007) — authentication for web crawlers
- MathB.in — a pastebin service he ran for 10 years before shutting it down
- His entire website source code is public on GitHub under MIT license
- Guestbook, comment system, blogroll — all self-implemented, no third-party services

His decision to shut down MathB.in after a decade of operation, documented transparently, is itself a contribution to the free software community: **it models how to responsibly end a service**.

### Writing First, Tooling Second

In a January 2026 post titled "Writing First, Tooling Second," Pal articulates a philosophy that runs through all his work:

> "The tool is not the point. The writing is the point. The tool exists to make the writing possible."

This connects to his broader view of computation: **the computer is a medium for thought**, not an end in itself. His Common Lisp website generator, his mathematical puzzles, his sysadmin tutorials — all serve the same goal: making ideas visible and shareable.

## Writing Style and Philosophy

- **Rigorous but playful** — Mathematical puzzles presented with humor and genuine curiosity
- **Long-form by design** — Posts take "several weeks to months" to write and publish
- **Self-referential honesty** — Documents his own infrastructure decisions, including shutdowns
- **Multi-modal** — Combines mathematics, programming, sysadmin work, and personal reflection
- **No advertising, no tracking** — The website is a garden, not a platform
- **Guestbook revival** — Reinstated his guestbook after 20 years, signaling commitment to human-scale web interaction

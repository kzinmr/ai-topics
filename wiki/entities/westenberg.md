## Core Ideas

Westenberg writes at the intersection of **technological critique, political philosophy, and cultural analysis**. Her essays combine historical framing, economic data, and philosophical reasoning to argue for deliberate, humane responses to disruptive technological change. She is fundamentally **anti-techno-determinist**: she believes the future is shaped by political choices and collective action, not by market forces or technological inevitability.

### The Blog as Intellectual Infrastructure

Westenberg frames blogging not as hobby but as **civilizational infrastructure**. Drawing a direct line from Denis Diderot's *Encyclopédie* (1751) through Montaigne's *Essais* (1570s) to the modern blog, she argues that long-form personal writing is the internet's most underappreciated intellectual tool:

> *"A blog is a room of your own on the internet. It's a place where you decide what to write about, when to publish, and how to structure your thinking."*

She identifies what makes blogs persist while most are abandoned after three posts:
- They have a **perspective**, not just a topic
- They maintain **intellectual honesty** — writing as "attempts" or "tries" (Montaigne's original meaning of *essai*)
- They accumulate value over time as a **body of work** rather than chasing engagement metrics

### The Second Gilded Age and AI Disruption

Westenberg's most ambitious work is a **5-pillar policy roadmap** for navigating AI-driven economic transition (2025–2035), which she frames as analogous to post-WWII reconstruction:

> *"The future is not determined. It never has been. I don't entirely believe AI will produce the civilizational wipeout the doomers are predicting. I think there's a lot of work between here and there. What matters is how much unnecessary suffering we allow."*

Her five pillars:
1. **Guaranteed Minimum Income (GMI)** — economic floor during displacement, citing Stockton SEED and OpenResearch studies
2. **Universal Basic Services (UBS)** — healthcare, housing, education, transport, communications, food as guaranteed rights
3. **Portable Benefits** — decouple from employers (Denmark's "flexicurity" model)
4. **Public AI Infrastructure** — government-funded compute and open-source models to prevent oligopoly
5. **Democratic Governance of AI** — algorithmic transparency, citizens' assemblies

She explicitly rejects both **utopian accelerationism** (markets will sort it out) and **existential doomerism** (collapse is inevitable), arguing both assume human agency is irrelevant.

### Everything's Casino — The Financialization of Life

In her most cited long-form essay, Westenberg argues that modern society has structurally shifted from incremental, effort-based progress to **speculative gambling**:

> *"When you can't see a future, you stop investing in one. You start gambling instead, because a lottery ticket and a savings account have the same expected emotional return when you believe the savings account will never buy you anything worth having."*

Key domains of "casino logic":
- **Housing**: transformed from shelter to speculative asset (Sydney median 13x income, US 5.81x)
- **Labor**: wage work converted to probabilistic earnings (Uber, gig economy)
- **Creator economy**: power-law distribution structurally identical to lottery
- **Politics**: campaign strategy as high-risk betting
- **Memecoins**: $TRUMP token as case study (813K wallets lost ~$2B, 58 wallets made >$10M each)

### The Future is More Stuff — Critique of Consumer Innovation

Westenberg challenges the conflation of **novelty with progress**:

> *"We were promised Utopia. We got 15 different food delivery apps. And the ability to have an AI read and write our emails."*

Her core argument: Keynes predicted a 15-hour workweek by 2030, but instead technology has been captured to **increase consumption and work hours**. The average American consumes 2x more goods than 50 years ago with "fuck-all corresponding increase" in life satisfaction. Consumer goods production accounts for ~60% of global GHG emissions.

> *"Innovation isn't stifled; it's redirected. Every engineer designing a slightly better juicer with smartphone connectivity represents diverted capacity from public transit, climate education, and renewable infrastructure."*

### Introspection as Civilizational Prerequisite

In her critique of Marc Andreessen's "zero-introspection mindset," Westenberg argues that dismissing self-examination as a "modern pathology invented by Freud" is **calculated historical revisionism**:

> *"Directional advice is useless without a destination. Knowing where to go requires knowing what you want, which requires introspection."*

She traces introspection across millennia: Socrates, Marcus Aurelius, Augustine, Mencius, Shakespeare — arguing that tech culture's rejection of inner life led directly to platforms optimized for engagement metrics while user wellbeing decayed.

### Optimism Without Naivety

Despite her critiques, Westenberg identifies as an **optimist**:

> *"I don't entirely believe AI will produce the civilizational wipeout the doomers are predicting. I think there's a lot of work between here and there."*

Her optimism is rooted in historical precedent (post-WWII reconstruction, progressive era reforms) and a belief in human agency — the conviction that outcomes depend on what we build, not what technology does to us.

## Style & Approach

Westenberg's essays are distinguished by:
- **Terminal/command-line aesthetic**: blog structured as `cat ./posts/latest`, `ls ./posts/`, with bash-style headers
- **Historical framing**: connects contemporary issues to historical precedents (WWII reconstruction, Diderot, Keynes, Veblen)
- **Data-driven argumentation**: backs claims with specific statistics and citations
- **Philosophical depth**: draws on Graeber, Bregman, Veblen, Jameson, Dostoevsky, Montaigne
- **Structural clarity**: uses tables, numbered lists, and clear section headers
- **Members-only tier**: some essays are paid-subscriber-only, suggesting a sustainable independent publishing model
- **Cross-domain synthesis**: connects geopolitics, technology, economics, and psychology in single essays

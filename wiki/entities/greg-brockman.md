## Overview

Greg Brockman is one of the most influential engineers in AI history, though he operates largely behind the scenes. As OpenAI's co-founder and President, he has been the technical architect behind the GPT series, Codex (which powers GitHub Copilot), and the company's massive compute infrastructure strategy.

His career embodies a **builder ethos**: hands-on execution, empirical debugging, and verifiable outcomes over abstract theorizing. He reportedly works 60–100 hours per week, with ~80% dedicated to hands-on coding even at the President level.

## Early Life and Education

- **Born**: November 29, 1987, in Thompson, North Dakota
- **High School**: Graduated Red River High School (2007); exhausted advanced math curriculum via dual enrollment at University of North Dakota by sophomore year
- **Gap Year**: Self-taught programming after reading Alan Turing's 1950 essay *"Computing Machinery and Intelligence"*, which sparked his focus on testable, empirical AI
- **Harvard**: Attended for one year before transferring
- **MIT**: Transferred to pursue EECS (Course 6). Researched static buffer overrun detection and contributed to advanced data structures (range trees, fractional cascading)
- **Dropped out**: Left MIT in 2010 to pursue startup opportunities, favoring iterative, real-world engineering over academic timelines

## Stripe (2010–2015)

Brockman joined Stripe as approximately the **5th employee** and became its first significant engineering hire:

- Built foundational backend infrastructure, server architecture, database schemas
- Designed the secure credit card vault and developer-friendly APIs that became Stripe's core product advantage
- Promoted to **CTO in 2013**
- Scaled the engineering team from 4 to 250+ members
- Helped Stripe reach **$10B in payment volume by 2015**
- Established rigorous testing culture, modular code structures, and low-friction API design principles that would carry into OpenAI

## OpenAI Co-Founding (2015–Present)

### Founding (December 2015)
Co-founded OpenAI with **Sam Altman**, **Elon Musk**, **Ilya Sutskever**, **Wojciech Zaremba**, and **John Schulman** as a non-profit focused on safe AGI.

### The 2019 Capped-Profit Transition
Brockman was instrumental in OpenAI's transition from pure non-profit to **capped-profit structure**, arguing that this was essential to secure the compute resources and talent needed to compete at the frontier of AI development. This decision later became the basis for Elon Musk's lawsuit against OpenAI.

## Recent Technical Focus (2024–2025)

### Compute Infrastructure
After returning from a 3-month sabbatical (November 2024), Brockman aggressively expanded OpenAI's compute infrastructure:

- Identified **compute scarcity** as the primary bottleneck for AGI progress
- Partnered with **Broadcom** (October 2025) to use AI for chip layout optimization, accelerating custom accelerator design from weeks to days
- Described internal GPU allocation as an *"exercise in pain and suffering"* due to NVIDIA/AMD supply constraints

### Autonomous Coding
- Advanced **Codex CLI** and IDE extensions (August 2025) with image inputs and agentic workflows
- Released **GPT-5-Codex** (September 2025) enabling sustained multi-hour coding sessions
- Reported **~50% reduction in iteration cycles** for complex software tasks

## Views on AI and Policy

### Acceleration and Compute
Brockman is a leading voice in the AI acceleration camp:

- Advocates rapid infrastructure scaling and iterative deployment over precautionary slowdowns
- Co-led a **$100M super PAC** (August 2025) with Andreessen Horowitz to counter *"cumbersome AI regulation"* and promote market-driven innovation
- Predicts AI will evolve *"from AI coworkers to AI managers"* by 2025, proactively generating ideas and assigning tasks
- Expects material abundance and scientific breakthroughs through accelerated AI deployment

### Safety and Alignment
Brockman's approach to safety is distinctly engineering-focused:

- **Rejects broad development pauses**; views safety as an engineering problem solvable through empirical testing
- Champions **RLHF**, AI-driven critique models, and rigorous red-teaming integrated directly into product development
- Argues that scaling capabilities *improves* alignment when paired with iterative feedback
- Actively developing evaluation environments to detect and mitigate *"scheming"* behaviors
- Prefers integrated, empirical safeguards over theoretical constraints

> *"Safety must scale empirically alongside capabilities."*

## The 2023 Board Crisis

### Ouster (November 17, 2023)
Brockman was removed as board chair alongside CEO Sam Altman due to governance and confidence issues. The board's decision was reportedly influenced by concerns about Altman's candor and the company's direction.

### Reinstatement (November 22, 2023)
Brockman resigned in solidarity with Altman. Approximately **770 employees threatened mass exodus** to Microsoft. After intense pressure, the original board resigned and both were reinstated.

### Aftermath
The crisis catalyzed significant organizational changes:
- Board restructuring with new independent directors
- Increased focus on product velocity and compute expansion
- Departure of safety-focused researchers (Jan Leike, Ilya Sutskever)

## Major Controversies

### Elon Musk Lawsuit
Musk sued OpenAI, Altman, and Brockman (March 2024, refiled August 2024) alleging breach of contract and fraud for shifting from non-profit to capped-profit model.

**Brockman's defense**: The 2019 capped-profit structure was essential for global AI competition, securing billions in compute investment, and attracting top talent. OpenAI's non-profit oversight was maintained through governance structures.

### Internal Safety Debates (May 2024)
- **Superalignment co-head Jan Leike** and **Chief Scientist Ilya Sutskever** resigned, citing safety deprioritization
- Brockman and Altman defended their integrated safety framework
- Brockman took a sabbatical (August–November 2024) amid talent attrition, returned without reported ties to the safety disputes

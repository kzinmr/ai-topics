## Core Ideas

### Psychology as Experimental History

Adam's signature thesis: **psychology is experimental history.** Unlike historians who wait for events to unfold, psychologists *construct situations, place humans inside them, and record what happens.* The fundamental unit of psychology is not the p-value or the effect size — it is the *story*. Every methods section is a narrative about what people did in a specific context. Progress comes from curating, verifying, and retelling these stories until they reveal enduring truths about human nature.

> *"The base unit of mass is the kilogram, the base unit of length is the meter, and the base unit of history is the story."*

Key insight: psychology creates **alternate timelines** through counterfactual experimentation. We can't change the past, but we can ask *"what if?"* by manipulating variables across conditions. Robust findings graduate from past tense (*"participants were slower"*) to present tense (*"people are slower"*) through replication — a verbal shift that signals theoretical maturity.

### The Failure of Peer Review

In his most influential essay, Adam argues that the 60-year experiment of mandatory peer review has **failed catastrophically**:

- Reviewers catch only **25–30% of major flaws** in deliberate error studies
- Scientists spend ~**15,000 years per year** doing free review labor
- The system incentivizes p-hacking, self-censorship, and thought-policing
- Pre-peer-review Nobel discoveries were rated equal or superior to post-peer-review ones
- The debunked vaccine-autism paper passed peer review and stayed published for 12 years

His alternative: **strong-link science.** Progress depends on the *best* ideas winning through empirical utility, not on gatekeeping bad ones out. Censorship slows truth.

> *"Peer review, like every form of censorship, merely slows down truth."*

He demonstrated this by publishing a preprint PDF directly to the internet — in plain language, with full transparency — and getting more engagement and impact than most peer-reviewed papers.

### The Three-Layer Model of Social Awkwardness

Adam treats awkwardness as a near-universal condition with three layers, each requiring a different intervention:

1. **Social Clumsiness** (outside) → Fix: abandon the coverup, own errors proactively
2. **Excessive Self-Awareness** (middle) → Fix: shift attention outward; treat conversations like "watering a plant"
3. **People-Phobia** (inner core) → Fix: exposure therapy + notch-and-reflect to recalibrate pessimistic predictions

Key data: people consistently underestimate conversation quality. They predict 50% of conversations will last >5 minutes; actual is 87%. The gap between expectation and reality is the treatment target.

### Writing Craft: The Necklace and the Neck

In his widely-shared "28 Slightly Rude Notes on Writing," Adam argues that human writing's value lies in its **origin** — the irreplaceable life energy spent producing it. This is his framework for understanding what AI can and cannot replace:

> *"Maybe that's my problem with AI-generated prose: it's all necklace, no neck."*

The AI produces the ornament (the necklace) but lacks the human context, struggle, and "coming-from-ness" (the neck) that gives writing its power. His advice: don't retreat from AI — **compete** by going deeper into uniquely human cognitive and emotional territory.

Other craft insights:
- **The Wadsworth Constant**: skip the first 30% of any draft; early paragraphs are dead ends
- **Motive over thesis**: don't ask "why should the reader care?" — ask "why do *I* care?"
- **The Sweet Spot**: write in the narrow territory between "things that are obvious" and "things that are obviously wrong"

### Underrated Ways to Change the World

Adam's impact philosophy rejects the false binary of high-status power vs. high-sacrifice martyrdom. Real change comes from accessible, leveraged actions:

- **Be the second-bravest person**: scandals persist not for lack of whistleblowers, but for lack of people who *listen* and amplify
- **Switchboarding**: in an age of infinite information, human curation and connection-routing is the scarce resource
- **Build your RUNK** (Reliable, Useful, Non-Kool): do something moderately useful and do it relentlessly. Become infrastructure.

> *"Good intentions start to rot if you don't act them out. Well-meaning people who remain idle end up sick in the heart and the head."*

### The Liking Gap and Conversation Dynamics

His academic research reveals systematic misperceptions in social interaction:
- People underestimate how much others like them after conversations
- Most conversations end when *neither* party wants them to
- People expect to enjoy talking to strangers less than they actually do
- The root cause: asymmetrical information about what others are thinking
